7.3 Current, Power Sources, and Measurement

Lights turn on, phones charge, and motors spin because electric charge moves through a circuit. That flow of charge is called current.
This section connects the idea of moving charge to the devices that provide electrical energy and the tools used to measure what is happening in a circuit.
“Measure what is measurable, and make measurable what is not so.” — Galileo Galilei
Why does this matter?

Current is one of the most important ideas in electricity because it connects microscopic charge motion to the behavior of real devices. Understanding current, power sources, and measurement helps students make sense of batteries, outlets, chargers, and every electrical system they encounter.

Learning Targets

Students will define current, describe how power sources drive charge flow, and interpret measurements in simple circuits.

  • I can calculate current from charge and time using $I = \frac{Q}{t}$ and solve for charge using $Q = It$.
  • I can describe how batteries and other power sources provide energy that drives current in a circuit.
  • I can distinguish between conventional current and electron flow.
  • I can interpret current and voltage measurements taken with basic circuit tools or diagrams.

Introduction

When you plug in a lamp and flip the switch, the light does not wait for a single electron to travel all the way from the power source to the bulb. Instead, the entire circuit responds because electric charges in the wire begin moving in an organized way. That motion is electric current.

Current is not something separate from charge. It is a way of describing how much charge passes a point in a certain amount of time. This makes current a rate, just like speed is distance per time.

To understand circuits, students also need to understand where the energy comes from and how current is measured. Batteries, solar cells, and generators provide energy to charges, while tools such as ammeters help us measure how much charge is flowing.

Key Idea: Electric current measures the rate of charge flow. Power sources provide energy, and measurement tools help us observe what the circuit is doing.

History

Early scientists studying electricity first focused on static charge, but the discovery of continuous electric current opened the door to modern electrical technology. In 1800, Alessandro Volta developed the voltaic pile, an early battery that could produce a steady current.

This invention allowed scientists to study current in ways that static electricity never could. Researchers such as André-Marie Ampère investigated the relationship between current and magnetism, and the unit of current was later named the ampere in his honor.

As electrical science developed, engineers created better sources of electrical energy and more accurate instruments for measuring current, voltage, and resistance. These advances made it possible to design reliable circuits and power systems.

Today, current and measurement remain central to everything from small electronics to national power grids.

Key Concepts

Electric Current

Electric current is the flow of electric charge through a conductor. It describes how much charge passes a point in a given amount of time.

$$I = \frac{Q}{t}$$

$I$ = current $\left(\text{A}\right)$
$Q$ = charge $\left(\text{C}\right)$
$t$ = time $\left(\text{s}\right)$

Current is a rate, meaning it tells how quickly charge is moving through a circuit rather than how much charge is present.

Ampere

The ampere (A) is the SI unit of electric current. It measures how much charge flows each second.

$$1\,\text{A} = 1\,\text{C/s}$$

A current of one ampere means one coulomb of charge passes a point every second.

Charge Flow and Drift Velocity

In a conductor, electric current is carried by moving charges. In metal wires, these charges are electrons.

Although electrons move randomly, an electric field causes them to drift in a preferred direction. This average motion is called drift velocity.

Key Idea: Drift velocity is very small, but the electric field causes charges throughout the circuit to respond quickly.

Conventional Current

Conventional current is defined as the direction that positive charge would move in a circuit.

In metal wires, electrons actually move in the opposite direction, but circuit diagrams and equations use conventional current for consistency.

Key Idea: Conventional current direction is opposite the motion of electrons in a conductor.

Power Sources

A power source provides electrical energy to a circuit. It does not create charge, but instead supplies energy that allows charges to move.

Examples of power sources:
  • Batteries - chemical cells
  • Solar cells - photoelectric effect
  • Generators

Without a power source, current cannot be sustained in a circuit.

Chemical Cells and Electrodes

A chemical cell converts chemical energy into electrical energy. A battery consists of one or more cells.

Each cell has two electrodes, which are the terminals where current enters and leaves the circuit.

Key Idea: Chemical reactions inside a cell create a potential difference that drives current.

Electrolyte

An electrolyte is a substance inside a chemical cell that allows ions to move and complete the internal circuit.

Without an electrolyte, the chemical cell cannot maintain current because charge cannot flow internally.

Measuring Current

Current is measured using an ammeter. To measure current correctly, the ammeter must be placed in series with the circuit so the same current flows through the meter.

Draw simple circuit with ammeter

An ideal ammeter has very low resistance so it does not significantly affect the current it measures.

Draw simple circuit with volt meter

Measuring Voltage

Voltage is measured using a voltmeter. A voltmeter is connected in parallel across two points in a circuit to measure potential difference.

An ideal voltmeter has very high resistance so it does not draw significant current from the circuit.

Multimeter

A multimeter is a device that can measure voltage, current, and resistance. It combines the functions of a voltmeter and an ammeter into one tool.

Correct setup is important. Using the wrong setting or connecting it incorrectly can give inaccurate readings or damage the device.

Strategies

Given → Find → Equation → Solve → Check Units

  1. Given: List known values for charge, time, or current.
  2. Find: Decide whether the problem asks for current, charge, or time.
  3. Equation: Use $I = \frac{Q}{t}$ or rearrange it if needed.
  4. Solve: Substitute carefully and isolate the unknown.
  5. Check Units: Make sure the final unit matches the quantity: amperes, coulombs, or seconds.

Circuit Diagram Tips

  • Mark the current direction using conventional current unless told otherwise.
  • Place an ammeter in series, not across a component.
  • Label the power source and the measured part of the circuit clearly.

Unit Analysis Tips

  • $1\,\text{A} = 1\,\text{C/s}$ is the key conversion.
  • If charge is in coulombs and time is in seconds, current will be in amperes.
  • A very large current means a large rate of charge flow, not necessarily very high electron speed.
Key Idea: Interpreting current correctly means thinking about how much charge passes a point each second.

Common Mistakes

  • Confusing current with charge: Current is a rate of charge flow, not the charge itself.
  • Mixing up current direction: Conventional current and electron flow are opposite in metal wires.
  • Incorrect ammeter placement: Ammeters must be placed in series, not in parallel across a component.
  • Unit errors: Students may forget that milliamperes must be converted to amperes.
  • Treating a battery as a source of charge: A battery provides energy, not new charge.
  • Ignoring time: More charge does not always mean more current unless time is also considered.

Calculations

5. A total charge of $12\,\text{C}$ passes in $4.0\,\text{s}$.

$$I = \frac{Q}{t} = \frac{12}{4.0} = 3.0\,\text{A}$$

Answer: $\boxed{3.0\,\text{A}}$

6. A device transfers $30\,\text{C}$ in $6.0\,\text{s}$, then runs for $18\,\text{s}$ total.

First, find the current:

$$I = \frac{Q}{t} = \frac{30}{6.0} = 5.0\,\text{A}$$

Then use the total time:

$$Q = It = (5.0)(18) = 90\,\text{C}$$

Answer: $\boxed{90\,\text{C}}$

Analyze & Explain

7. The statement is not necessarily correct. Current depends on how much charge passes a point per second, not just how fast individual charges move.

In many conductors, especially metals, the drift velocity of electrons is very small. However, a large number of charges moving together can still produce a large current. Increasing current often means more charges are moving, not that each charge is moving much faster.

Detailed Example Solutions

Example 1 — Current

Question: A total charge of $20\,\text{C}$ passes through a wire in $5.0\,\text{s}$. What is the current?

$$I = \frac{Q}{t} = \frac{20}{5.0} = 4.0\,\text{A}$$

Answer: $\boxed{4.0\,\text{A}}$

Example 2 — Charge

Question: A circuit carries a current of $3.5\,\text{A}$ for $4.0\,\text{s}$. How much charge flows through the circuit?

$$Q = It = (3.5)(4.0) = 14\,\text{C}$$

Answer: $\boxed{14\,\text{C}}$

Example 3 — Multi-Step Scenario

Question: A device operates with a current of $2.0\,\text{A}$ for $6.0\,\text{s}$. If the device continues to run for a total of $18\,\text{s}$, how much total charge flows?

Use total time:

$$Q = It = (2.0)(18) = 36\,\text{C}$$

Answer: $\boxed{36\,\text{C}}$

Example 4 — Multi-Step Scenario

Question: A battery-powered device transfers $36\,\text{C}$ of charge in $12\,\text{s}$. If the current is reduced to half its original value, how much charge flows in the same time?

Step 1: Find original current

$$I = \frac{36}{12} = 3.0\,\text{A}$$

Step 2: New current

$$I = 1.5\,\text{A}$$

Step 3: Find charge

$$Q = (1.5)(12) = 18\,\text{C}$$

Answer: $\boxed{18\,\text{C}}$
