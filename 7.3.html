7.3 Current, Power Sources, and Measurement

Lights turn on, phones charge, and motors spin because electric charge moves through a circuit. That flow of charge is called current.
This section connects the idea of moving charge to the devices that provide electrical energy and the tools used to measure what is happening in a circuit.
“Measure what is measurable, and make measurable what is not so.” — Galileo Galilei
Why does this matter?

Current is one of the most important ideas in electricity because it connects microscopic charge motion to the behavior of real devices. Understanding current, power sources, and measurement helps students make sense of batteries, outlets, chargers, and every electrical system they encounter.

Introduction

When you plug in a lamp and flip the switch, the light does not wait for a single electron to travel all the way from the power source to the bulb. Instead, the entire circuit responds because electric charges in the wire begin moving in an organized way. That motion is electric current.

Current is not something separate from charge. It is a way of describing how much charge passes a point in a certain amount of time. This makes current a rate, just like speed is distance per time.

To understand circuits, students also need to understand where the energy comes from and how current is measured. Batteries, solar cells, and generators provide energy to charges, while tools such as ammeters help us measure how much charge is flowing.

Key Idea: Electric current measures the rate of charge flow. Power sources provide energy, and measurement tools help us observe what the circuit is doing.

History

Early scientists studying electricity first focused on static charge, but the discovery of continuous electric current opened the door to modern electrical technology. In 1800, Alessandro Volta developed the voltaic pile, an early battery that could produce a steady current.

This invention allowed scientists to study current in ways that static electricity never could. Researchers such as André-Marie Ampère investigated the relationship between current and magnetism, and the unit of current was later named the ampere in his honor.

As electrical science developed, engineers created better sources of electrical energy and more accurate instruments for measuring current, voltage, and resistance. These advances made it possible to design reliable circuits and power systems.

Today, current and measurement remain central to everything from small electronics to national power grids.

Key Concepts

Electric Current

Electric current is the rate at which charge flows past a point in a circuit. If more charge passes each second, the current is larger.

$$I = \frac{Q}{t}$$

Current is measured in amperes (A), where $$1\,\text{A} = 1\,\text{C/s}$$

This means one ampere is one coulomb of charge passing a point each second.

Conventional Current and Electron Flow

Historically, scientists defined current direction as the direction positive charge would move. This is called conventional current.

In metal wires, the actual moving particles are electrons, which are negatively charged. Because electrons move opposite the direction of conventional current, electron flow and current direction are opposite.

Key Idea: Circuits are usually labeled using conventional current, even though electrons move the other way in a metal conductor.

Charge Carriers and Drift Velocity

Charges in a conductor do not move randomly only. When a circuit is connected to a power source, the electric field in the wire causes the charge carriers to drift in a preferred direction.

This average directional motion is called drift velocity. Even though the drift speed of electrons is often small, the electrical effect through the circuit is established quickly.

Power Sources

A power source is a device that provides electrical energy to charges in a circuit. Batteries, chemical cells, generators, and solar cells are all examples of power sources.

Examples of power sources:
  • Chemical cells convert chemical energy into electrical energy.
  • Solar cells convert light energy into electrical energy.
  • Generators convert mechanical energy into electrical energy.

A power source does not create charge. Instead, it gives energy to charges already present in the circuit.

Electrodes and Circuit Terminals

Many power sources have two terminals, often called electrodes in chemical cells. These terminals create a voltage difference that pushes charges through the circuit.

The arrangement of chemical or physical conditions at the terminals helps maintain the electric potential difference that keeps current moving.

Measuring Current

Current is measured with an ammeter. To measure current correctly, the ammeter must be placed in series with the part of the circuit being measured so that the same charge flow passes through the meter.

If the meter is placed incorrectly, the reading will not represent the current through the intended part of the circuit.

Interpreting Current

A larger current means more charge passes a point each second. That does not automatically mean the charges are moving extremely fast. Instead, it means the overall rate of charge flow is greater.

Current is best understood as a rate: $$\text{current} = \text{charge flow per second}$$

Worked Derivation

Current is defined as a rate of charge flow. In words, that means:

$$\text{current} = \frac{\text{charge}}{\text{time}}$$

Replacing the words with symbols gives: $$I = \frac{Q}{t}$$

If one coulomb of charge passes a point every second, then: $$I = \frac{1\,\text{C}}{1\,\text{s}} = 1\,\text{A}$$

Why this matters: Students should see current as a rate, not as a separate substance moving through the wire.

Strategies

Given → Find → Equation → Solve → Check Units

  1. Given: List known values for charge, time, or current.
  2. Find: Decide whether the problem asks for current, charge, or time.
  3. Equation: Use $I = \frac{Q}{t}$ or rearrange it if needed.
  4. Solve: Substitute carefully and isolate the unknown.
  5. Check Units: Make sure the final unit matches the quantity: amperes, coulombs, or seconds.

Circuit Diagram Tips

  • Mark the current direction using conventional current unless told otherwise.
  • Place an ammeter in series, not across a component.
  • Label the power source and the measured part of the circuit clearly.

Unit Analysis Tips

  • $1\,\text{A} = 1\,\text{C/s}$ is the key conversion.
  • If charge is in coulombs and time is in seconds, current will be in amperes.
  • A very large current means a large rate of charge flow, not necessarily very high electron speed.
Key Idea: Interpreting current correctly means thinking about how much charge passes a point each second.

Common Mistakes

  • Confusing current with charge: Current is a rate of charge flow, not the charge itself.
  • Mixing up current direction: Conventional current and electron flow are opposite in metal wires.
  • Incorrect ammeter placement: Ammeters must be placed in series, not in parallel across a component.
  • Unit errors: Students may forget that milliamperes must be converted to amperes.
  • Treating a battery as a source of charge: A battery provides energy, not new charge.
  • Ignoring time: More charge does not always mean more current unless time is also considered.

Summary

  • Electric current is the rate of charge flow.
  • The equation for current is $I = \frac{Q}{t}$.
  • $1\,\text{A} = 1\,\text{C/s}$.
  • Conventional current points in the direction positive charge would move.
  • Power sources provide energy to charges in a circuit.
  • Current is measured with an ammeter placed in series.

Detailed Example Solutions

Example 1 — Conceptual

Conventional current is defined as the direction positive charge would move. In a metal wire, the moving charges are electrons, so the electrons move opposite the direction of conventional current.

Example 2 — Single-Formula

Given: $Q = 12\,\text{C}$, $t = 4.0\,\text{s}$

Equation:

$$I = \frac{Q}{t}$$

Solve:

$$I = \frac{12}{4.0} = 3.0\,\text{A}$$

Example 3 — Multi-Step

Given: $I = 3.0\,\text{A}$, $t = 8.0\,\text{s}$

Rearrange the equation: $$Q = It$$

Now substitute: $$Q = (3.0)(8.0) = 24\,\text{C}$$

Example 4 — Unit Conversion Embedded

Milli means one-thousandth: $$250\,\text{mA} = 0.250\,\text{A}$$

Example 5 — Graph / Relationship Interpretation

From $$I = \frac{Q}{t}$$ if the same charge flows in less time, the denominator becomes smaller, so the current becomes larger.

Example 6 — Measurement

The ammeter must be placed in series with the lamp so the same current that passes through the lamp also passes through the meter.

Example 7 — Draw It

A correct sketch shows a closed loop with a battery, a lamp, and arrows showing conventional current moving from the positive terminal through the circuit and back to the negative terminal.
