SECTION 7.3

Current, Power Sources, and Measurement

Electric current powers our modern world—from the lights in your home to the smartphone in your pocket. Current is the flow of electric charge through a conductor, and understanding how it works is essential to mastering electricity. This section explores what current is, how we measure it, and how batteries and solar cells create the voltage that drives current.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this section, I can:

  • Define electric current in terms of charge flow, explain conventional current direction, and measure current accurately using an ammeter in a circuit.
  • Describe how chemical cells and solar cells generate voltage through energy conversion, and explain the differences between these two types of power sources.
  • Use ammeters and voltmeters correctly in circuits to measure current and voltage, and interpret circuit measurements to determine circuit behavior.

Introduction

Every time you flip a light switch, plug in your phone charger, or start your car, you're using electric current. Current is one of the most practical concepts in physics—it's the foundation of all electrical technology. But what exactly is electric current, and how does it differ from the static electricity we studied in previous sections?

Think about water flowing through a pipe. The water doesn't just sit there—it moves from one place to another, carrying energy with it. Electric current is similar: it's the flow of electric charge through a conductor. While static electricity involves stationary charges (like on a rubbed balloon), current electricity involves charges in motion.

Understanding current requires us to answer several key questions: What makes charges flow? How do we measure this flow? What devices create the voltage that drives current? And how do we safely measure electrical quantities in real circuits? This section will answer all of these questions and prepare you to work with practical electrical circuits.

What Is Electric Current?

Electric current is the rate of flow of electric charge past a point in a circuit. When charges move through a conductor like a wire, we say that a current exists. Current is measured by how much charge flows past a point per unit time.

Key Definition: Electric Current

Electric Current: The rate of flow of electric charge. Mathematically:

$$I = \frac{Q}{t}$$

where $I$ is current (amperes), $Q$ is charge (coulombs), and $t$ is time (seconds).

The unit of electric current is the ampere (A), named after French physicist André-Marie Ampère. One ampere is defined as one coulomb of charge passing a point per second:

$$1 \text{ A} = 1 \text{ C/s}$$

For example, if 3 coulombs of charge flow past a point in a wire in 2 seconds, the current is $I = 3 \text{ C} / 2 \text{ s} = 1.5$ A.

The Mechanism of Current Flow

In a metal wire, electric current is carried by electrons—the negatively charged particles that surround atomic nuclei. When a voltage difference is applied across a wire, these free electrons drift through the metal, creating an electric current.

Here's what happens at the microscopic level:

  • Metal atoms have loosely bound outer electrons that can move freely through the material
  • When a voltage is applied, these free electrons experience an electric force
  • The electrons drift slowly in the direction opposite to the electric field (because they're negative)
  • This organized drift of electrons constitutes an electric current

It's important to note that individual electrons move quite slowly—typically less than 1 millimeter per second! This slow drift speed is called the drift velocity. However, the electric signal (the field that causes the drift) propagates through the wire at nearly the speed of light. This is why your light turns on instantly when you flip a switch, even though individual electrons are crawling along.

Analogy: Water in a Pipe

Think of electric current like water flowing through a pipe. The voltage is like water pressure—it's what pushes the water (charges) through the pipe (wire). The current is the flow rate—how much water passes a point each second. Just as you need pressure to make water flow, you need voltage to make charges flow. And just as pipes can have different diameters affecting flow rate, conductors have different properties (resistance) that affect current.

Conventional Current vs. Electron Flow

There's an important historical quirk we need to address. When scientists first studied electricity in the 1700s and 1800s, they didn't know about electrons. Benjamin Franklin proposed that electric current was the flow of positive charge from positive to negative. This convention stuck, and today we still define conventional current as flowing from the positive terminal of a battery through the circuit to the negative terminal.

However, we now know that in metal wires, it's actually negative electrons that flow, and they flow in the opposite direction—from negative to positive. So which direction should we use?

In physics, we typically use conventional current direction—from positive to negative—even though electrons actually move the other way. This convention works perfectly well for analyzing circuits, and it's what engineers and scientists use worldwide. Just remember: conventional current flows from + to −, but electrons flow from − to +.

Which Direction Should You Use?

For solving circuit problems and analyzing electric circuits, always use conventional current direction (positive to negative). This is the standard convention in physics and engineering. Thinking about electron flow is useful for understanding the microscopic mechanism, but for circuit analysis, stick with conventional current.

Power Sources: Batteries and Cells

For current to flow continuously in a circuit, there must be a potential difference (voltage) to push the charges. This voltage is provided by a power source such as a battery. But how do batteries create voltage?

Chemical Cells (Batteries)

A chemical cell (commonly called a battery) converts chemical energy into electrical energy. Inside the cell, chemical reactions separate positive and negative charges, creating a voltage difference between the two terminals.

Here's how a simple chemical cell works:

  • Two electrodes: The cell contains two different metals (such as zinc and copper) called electrodes
  • Electrolyte: The electrodes are immersed in a conducting solution called an electrolyte
  • Chemical reactions: At one electrode, atoms lose electrons (oxidation). At the other electrode, atoms gain electrons (reduction)
  • Charge separation: These reactions create a buildup of electrons at one terminal (negative) and a deficit at the other (positive)
  • Voltage: The charge separation creates a voltage difference between the terminals

Common types of chemical cells include:

  • Alkaline batteries: The kind you buy at the store for flashlights (1.5 V each)
  • Lithium-ion batteries: Rechargeable batteries used in phones and laptops (3.7 V typical)
  • Lead-acid batteries: Heavy-duty batteries used in cars (12 V)
  • Nickel-metal hydride batteries: Rechargeable batteries used in some electronics

Historical Note: Alessandro Volta's Invention

The first true battery was invented in 1800 by Italian physicist Alessandro Volta. His "voltaic pile" consisted of alternating discs of zinc and copper separated by cardboard soaked in salt water. When he touched wires to the top and bottom of the stack, current flowed! This invention proved that electricity could flow continuously through a circuit, not just in brief sparks as with static electricity. Volta's work revolutionized science and made all modern electrical technology possible.

Solar Cells (Photovoltaic Cells)

A solar cell (also called a photovoltaic cell) converts light energy directly into electrical energy. Unlike chemical cells, which use chemical reactions, solar cells use the photoelectric effect—the ability of light to knock electrons loose from atoms.

Here's how solar cells work:

  • Semiconductor material: Solar cells are made from semiconductors like silicon
  • Light absorption: When photons (particles of light) hit the cell, they transfer energy to electrons
  • Electron liberation: If the photon has enough energy, it knocks an electron free from its atom
  • Electric field: The cell is designed with an internal electric field that pushes freed electrons in one direction
  • Current flow: The moving electrons create a current that can be used in a circuit

Solar cells produce a voltage of about 0.5 V each when illuminated. Multiple cells are connected together to create solar panels with higher voltages (typically 12-48 V for practical applications).

Measuring Current and Voltage

To understand and analyze circuits, we need to measure two key quantities: current and voltage. We use specialized instruments called ammeters and voltmeters.

Measuring Current with an Ammeter

An ammeter is a device that measures electric current. To measure the current flowing through a component, the ammeter must be placed in series with that component—meaning the current must flow through the ammeter.

Key points about using ammeters:

  • Always connect in series: The ammeter must be part of the current path
  • Break the circuit: You must disconnect the circuit at one point and insert the ammeter
  • Observe polarity: Connect the positive (red) terminal toward the positive side of the battery
  • Low resistance: Ammeters are designed to have very low resistance so they don't significantly affect the current they're measuring

Warning: Don't Connect an Ammeter in Parallel!

Never connect an ammeter directly across a battery or power source (in parallel). Because ammeters have very low resistance, this would create a short circuit, allowing huge current to flow. This can damage the ammeter, drain the battery instantly, and create a safety hazard. Always connect ammeters in series with the circuit.

Measuring Voltage with a Voltmeter

A voltmeter measures the voltage difference (potential difference) between two points. Unlike an ammeter, a voltmeter is placed in parallel with the component you want to measure across.

Key points about using voltmeters:

  • Always connect in parallel: Place the voltmeter across the two points where you want to measure voltage
  • Don't break the circuit: The circuit continues to operate normally while you measure voltage
  • Observe polarity: Connect the positive (red) terminal to the higher voltage point
  • High resistance: Voltmeters are designed to have very high resistance so they draw minimal current and don't affect the circuit

Multimeters: The Swiss Army Knife of Electronics

In practice, most people use a multimeter—a single device that can measure current, voltage, and resistance. You select the desired measurement mode with a dial or buttons. Digital multimeters are inexpensive (often under $20) and are essential tools for anyone working with electronics. They can measure DC (direct current) and AC (alternating current) quantities, test batteries, check continuity, and more.

Example Problems

Example 1: Calculating Current from Charge Flow

Given:

  • A total charge of $Q = 15$ C flows past a point in a wire in $t = 3.0$ seconds

Find: The current in the wire.

Solution:

Step 1: Write the equation for current.

$$I = \frac{Q}{t}$$

Step 2: Substitute the known values.

$$I = \frac{15 \text{ C}}{3.0 \text{ s}} = 5.0 \text{ A}$$

Answer: The current is 5.0 A (amperes).

Example 2: Finding Charge from Current and Time

Given:

  • A current of $I = 0.25$ A flows through a circuit
  • The current flows for $t = 120$ seconds (2 minutes)

Find: The total charge that flowed through the circuit.

Solution:

Step 1: Write the equation for current and rearrange for charge.

$$I = \frac{Q}{t} \quad \Rightarrow \quad Q = I \cdot t$$

Step 2: Substitute the known values.

$$Q = (0.25 \text{ A})(120 \text{ s}) = 30 \text{ C}$$

Answer: A total of 30 coulombs of charge flowed through the circuit.

Example 3: Multi-Step Problem—Current, Electrons, and Energy

Given:

  • A 9.0 V battery drives a current of $I = 0.50$ A through a circuit for $t = 10$ minutes
  • The charge on one electron is $e = 1.60 \times 10^{-19}$ C

Find:

(a) The total charge that flows through the circuit.

(b) The number of electrons that flow through the circuit.

(c) The total electrical energy supplied by the battery.

Solution:

(a) Total charge

Step 1: Convert time to seconds: $t = 10 \text{ min} = 600 \text{ s}$

Step 2: Calculate charge using $Q = It$.

$$Q = (0.50 \text{ A})(600 \text{ s}) = 300 \text{ C}$$

(b) Number of electrons

Step 3: Each electron carries charge $e = 1.60 \times 10^{-19}$ C. The number of electrons is:

$$N = \frac{Q}{e} = \frac{300 \text{ C}}{1.60 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C/electron}}$$ $$N = 1.875 \times 10^{21} \text{ electrons}$$

(c) Electrical energy

Step 4: The electrical energy supplied by a battery is given by $E = QV$.

$$E = (300 \text{ C})(9.0 \text{ V}) = 2{,}700 \text{ J}$$

Answers:

(a) 300 coulombs of charge flows through the circuit.

(b) 1.9 × 10²¹ electrons flow through the circuit (nearly 2 billion trillion electrons!).

(c) The battery supplies 2,700 joules of energy (equivalent to 2.7 kilojoules).

Summary

  • Electric current is the rate of flow of electric charge, defined as $I = Q/t$, where $I$ is current (amperes), $Q$ is charge (coulombs), and $t$ is time (seconds).
  • One ampere equals one coulomb of charge flowing past a point per second: 1 A = 1 C/s.
  • In metal wires, current is carried by electrons that drift slowly through the material when a voltage is applied.
  • Conventional current is defined as flowing from positive to negative, even though electrons actually flow from negative to positive. We use conventional current direction for circuit analysis.
  • Chemical cells (batteries) convert chemical energy into electrical energy through chemical reactions that separate charges, creating a voltage between terminals.
  • Solar cells convert light energy directly into electrical energy through the photoelectric effect, where photons knock electrons free from atoms.
  • Chemical cells work in darkness and have high power density but have limited lifetimes. Solar cells are renewable and produce no waste but require light and are initially expensive.
  • An ammeter measures current and must be connected in series with the circuit so current flows through it. Ammeters have low resistance.
  • A voltmeter measures voltage difference and must be connected in parallel across the component being measured. Voltmeters have high resistance.
  • Multimeters combine the functions of ammeters, voltmeters, and ohmmeters (resistance meters) in one device, making them practical tools for circuit work.

Answer Key

1. Electric current is the rate of flow of electric charge through a conductor, measured in amperes (A). Static electricity involves stationary charges that accumulate on objects, while current electricity involves charges in continuous motion through a conductor. For current to flow, there must be: (1) a closed conducting path (complete circuit), (2) a voltage difference (potential difference) to push the charges, and (3) free charges available to move (such as electrons in a metal wire).

2. Conventional current is defined as flowing from the positive terminal to the negative terminal, representing the direction positive charges would move. Electron flow is the actual motion of electrons, which are negative, so they flow from negative to positive—opposite to conventional current. When analyzing circuits, you should use conventional current direction (positive to negative) because this is the standard convention used by physicists and engineers worldwide. Circuit analysis works correctly with either convention, but conventional current is the established standard.

3. Chemical cells convert chemical energy into electrical energy through chemical reactions that separate charges at two different electrodes, creating a voltage. Solar cells convert light energy into electrical energy through the photoelectric effect, where photons knock electrons free from semiconductor atoms. Advantage of chemical cells: they work in darkness and are portable. Advantage of solar cells: they are renewable (don't consume fuel) and produce no chemical waste. Both have trade-offs in cost, lifetime, and practical applications.

4. An ammeter must be connected in series with the circuit—you break the circuit at one point and insert the ammeter so current flows through it. A voltmeter must be connected in parallel across the component you're measuring—you connect it to two points without breaking the circuit. It's critical to connect them correctly because: (1) an ammeter connected in parallel would create a short circuit due to its low resistance, potentially damaging the ammeter and circuit; (2) a voltmeter connected in series would block current due to its high resistance, preventing the circuit from functioning.
