SECTION 6.2

Refraction and Snell's Law

Have you ever noticed that a stick partially submerged in water appears bent at the water's surface? Or that objects underwater look closer to the surface than they actually are? These phenomena occur because light bends when it travels from one material to another. This bending of light is called refraction, and it follows a precise mathematical relationship known as Snell's Law.

Learning Objectives

  • I can explain why light bends when it passes between materials with different optical densities
  • I can use Snell's Law ($n_1 \sin \theta_1 = n_2 \sin \theta_2$) to calculate angles of refraction
  • I can predict the direction light will bend based on which material it is entering
  • I can describe real-world examples of refraction such as objects appearing in water or mirages

Introduction

In the previous section, we learned that light reflects off surfaces following the law of reflection. But what happens when light travels through transparent materials like glass, water, or plastic instead of bouncing off a surface? The answer is that light refracts—it bends.

Refraction is the bending of light when it passes from one material to another. Unlike reflection, which happens at the surface, refraction happens as light travels through the material. The reason light bends is that light travels at different speeds in different materials.

Historical Note: Snell and Descartes

The law of refraction was discovered independently by Willebrord Snell (a Dutch astronomer) and René Descartes (a French mathematician) in the early 1600s. Though Snell discovered it first, the law is sometimes called Snell's Law. This law is absolutely fundamental to understanding lenses, glasses, telescopes, and fiber optic cables that transmit information around the world.

In this section, you will learn why light refracts, how to predict the direction it will bend, and how to use mathematics to calculate angles of refraction. Understanding refraction is essential for understanding how lenses work and for explaining many optical phenomena we observe in nature.

Why Does Light Bend? Optical Density and the Speed of Light

To understand why light refracts, we need to think about how fast light travels in different materials. In empty space (a vacuum), light travels at its maximum speed: $c = 3 \times 10^8$ m/s. However, when light enters a material like glass or water, it slows down.

The refractive index (n) of a material tells us how much slower light travels in that material compared to empty space. It is defined mathematically as:

Refractive Index

$$n = \frac{c}{v}$$

where $c = 3 \times 10^8$ m/s (speed of light in vacuum) and $v$ = speed of light in the material

The optical density of a material is related to its refractive index. A material with a high refractive index (like diamond) is optically dense, meaning light travels slowly through it. A material with a low refractive index (like air) is optically thin, meaning light travels quickly through it.

Refractive Index of Common Materials

Material Refractive Index (n)
Vacuum 1.00 (by definition)
Air 1.00 (approximately)
Water 1.33
Glass (typical) 1.50
Diamond 2.42

When light travels from air (n = 1.00) into water (n = 1.33), the light slows down. This change in speed causes the light to change direction—it bends. The direction and amount of bending depend on the refractive indices of the two materials and the angle at which the light strikes the interface.

Key Insight: Higher Refractive Index = More Bending

Light bends more when it travels between materials with very different refractive indices (like air and diamond) than when it travels between materials with similar refractive indices (like air and water). This is because the change in speed is greater.

Snell's Law

The precise mathematical relationship between the angle of incidence, angle of refraction, and refractive indices is called Snell's Law. It states:

Snell's Law

$$n_1 \sin \theta_1 = n_2 \sin \theta_2$$

where:

$n_1$ = refractive index of the first material

$\theta_1$ = angle of incidence (measured from the normal)

$n_2$ = refractive index of the second material

$\theta_2$ = angle of refraction (measured from the normal)

Notice that Snell's Law is similar to the law of reflection, but with an important difference: the angles are not equal. Instead, the product of the refractive index and the sine of the angle in each material are equal.

Rules for Predicting the Direction of Refraction

Snell's Law tells us the exact amount of bending, but we can also predict the general direction by remembering these simple rules:

When Light Enters a More Optically Dense Material (Higher n):

Light bends toward the normal. The angle of refraction is smaller than the angle of incidence.

Example: Light entering from air (n = 1.00) into water (n = 1.33) bends toward the normal.

When Light Enters a Less Optically Dense Material (Lower n):

Light bends away from the normal. The angle of refraction is larger than the angle of incidence.

Example: Light entering from water (n = 1.33) into air (n = 1.00) bends away from the normal.

When Light Hits Perpendicularly:

If light strikes the surface straight on (perpendicular to the interface), the angle of incidence is 0°. In this case, light does not bend at all—it continues straight through.

Why Objects Underwater Appear Closer

When you look at an object underwater, light from the object refracts as it leaves the water and enters the air. This refraction makes the object appear to be closer to the surface and closer to the normal than it actually is. This is why a fish underwater appears shallower than it really is, and why you have to aim lower when trying to spear a fish with a stick!

Example Problems

Example 1: Calculating the Angle of Refraction

Given: Light travels from air ($n_1 = 1.00$) into water ($n_2 = 1.33$). The angle of incidence is 45°.

Find: The angle of refraction.

Solution:

Step 1: Write Snell's Law.

$$n_1 \sin \theta_1 = n_2 \sin \theta_2$$

Step 2: Substitute the known values.

$$(1.00) \sin(45°) = (1.33) \sin(\theta_2)$$

Step 3: Calculate $\sin(45°)$ and solve for $\sin(\theta_2)$.

$$(1.00)(0.707) = (1.33) \sin(\theta_2)$$

$$0.707 = (1.33) \sin(\theta_2)$$

$$\sin(\theta_2) = \frac{0.707}{1.33} = 0.531$$

Step 4: Find $\theta_2$ by taking the inverse sine.

$$\theta_2 = \sin^{-1}(0.531) = 32.1°$$

Answer: The angle of refraction is 32.1°. Notice that θ₂ (32.1°) is smaller than θ₁ (45°), as expected when light enters a more optically dense material.

Example 2: Predicting Direction of Refraction

Given: Light travels from glass ($n_1 = 1.50$) into air ($n_2 = 1.00$). The angle of incidence is 30°.

Find: Predict whether the light bends toward or away from the normal. Then calculate the angle of refraction.

Solution:

Step 1: Determine the direction of bending.

Light is going from a denser material (glass, n = 1.50) to a less dense material (air, n = 1.00). Light bends away from the normal. We expect θ₂ > θ₁.

Step 2: Apply Snell's Law.

$$n_1 \sin \theta_1 = n_2 \sin \theta_2$$

$$(1.50) \sin(30°) = (1.00) \sin(\theta_2)$$

Step 3: Calculate $\sin(30°)$ and solve for $\sin(\theta_2)$.

$$(1.50)(0.500) = (1.00) \sin(\theta_2)$$

$$\sin(\theta_2) = 0.750$$

$$\theta_2 = \sin^{-1}(0.750) = 48.6°$$

Answer: The light bends away from the normal. The angle of refraction is 48.6°, which is larger than the 30° angle of incidence, confirming our prediction.

Example 3: Finding the Incident Angle

Given: Light travels from water ($n_1 = 1.33$) into glass ($n_2 = 1.50$). The angle of refraction in the glass is 25°.

Find: What was the angle of incidence in the water?

Solution:

Step 1: Write Snell's Law and rearrange to solve for $\theta_1$.

$$n_1 \sin \theta_1 = n_2 \sin \theta_2$$

$$\sin \theta_1 = \frac{n_2 \sin \theta_2}{n_1}$$

Step 2: Substitute the values.

$$\sin \theta_1 = \frac{(1.50) \sin(25°)}{1.33}$$

$$\sin \theta_1 = \frac{(1.50)(0.423)}{1.33} = \frac{0.634}{1.33} = 0.477$$

Step 3: Find $\theta_1$.

$$\theta_1 = \sin^{-1}(0.477) = 28.5°$$

Answer: The angle of incidence was 28.5°. Notice that θ₁ (28.5°) is larger than θ₂ (25°), which is expected because light goes from a less dense material (water) to a more dense material (glass).

Summary

Key Concepts

  • Refraction is the bending of light when it passes from one material to another
  • Light travels at different speeds in different materials
  • The refractive index of a material tells us how much slower light travels in that material compared to empty space
  • Optical density is related to refractive index; higher n means more optically dense
  • Snell's Law mathematically relates the angles and refractive indices: $n_1 \sin \theta_1 = n_2 \sin \theta_2$
  • When light enters a more optically dense material, it bends toward the normal
  • When light enters a less optically dense material, it bends away from the normal
  • When light hits a surface perpendicularly, it does not bend
  • Refraction explains why objects underwater appear closer to the surface than they really are
  • All angles in Snell's Law are measured from the normal, not from the surface

Answer Key

Conceptual Questions

1. Explain why light bends when it passes from one material to another. What physical property causes this bending?

Answer: Light bends (refracts) because it travels at different speeds in different materials. The optical density of the material, described by its refractive index, causes light to slow down or speed up. When light speed changes, the direction of the light ray changes as well. The greater the difference in speed between the two materials, the more the light bends.

2. When light enters a material with a higher refractive index, does it bend toward or away from the normal? Explain your answer.

Answer: Light bends toward the normal when entering a material with a higher refractive index (more optically dense). This happens because the light slows down, and the change in speed causes the ray to bend toward the normal. The higher the refractive index of the material it's entering, the greater the bending toward the normal.

3. Would light refract more when going from air (n = 1.00) into water (n = 1.33), or when going from air into diamond (n = 2.42)? Explain.

Answer: Light would refract more when going from air into diamond because diamond has a much higher refractive index (2.42 vs. 1.33). The greater the difference in refractive indices between two materials, the more light bends when passing between them. The light experiences a much greater change in speed when entering diamond compared to entering water.

4. Why do objects underwater appear to be closer to the surface than they actually are?

Answer: When light from an underwater object travels upward through the water-air interface, it refracts (bends away from the normal). This refraction makes the light ray appear to come from a shallower angle than it actually did. Our brain traces the light ray backward in a straight line, making the object appear to be closer to the surface than it really is. This is why fish appear shallower than their actual depth.

Calculation Problems

5. Light travels from air (n = 1.00) into glass (n = 1.50). If the angle of incidence is 40°, what is the angle of refraction?

Solution:

Using Snell's Law: $n_1 \sin \theta_1 = n_2 \sin \theta_2$

$(1.00) \sin(40°) = (1.50) \sin(\theta_2)$

$(1.00)(0.643) = (1.50) \sin(\theta_2)$

$\sin(\theta_2) = \frac{0.643}{1.50} = 0.429$

$\theta_2 = \sin^{-1}(0.429) = 25.4°$

Answer: The angle of refraction is approximately 25.4°. (Note: θ₂ < θ₁, as expected when entering a more optically dense material.)

6. Light travels from glass (n = 1.50) into water (n = 1.33). The angle of incidence is 35°. Calculate the angle of refraction.

Solution:

Using Snell's Law: $n_1 \sin \theta_1 = n_2 \sin \theta_2$

$(1.50) \sin(35°) = (1.33) \sin(\theta_2)$

$(1.50)(0.574) = (1.33) \sin(\theta_2)$

$0.861 = (1.33) \sin(\theta_2)$

$\sin(\theta_2) = \frac{0.861}{1.33} = 0.647$

$\theta_2 = \sin^{-1}(0.647) = 40.3°$

Answer: The angle of refraction is approximately 40.3°. (Note: θ₂ > θ₁, as expected when entering a less optically dense material.)

Multi-Step Problems

7. A light ray hits the water surface at an angle of incidence of 60°.

Solution:

a) Air to water (air is less dense):

$n_{air} \sin \theta_{air} = n_{water} \sin \theta_{water}$

$(1.00) \sin(60°) = (1.33) \sin(\theta_{water})$

$(1.00)(0.866) = (1.33) \sin(\theta_{water})$

$\sin(\theta_{water}) = \frac{0.866}{1.33} = 0.651$

$\theta_{water} = \sin^{-1}(0.651) = 40.6°$

b) Water to glass (water is less dense):

$(1.33) \sin(40°) = (1.50) \sin(\theta_{glass})$

$(1.33)(0.643) = (1.50) \sin(\theta_{glass})$

$0.855 = (1.50) \sin(\theta_{glass})$

$\sin(\theta_{glass}) = \frac{0.855}{1.50} = 0.570$

$\theta_{glass} = \sin^{-1}(0.570) = 34.8°$

c) The greatest change in direction occurs at the air-water interface (from 60° to 40.6°, a change of 19.4°) compared to the water-glass interface (from 40° to 34.8°, a change of 5.2°). This is because the difference in refractive indices is much larger between air and water (Δn = 0.33) than between water and glass (Δn = 0.17).

8. A fish swimming at a depth appears shallower due to refraction.

Answer:

a) When light from the fish exits the water and enters the air, it refracts away from the normal (because water has a higher refractive index than air). This makes the light ray bend away from the normal at the water-air interface. Our brain traces the light ray backward in a straight line, so we perceive the fish to be at a shallower angle, making it appear closer to the surface than it actually is.

b) Using Snell's Law (water to air, light bends away from normal):

$n_{water} \sin \theta_{water} = n_{air} \sin \theta_{air}$

$(1.33) \sin(25°) = (1.00) \sin(\theta_{air})$

$(1.33)(0.423) = (1.00) \sin(\theta_{air})$

$\sin(\theta_{air}) = 0.563$

$\theta_{air} = \sin^{-1}(0.563) = 34.3°$

c) The light bends away from the normal when leaving the water. This is because water (n = 1.33) is more optically dense than air (n = 1.00). When light exits to a less dense medium, it always bends away from the normal.
