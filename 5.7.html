SECTION 5.7

Speed of Sound

Have you ever noticed that you see lightning long before you hear thunder? This happens because light travels incredibly fast, while sound moves much more slowly through the air. Understanding how sound travels at different speeds through different materials reveals fascinating physics about waves, energy, and the world around us.

Learning Objectives

  • I can define the speed of sound and explain how it is measured
  • I can compare the speed of sound in different media and explain why these differences occur
  • I can describe how temperature affects the speed of sound and predict sound speed under various conditions
  • I can calculate distances and times related to sound travel using the speed of sound

Why Does Sound Travel at Different Speeds?

Think about what happens when you're at a football stadium and the kicker boots the ball. You see the ball fly through the air long before you hear the "kick" sound. In a thunderstorm, lightning illuminates the sky instantly, but the thunder rumbles several seconds later. These everyday experiences reveal a fundamental truth about physics: sound is not instantaneous; it travels at a measurable speed that depends on its surroundings.

Unlike light, which can travel through empty space, sound requires a medium—a substance through which the energy can travel. Sound cannot travel through a vacuum. This simple fact explains why sound moves at different speeds through air, water, and solids. Understanding the speed of sound is crucial for many real-world applications, from designing concert halls with proper acoustics to calculating distances to storms, to enabling underwater communication.

Real-World Connection: Measuring Storm Distance

When you observe a thunderstorm, you can estimate its distance by counting the seconds between the lightning flash and the thunder rumble. Since light reaches you almost instantly, the time delay is mainly due to sound's slower travel speed. For every 5 seconds you count, the storm is approximately 1 mile away (or about 3 seconds per kilometer). This practical application of sound speed has helped people gauge storm danger for centuries.

What Is the Speed of Sound?

The speed of sound is the distance that sound waves travel in a given amount of time. Like any speed, we measure it in distance per unit time: meters per second (m/s), kilometers per hour (km/h), or feet per second (ft/s).

You've probably heard that the speed of sound is about 343 meters per second. But here's an important detail: this value only applies to specific conditions. Specifically, 343 m/s is the speed of sound through dry air at exactly 20°C (68°F). Under different conditions—different temperatures or different materials—the speed of sound changes significantly. This means we need to think carefully about the conditions whenever we work with sound speed calculations.

Key Terms

Speed of sound: The distance sound waves travel per unit time, measured in m/s

Medium (plural: media): Any substance or material through which sound waves travel

Mechanical wave: A wave that requires a medium to travel; sound is a mechanical wave

Speed of Sound in Different Media

Sound is a mechanical wave, which means it requires matter to travel through. The substance through which sound travels is called the medium. Remarkably, sound travels at vastly different speeds depending on the medium.

Look at the data in the table below. Notice something surprising: sound travels fastest through solids, slower through liquids, and slowest through gases. Why? The answer lies in how particles are arranged.

Medium (at 20°C) Speed of Sound (m/s)
Dry Air 343
Water 1,437
Wood 3,850
Glass 4,540
Aluminum 6,320
Why Does Sound Travel Faster in Solids?

In a solid, atoms and molecules are packed tightly together and held in place by strong forces. When sound energy passes through, it transfers quickly from one particle to the next—like a closely packed crowd passing a message from person to person. In liquids, particles can move more freely, so energy transfer is slower. In gases like air, particles are far apart and move randomly, so sound transfers even more slowly. The closer together the particles, the faster sound travels.

From the table, you can see that sound travels about 4 times faster in water than in air, and about 18 times faster in aluminum than in air. This explains why whales can communicate over enormous distances underwater—sound doesn't lose energy as quickly in water, and it travels much faster.

Application: Underwater Communication

Whales and dolphins rely on sound to communicate, navigate, and find food underwater. Because sound travels so quickly and efficiently through water (1,437 m/s), these marine mammals can send and receive messages across vast ocean distances. A whale's call can travel hundreds of miles underwater. Scientists studying ocean acoustics must account for how sound speed changes with water temperature and depth when tracking whale populations.

How Temperature Affects Sound Speed

Temperature is another crucial factor affecting sound speed. For any given medium, higher temperature increases the speed of sound, while lower temperature decreases it.

Why? Remember that temperature measures how fast particles are moving. At higher temperatures, particles vibrate more vigorously. When sound waves pass through, faster-moving particles transfer the sound energy to their neighbors more quickly. Conversely, at lower temperatures, particles move sluggishly, and energy transfer becomes slower.

Temperature (°C) Speed of Sound in Air (m/s)
0°C (Freezing) 331
20°C (Room Temperature) 343
100°C (Boiling) 386

Look at the pattern: for each 1°C temperature increase, sound speed in dry air increases by approximately 0.6 m/s. This relationship is quite regular and predictable. So if you know the temperature, you can calculate the sound speed.

Sound Speed in Air (approximate):

$$v = 331 + 0.6T$$

where $v$ = speed of sound (m/s) and $T$ = temperature (°C)

Important Misconception Alert!

Students often think that sound speed depends on how "loud" a sound is. Not true! A whisper and a shout travel at the same speed. What differs is the amplitude (loudness) of the sound waves, not their speed. A sound's speed depends only on the medium and temperature, not on whether the sound is soft or loud.

Example Problems

Example 1: Finding Sound Speed in Different Media

Given:

  • Sound travels through water
  • Medium: water at 20°C

Find: How many times faster does sound travel in water than in air?

Solution:

Step 1: Identify the speed of sound in each medium from the table.

  • Speed of sound in water = 1,437 m/s
  • Speed of sound in air = 343 m/s

Step 2: Divide the water speed by the air speed to find how many times faster.

$$\text{Factor} = \frac{1,437 \text{ m/s}}{343 \text{ m/s}} = 4.19$$

Answer: Sound travels approximately 4.2 times faster in water than in air.

Example 2: Calculating Distance from Sound Travel Time

Given:

  • You see lightning flash
  • Thunder arrives 4 seconds later
  • Temperature is 20°C (use 343 m/s for sound speed)

Find: How far away is the lightning strike?

Solution:

Step 1: Use the relationship between speed, distance, and time.

$$\text{speed} = \frac{\text{distance}}{\text{time}}$$

Rearranging: $$\text{distance} = \text{speed} \times \text{time}$$

Step 2: Plug in the values.

$$d = 343 \text{ m/s} \times 4 \text{ s} = 1,372 \text{ m}$$

Step 3: Convert to kilometers for easier interpretation.

$$d = 1,372 \text{ m} = 1.37 \text{ km}$$

Answer: The lightning strike is approximately 1,372 meters (or 1.37 km) away.

Example 3: Multi-Step Problem - Sound Speed at Different Temperatures

Given:

  • A train whistle sound is measured on a cold winter morning at 0°C
  • The same measurement is taken on a hot summer day at 30°C
  • The distance from the whistle to the observer is 500 meters

Find:

  1. The speed of sound at 0°C
  2. The speed of sound at 30°C
  3. The difference in travel time between the two temperatures

Solution:

Part (a): Speed of sound at 0°C

Using the formula: $v = 331 + 0.6T$

$$v = 331 + 0.6(0) = 331 \text{ m/s}$$

Part (b): Speed of sound at 30°C

$$v = 331 + 0.6(30) = 331 + 18 = 349 \text{ m/s}$$

Part (c): Calculate travel time at each temperature

Using: $\text{time} = \frac{\text{distance}}{\text{speed}}$

At 0°C:

$$t_1 = \frac{500 \text{ m}}{331 \text{ m/s}} = 1.51 \text{ s}$$

At 30°C:

$$t_2 = \frac{500 \text{ m}}{349 \text{ m/s}} = 1.43 \text{ s}$$

Difference in travel time:

$$\Delta t = 1.51 - 1.43 = 0.08 \text{ s}$$

Answer:

  • (a) Speed at 0°C = 331 m/s
  • (b) Speed at 30°C = 349 m/s
  • (c) The sound arrives 0.08 seconds faster on the hot day (about 80 milliseconds)

Summary

  • The speed of sound is the distance that sound waves travel per unit time, typically measured in meters per second (m/s)
  • The speed of sound in dry air at 20°C is 343 m/s, which is standard reference value in physics
  • Sound requires a medium to travel; it cannot travel through a vacuum
  • Sound travels fastest in solids, slower in liquids, and slowest in gases because particle spacing and density affect energy transfer
  • In water, sound travels about 4 times faster than in air; in solids like aluminum, it travels about 18 times faster
  • Temperature dramatically affects sound speed: for each 1°C increase, sound speed in air increases by approximately 0.6 m/s
  • The approximate formula for sound speed in air is: $v = 331 + 0.6T$ (where $T$ is temperature in °C)
  • Practical applications include calculating storm distance (thunder delay), underwater communication, and designing acoustic spaces
  • The loudness (amplitude) of sound does not affect its speed—whispers and shouts travel at the same speed
  • Understanding sound speed requires considering both the medium and the temperature conditions

Answer Key

For Teacher Reference Only

Question 1 - Conceptual Answer

Correct Answer: In a steel bar, atoms are packed much more tightly together than air molecules. When sound vibrates through the steel, each atom is very close to its neighbors, so the vibrational energy transfers almost immediately from one atom to the next. In air, molecules are far apart and moving randomly, so it takes much longer for the energy of vibrations to reach neighboring molecules. The closer particles are together, the faster energy can transfer, so sound travels much faster in steel.

Question 2 - Conceptual Answer

Correct Answer: The speed of sound would increase. When temperature increases, air molecules move faster and vibrate more vigorously. This means they can transfer the energy of sound vibrations to neighboring molecules more quickly. The formula $v = 331 + 0.6T$ shows this positive relationship: higher temperature means higher speed. At 40°C, the speed would be $v = 331 + 0.6(40) = 331 + 24 = 355$ m/s, compared to 331 m/s at 0°C.

Question 3 - Application Answer

Correct Answer: The delay occurs because light travels much faster than sound. Light reaches your eyes almost instantaneously (light speed ≈ 300,000 km/s), while sound travels much slower (≈ 343 m/s in air). This means sound takes much longer to cover the same distance. This demonstrates that light is approximately 875,000 times faster than sound in air, which is why we always see events before we hear them when they occur at a distance.

Question 4 - Vocabulary Answer

Correct Answer: A medium is any substance or material through which sound waves travel. Examples include: air (gas), water (liquid), and steel (solid). Sound cannot travel through a vacuum because there are no particles present to vibrate and transfer the vibrational energy. Since sound is a mechanical wave that requires matter to transmit its energy, an empty space (vacuum) provides no medium through which vibrations can propagate.

Question 5 - Calculation Solution

Given: $t = 2.5$ s, $v = 343$ m/s (at 20°C)

Find: $d = ?$

Solution:

$$d = v \times t = 343 \text{ m/s} \times 2.5 \text{ s} = 857.5 \text{ m}$$

Answer: The sound has traveled 857.5 meters (or about 858 m).

Question 6 - Calculation Solution

Given: $d = 5,000$ m, $v = 1,437$ m/s (water at 20°C)

Find: $t = ?$

Solution:

$$t = \frac{d}{v} = \frac{5,000 \text{ m}}{1,437 \text{ m/s}} = 3.48 \text{ s}$$

Answer: The whale's call takes approximately 3.48 seconds to reach the diver.

Question 7 - Multi-Step Solution

Given: $d = 30$ m, $T = 25°C$ (and later 0°C for comparison)

Part (a): Speed at 25°C

$$v = 331 + 0.6T = 331 + 0.6(25) = 331 + 15 = 346 \text{ m/s}$$

Part (b): Time at 25°C

$$t = \frac{d}{v} = \frac{30 \text{ m}}{346 \text{ m/s}} = 0.0867 \text{ s}$$

Part (c): Speed at 0°C and comparison

At 0°C: $v = 331 + 0.6(0) = 331$ m/s

$$t = \frac{30 \text{ m}}{331 \text{ m/s}} = 0.0906 \text{ s}$$

Difference: $0.0906 - 0.0867 = 0.0039$ s (about 4 milliseconds faster at 25°C)

Answer:

  • (a) Speed at 25°C = 346 m/s
  • (b) Time at 25°C = 0.087 seconds (or about 87 milliseconds)
  • (c) At 0°C the speed is 331 m/s and time is 0.091 s; the sound reaches the observer about 4 milliseconds slower at 0°C

Question 8 - Multi-Step Solution

Given: Observer A: $d_A = 2$ km $= 2,000$ m (air, 20°C); Observer B: $d_B = 3$ km $= 3,000$ m (water, 20°C)

Part (a): Time for Observer A (air)

Speed in air at 20°C = 343 m/s

$$t_A = \frac{d_A}{v_A} = \frac{2,000 \text{ m}}{343 \text{ m/s}} = 5.83 \text{ s}$$

Part (b): Time for Observer B (water)

Speed in water at 20°C = 1,437 m/s

$$t_B = \frac{d_B}{v_B} = \frac{3,000 \text{ m}}{1,437 \text{ m/s}} = 2.09 \text{ s}$$

Part (c): Which observer hears first?

$t_B < t_A$ (2.09 s < 5.83 s)

Difference: $5.83 - 2.09 = 3.74$ s

Answer:

  • (a) Time for Observer A (air) = 5.83 seconds
  • (b) Time for Observer B (water) = 2.09 seconds
  • (c) Observer B hears the sound first by 3.74 seconds. Even though Observer B is farther away (3 km vs. 2 km), the much faster speed of sound in water (1,437 m/s vs. 343 m/s) means the sound reaches them much sooner!
