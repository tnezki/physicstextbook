SECTION 5.1

Simple Harmonic Motion

Simple harmonic motion describes the rhythmic back-and-forth movement found throughout nature and technology. From the strings of musical instruments to the suspension systems in cars, from the vibrations of atoms to the motion of planets, understanding how objects oscillate is essential to physics. This section explores the fundamental principles that govern any object undergoing simple harmonic motion.

Learning Objectives

  • I can define and identify simple harmonic motion in real-world contexts and explain what distinguishes it from other types of oscillatory motion.
  • I can describe the relationship between restoring force, displacement, and the spring constant, and apply Hooke's Law to predict how springs respond to forces.
  • I can calculate fundamental properties of oscillating systems including period, frequency, and amplitude, and explain how these quantities relate to each other.

Introduction

Hook: The Rhythm of the Universe

Close your eyes and listen to your heartbeat. Lub-dub, lub-dub, lub-dub—a rhythm so consistent that it defines life itself. Now think about a guitar string being plucked, the pendulum in an old grandfather clock, or the water in a swimming pool after someone dives in. Each of these phenomena—despite appearing completely different—shares something fundamental: they all move back and forth in a predictable, repeating pattern. This type of motion is called simple harmonic motion, and it's one of the most important concepts in all of physics because it appears everywhere.

Real-World Relevance

Understanding simple harmonic motion isn't just an academic exercise—it directly impacts your daily life. When you drive a car over a bump, springs and shock absorbers suppress the resulting oscillations so you don't bounce uncomfortably. The suspension system in a car uses principles of SHM to keep you safe. Engineers design buildings that can withstand earthquakes by understanding how structures naturally oscillate. The vibrations in a speaker that produce sound waves, the oscillations in an electrical circuit that generate radio waves, and even the quantum mechanical behavior of atoms in molecules all involve simple harmonic motion. Medical ultrasound machines use oscillating transducers, and every electronic device with a clock relies on precise oscillations to keep time.

Historical Context

The mathematical description of oscillatory motion developed over centuries. In 1602, the Italian scientist Galileo Galilei observed a swinging chandelier in a cathedral and noticed that regardless of how far it swung, the time between swings remained nearly constant. This insight, that the period of a pendulum is independent of its amplitude (for small angles), helped establish the foundation for understanding SHM. Later, in the 1600s, Robert Hooke discovered the law that bears his name: the force exerted by a spring is proportional to its displacement. Hooke's Law ($F = -kx$) became the mathematical basis for analyzing any system with a restoring force. In the 18th and 19th centuries, scientists like Isaac Newton and Leonhard Euler developed the calculus needed to fully describe SHM and realized its universal importance. Today, SHM is recognized as one of the most fundamental patterns in nature.

Connection to Prior Knowledge

You've already studied forces and Newton's Laws, learning that forces cause acceleration and that objects resist changes in motion through inertia. In simple harmonic motion, these principles combine in a special way: the farther an object moves from its resting position, the stronger the force pushing it back. This creates a self-sustaining cycle of motion. You've also learned about energy transformations. In SHM, energy constantly transforms between potential and kinetic forms as the object oscillates, yet the total energy remains constant in an ideal frictionless system. The mathematical relationships you'll learn here follow directly from kinematics and dynamics.

Understanding Simple Harmonic Motion

What is Simple Harmonic Motion?

Simple harmonic motion (SHM) is the repeating back-and-forth motion of an object in which a restoring force is always directed toward an equilibrium position and is proportional to the object's displacement from that position. This is a very specific type of oscillatory motion. Not all back-and-forth motion is SHM; the motion must follow the mathematical relationship where force is directly proportional to displacement and always opposes the displacement.

Real-World Examples of SHM:

Mass on a Spring: A weight hanging from a vertical spring oscillates up and down. The farther it stretches downward, the greater the upward force from the spring.

Pendulum (small angles): A pendulum swinging back and forth undergoes SHM for small angle oscillations. The farther it swings from vertical, the greater the restoring force pulling it back.

Vibrating Guitar String: When plucked, a guitar string oscillates perpendicular to its length, executing SHM at each point along its length.

Atom in a Molecule: Atoms bonded together vibrate back and forth around their equilibrium positions, following SHM (in most cases).

The Equilibrium Position and Restoring Force

The key to understanding SHM is the concept of an equilibrium position. This is the position where the net force on an object is zero and the object would remain at rest if not disturbed. When the object is displaced from equilibrium—pulled away or pushed away—the restoring force acts to return it to equilibrium.

The crucial insight: the restoring force is proportional to the displacement. This relationship is expressed mathematically as Hooke's Law:

$$F = -kx$$

In this equation:

  • $F$ is the restoring force (in Newtons, N)
  • $k$ is the spring constant (in N/m), which measures how stiff the spring is
  • $x$ is the displacement from equilibrium (in meters, m)
  • The negative sign indicates the force opposes the displacement (points toward equilibrium)

A larger spring constant $k$ means a stiffer spring that requires more force to stretch or compress it. A stiff spring will oscillate faster than a soft spring. Similarly, a larger displacement $x$ means the restoring force is larger. This is what makes the motion "simple"—the force depends only on position, not on velocity or time.

Important Distinction:

The negative sign in Hooke's Law ($F = -kx$) is crucial. It indicates that the force is a restoring force that always points opposite to the displacement. If you displace the mass to the right (positive $x$), the force points to the left (negative $F$). The negative sign is not a subtraction operation—it's a direction indicator.

Characteristics of Simple Harmonic Motion

Amplitude

The amplitude (symbol: $A$) is the maximum displacement of an oscillating object from its equilibrium position. It's measured in meters (m). If a mass on a spring oscillates back and forth, swinging 0.10 m to the left of equilibrium and 0.10 m to the right of equilibrium, the amplitude is 0.10 m. The amplitude depends on how much energy you put into the system initially. A mass released from farther away will have a larger amplitude than one released from nearby.

Key insight: Amplitude is independent of the spring constant and independent of the mass. You can have a large-amplitude oscillation on a stiff spring or a small-amplitude oscillation on a soft spring. The amplitude tells you the size of the oscillation, not its speed.

Period and Frequency

The period (symbol: $T$) is the time required for one complete oscillation—from some starting position, all the way to one extreme, back through equilibrium, to the other extreme, and finally back to the starting position. Period is measured in seconds (s).

The frequency (symbol: $f$) is the number of complete oscillations that occur in one second. It's measured in Hertz (Hz), which means oscillations per second. Frequency and period are reciprocals:

$$f = \frac{1}{T} \quad \text{and} \quad T = \frac{1}{f}$$

If an object completes 5 oscillations in 1 second, its frequency is 5 Hz and its period is 0.2 seconds. For a mass on a spring, the period depends on two factors: the mass and the spring constant. A heavier mass oscillates more slowly (larger period), while a stiffer spring makes the mass oscillate faster (smaller period). This relationship is:

$$T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{m}{k}}$$

This is one of the most important equations in SHM. Notice that period increases with larger mass (harder to accelerate) and decreases with larger spring constant (stronger restoring force).

Common Misconception:

Wrong: "A heavier mass always oscillates slower because it moves slower."

Correct: A heavier mass oscillates slower because its greater inertia resists acceleration from the restoring force. However, a heavier mass also experiences less acceleration for a given force. These effects combine so that period increases with mass, as shown in the equation $T = 2\pi\sqrt{m/k}$.

Velocity and Acceleration in SHM

A crucial feature of SHM is that velocity and acceleration vary as the object moves. Unlike uniform circular motion where speed is constant, in SHM the speed changes continuously.

  • At the equilibrium position: The displacement is zero, so the restoring force is zero. However, the object is moving at maximum speed. The object has maximum kinetic energy here.
  • At maximum displacement (turning points): The displacement is maximum ($\pm A$), so the restoring force is maximum. The object momentarily stops (velocity = 0) before reversing direction. The object has maximum potential energy here.
  • At intermediate positions: Both velocity and acceleration are at intermediate values.

Energy in Simple Harmonic Motion

In SHM, energy constantly transforms between kinetic energy (energy of motion) and potential energy (energy of position). The total mechanical energy remains constant in a frictionless system.

At equilibrium: Potential energy is minimum (zero if we set equilibrium as the reference point), and kinetic energy is maximum because the object is moving fastest.

At maximum displacement: Potential energy is maximum ($E_p = \frac{1}{2}kA^2$), and kinetic energy is zero because the object momentarily stops.

At intermediate positions: Energy is shared between kinetic and potential forms, but the total remains:

$$E_{total} = \frac{1}{2}kA^2$$

This equation shows that total energy depends only on the spring constant and amplitude, not on the mass or the details of the oscillation. A system with a larger amplitude stores more total energy.

Example Problems

Example 1: Applying Hooke's Law

Given: A spring with spring constant $k = 200$ N/m is compressed 0.15 m from equilibrium by a mass.

Find: What is the magnitude of the restoring force?

Solution:

Step 1: Identify which equation applies.

We're given the spring constant and displacement, and asked to find force. This is a direct application of Hooke's Law: $F = -kx$

Step 2: Substitute the given values.

$$|F| = k|x| = (200 \text{ N/m})(0.15 \text{ m})$$

Step 3: Calculate the result.

$$|F| = 30 \text{ N}$$

Answer: The magnitude of the restoring force is 30 N. The direction of this force is toward the equilibrium position (if compressed, it pushes outward; if stretched, it pulls inward).

Physical Interpretation: For every 0.01 m (1 cm) of compression or stretch, this spring exerts 2 N of restoring force. The spring constant of 200 N/m tells us this is a moderately stiff spring—not as stiff as a car suspension spring (which might be 20,000 N/m) but stiffer than a toy spring.

Example 2: Calculating Period from Mass and Spring Constant

Given: A 0.50 kg mass is attached to a spring with spring constant $k = 800$ N/m. The mass is displaced and released to undergo simple harmonic motion.

Find: (a) What is the period of oscillation? (b) What is the frequency?

Solution:

Part (a): Find the period

Step 1: Identify the equation relating period, mass, and spring constant.

$$T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{m}{k}}$$

Step 2: Substitute values with proper units.

$$T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{0.50 \text{ kg}}{800 \text{ N/m}}}$$

Step 3: Simplify inside the square root.

$$T = 2\pi\sqrt{0.000625 \text{ s}^2} = 2\pi(0.02500 \text{ s})$$

Step 4: Calculate the final result.

$$T = 2(3.14159)(0.02500 \text{ s}) = 0.157 \text{ s}$$

Part (b): Find the frequency

Step 1: Use the reciprocal relationship.

$$f = \frac{1}{T} = \frac{1}{0.157 \text{ s}} = 6.37 \text{ Hz}$$

Answer: (a) Period = 0.157 s ≈ 0.16 s

(b) Frequency = 6.37 Hz ≈ 6.4 Hz

Physical Interpretation: This mass completes about 6.4 complete oscillations every second, or one oscillation every 0.16 seconds. This is relatively fast oscillation—faster than the pendulum on a grandfather clock (which might oscillate once per second) but slower than a vibrating guitar string (which oscillates hundreds of times per second). This rate would be audible to human ears as a low tone.

Example 3: Multi-Step Problem—Analyzing a Complete Oscillating System

Given: A 2.0 kg mass attached to a spring is pulled 0.30 m from equilibrium and released. The spring constant is $k = 450$ N/m. Assume frictionless motion.

Find: (a) What is the period of oscillation? (b) What is the maximum restoring force? (c) What is the total mechanical energy? (d) What is the maximum velocity of the mass?

Solution:

Part (a): Period of oscillation

Step 1: Apply the period equation.

$$T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{m}{k}} = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{2.0 \text{ kg}}{450 \text{ N/m}}}$$

Step 2: Calculate.

$$T = 2\pi\sqrt{0.00444 \text{ s}^2} = 2\pi(0.0667 \text{ s}) = 0.419 \text{ s}$$

Part (b): Maximum restoring force

Step 1: The maximum force occurs at maximum displacement ($x = A = 0.30$ m).

$$|F_{max}| = k \cdot A = (450 \text{ N/m})(0.30 \text{ m})$$

Step 2: Calculate.

$$|F_{max}| = 135 \text{ N}$$

Part (c): Total mechanical energy

Step 1: Total energy in SHM is stored as potential energy when displacement is maximum.

$$E_{total} = \frac{1}{2}kA^2 = \frac{1}{2}(450 \text{ N/m})(0.30 \text{ m})^2$$

Step 2: Calculate.

$$E_{total} = \frac{1}{2}(450)(0.09) = 20.25 \text{ J} \approx 20 \text{ J}$$

Part (d): Maximum velocity

Step 1: At equilibrium, all energy is kinetic. Set kinetic energy equal to total energy.

$$\frac{1}{2}mv_{max}^2 = E_{total}$$

Step 2: Solve for maximum velocity.

$$v_{max} = \sqrt{\frac{2E_{total}}{m}} = \sqrt{\frac{2(20.25 \text{ J})}{2.0 \text{ kg}}}$$

Step 3: Calculate.

$$v_{max} = \sqrt{\frac{40.5}{2.0}} = \sqrt{20.25} = 4.5 \text{ m/s}$$

Answer:

(a) Period = 0.42 s

(b) Maximum force = 135 N

(c) Total energy = 20 J

(d) Maximum velocity = 4.5 m/s

Verification and Physical Insight: Notice that we could also calculate maximum velocity as $v_{max} = A\omega$ where $\omega = 2\pi/T = 2\pi/0.419 = 15 \text{ rad/s}$, giving $v_{max} = (0.30)(15) = 4.5$ m/s. ✓ This confirms our answer. The mass spends most time moving slowly near the turning points and moves fastest when passing through equilibrium at 4.5 m/s (about 10 mph).

Summary

  • Simple harmonic motion is a repeating back-and-forth oscillation where a restoring force is proportional to displacement and always directed toward equilibrium.
  • Hooke's Law ($F = -kx$) describes the restoring force, where the spring constant $k$ determines how stiff the spring is and the negative sign indicates the force opposes displacement.
  • Amplitude is the maximum displacement from equilibrium and depends on how much energy is put into the system initially.
  • Period ($T$) and frequency ($f$) are reciprocals: $f = 1/T$. Period increases with mass and decreases with spring stiffness.
  • The period of a mass-spring system is $T = 2\pi\sqrt{m/k}$, showing that heavier masses oscillate more slowly and stiffer springs oscillate faster.
  • In SHM, velocity and acceleration vary continuously. Maximum velocity occurs at equilibrium, and maximum acceleration occurs at maximum displacement.
  • Energy in SHM oscillates between kinetic and potential forms, but total mechanical energy remains constant.
  • SHM appears throughout nature and technology: springs, pendulums, vibrating strings, atoms in molecules, and countless other systems all exhibit SHM.

Answer Key for Instructors

Conceptual Questions

Question 1: Acceleration and Velocity at Equilibrium

Answer: At the equilibrium position, the displacement is zero. Since the restoring force is $F = -kx$, when $x = 0$, the force is also zero. With zero net force, the acceleration must be zero (by Newton's Second Law: $F = ma$). However, the velocity is maximum at equilibrium because the mass has been accelerated toward equilibrium from the turning points and hasn't yet begun decelerating again. The velocity is maximum while acceleration is zero—this seems paradoxical until we remember that acceleration is the rate of change of velocity. At equilibrium, velocity is at a turning point in its own behavior (changing from increasing to decreasing), so the rate of change of velocity is zero.

Question 2: Comparing Spring Constants

Answer: Spring B ($k = 400$ N/m) will oscillate faster, meaning it has a smaller period. Using $T = 2\pi\sqrt{m/k}$, when spring constant increases, the quantity under the square root decreases, so period decreases. Spring B is stiffer, providing a larger restoring force that accelerates the mass more quickly, resulting in faster oscillations. For example, if $m = 1$ kg: Spring A gives $T = 2\pi\sqrt{1/100} = 0.628$ s, while Spring B gives $T = 2\pi\sqrt{1/400} = 0.314$ s. Spring B's period is half that of Spring A.

Question 3: Pendulum Velocity and Acceleration

Answer: In a pendulum swing: Velocity is maximum at the bottom of the swing (passing through equilibrium position). At this point, the displacement is zero, the restoring force is zero, and the acceleration is zero. Acceleration is maximum at the extreme positions (farthest left and farthest right), where the displacement is maximum, the restoring force is maximum, and the velocity is zero. As the pendulum swings down from one extreme, it accelerates, reaching maximum velocity at the bottom. Then it decelerates as it swings up to the opposite extreme.

Question 4: Series Springs Configuration

Answer: Configuration B (two springs in series) will have the longer period. When springs are connected in series, the effective spring constant decreases (the combination is "softer" than either individual spring). Since $T = 2\pi\sqrt{m/k}$, a smaller effective spring constant results in a larger period. The physics: when forces are distributed between two springs in series, each spring stretches more than it would alone, making the system more flexible. In contrast, if springs were in parallel, the effective spring constant would increase, resulting in a shorter period.

Calculation Problems

Question 5: Spring Extension

Given: $k = 250$ N/m, $F = 5.0$ N

Solution:

Using Hooke's Law: $F = kx$

$$x = \frac{F}{k} = \frac{5.0 \text{ N}}{250 \text{ N/m}} = \boxed{0.020 \text{ m} = 2.0 \text{ cm}}$$

Question 6: Period and Frequency

Given: $m = 0.80$ kg, $k = 320$ N/m

Solution:

(a) Period:

$$T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{m}{k}} = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{0.80}{320}} = 2\pi\sqrt{0.0025}$$

$$T = 2\pi(0.050) = \boxed{0.31 \text{ s}}$$

(b) Frequency:

$$f = \frac{1}{T} = \frac{1}{0.31} = \boxed{3.2 \text{ Hz}}$$

Multi-Step Problems

Question 7: Complete Spring-Mass Analysis

Given: $k = 600$ N/m, $m = 1.5$ kg, $A = 0.20$ m

Solution:

(a) Maximum restoring force (occurs at maximum displacement):

$$F_{max} = kA = (600)(0.20) = \boxed{120 \text{ N}}$$

(b) Period of oscillation:

$$T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{m}{k}} = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{1.5}{600}} = 2\pi\sqrt{0.0025}$$

$$T = 2\pi(0.050) = \boxed{0.314 \text{ s} \approx 0.31 \text{ s}}$$

(c) Total mechanical energy:

$$E_{total} = \frac{1}{2}kA^2 = \frac{1}{2}(600)(0.20)^2 = \frac{1}{2}(600)(0.04)$$

$$E_{total} = \boxed{12 \text{ J}}$$

(d) Maximum velocity:

$$v_{max} = A\omega = A \cdot \frac{2\pi}{T} = 0.20 \times \frac{2\pi}{0.314}$$

$$v_{max} = 0.20 \times 20 = \boxed{4.0 \text{ m/s}}$$

Alternative method: $\frac{1}{2}mv_{max}^2 = E_{total}$ gives $v_{max} = \sqrt{\frac{2(12)}{1.5}} = \sqrt{16} = 4.0$ m/s ✓

Question 8: Complete Analysis with Energy Conservation

Given: $m = 2.0$ kg, $k = 800$ N/m, $A = 0.25$ m

Solution:

(a) Period and frequency:

$$T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{m}{k}} = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{2.0}{800}} = 2\pi\sqrt{0.0025} = 2\pi(0.050)$$

$$T = \boxed{0.314 \text{ s} \approx 0.31 \text{ s}}$$

$$f = \frac{1}{T} = \frac{1}{0.314} = \boxed{3.2 \text{ Hz}}$$

(b) Maximum force and acceleration:

$$F_{max} = kA = (800)(0.25) = \boxed{200 \text{ N}}$$

$$a_{max} = \frac{F_{max}}{m} = \frac{200}{2.0} = \boxed{100 \text{ m/s}^2}$$

(c) Displacement where velocity equals half maximum velocity:

First, find maximum velocity:

$$v_{max} = A\omega = 0.25 \times \frac{2\pi}{0.314} = 0.25 \times 20 = 5.0 \text{ m/s}$$

Half of maximum: $v = 2.5$ m/s

Using energy conservation: $\frac{1}{2}kA^2 = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 + \frac{1}{2}kx^2$

$$\frac{1}{2}(800)(0.25)^2 = \frac{1}{2}(2.0)(2.5)^2 + \frac{1}{2}(800)x^2$$

$$25 = 6.25 + 400x^2$$

$$18.75 = 400x^2$$

$$x^2 = 0.04688$$

$$x = \boxed{0.217 \text{ m} \approx 0.22 \text{ m}}$$
