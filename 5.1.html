SECTION 5.1

Introduction to Simple Harmonic Motion

Simple harmonic motion is the back-and-forth oscillation of an object around an equilibrium position. From the swing of a pendulum to the vibration of a guitar string, this repeating motion appears throughout nature and technology. Understanding harmonic motion is the foundation for studying waves, sound, and many mechanical systems that shape our world.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this section, you will be able to:

  • I can identify and describe examples of simple harmonic motion and explain what causes objects to oscillate.
  • I can create and use models (diagrams, graphs) to represent the motion of objects undergoing simple harmonic motion, including mass-spring systems and pendulums.

Introduction

Have you ever watched a playground swing move back and forth, or noticed how a tree branch bounces up and down after a bird flies away? These are examples of oscillation—repeating motion around a central position. This type of motion is so common in nature and technology that understanding it unlocks explanations for everything from earthquake protection systems to the timekeeping in watches.

Simple harmonic motion is special because it follows predictable mathematical patterns. The motion repeats itself over and over in exactly the same way. A mass bouncing on a spring, a pendulum swinging back and forth, and even the vibrations of atoms in a solid all exhibit simple harmonic motion. These systems share common characteristics that make them easier to analyze and understand.

Historical Context: Galileo and the Pendulum

In 1602, Italian scientist Galileo Galilei made a remarkable discovery while watching a chandelier swing in the cathedral at Pisa. He noticed that regardless of how far the chandelier swung, it took the same amount of time to complete each swing. This observation led him to study pendulums systematically, using his own pulse as a timer. Galileo's work on pendulums laid the foundation for accurate timekeeping and contributed to our understanding of motion and gravity. His discovery that the period of a pendulum depends only on its length revolutionized clock design.

Understanding simple harmonic motion connects to concepts you have already learned. You know that forces cause acceleration, and acceleration changes an object's velocity. In harmonic motion, we'll see how a special type of force—called a restoring force—creates the repeating pattern of oscillation. This builds directly on Newton's laws of motion that govern all moving objects.

What is Simple Harmonic Motion?

Simple harmonic motion (SHM) is the repetitive back-and-forth movement of an object through an equilibrium position, where the restoring force is directly proportional to the displacement from equilibrium. This means that the farther you pull or push the object away from its resting position, the stronger the force that tries to pull it back.

Key Vocabulary

Equilibrium position: The central resting position where the net force on an object is zero and the object would remain at rest if not disturbed.

Displacement: The distance and direction of an object from its equilibrium position, measured in meters (m).

Restoring force: The force that acts to bring an oscillating object back toward its equilibrium position; it always points toward equilibrium.

Think about a mass attached to a spring hanging vertically. When you pull the mass down and release it, the spring pulls it back up. When the mass moves past equilibrium and goes too high, the spring pulls it back down. This push and pull creates continuous oscillation. The key feature is that the restoring force always points back toward equilibrium and gets stronger the farther away the object moves.

Characteristics of Simple Harmonic Motion

All systems exhibiting simple harmonic motion share several important characteristics. First, the motion is periodic, meaning it repeats in a regular cycle. Second, the restoring force follows Hooke's Law (for springs) or a similar proportional relationship. Third, if there is no friction or air resistance, the oscillation continues forever with constant amplitude.

More Key Terms

Amplitude (A): The maximum displacement from equilibrium position, measured in meters (m). This represents how far the object moves from center to its farthest point.

Period (T): The time required for one complete cycle of motion, measured in seconds (s). One complete cycle means going from a starting point, through all positions, and back to the starting point moving in the same direction.

Frequency (f): The number of complete cycles per second, measured in hertz (Hz) or cycles per second (1 Hz = 1 cycle/s).

The relationship between period and frequency is inverse. If something oscillates quickly (high frequency), each cycle takes less time (small period). If it oscillates slowly (low frequency), each cycle takes more time (large period). Mathematically, this relationship is expressed as:

$$f = \frac{1}{T} \quad \text{or} \quad T = \frac{1}{f}$$

For example, if a pendulum completes one full swing in 2 seconds, its period is $T = 2 \text{ s}$ and its frequency is $f = 1/2 = 0.5 \text{ Hz}$. This means it completes half an oscillation every second.

The Mass-Spring System

One of the most common examples of simple harmonic motion is a mass attached to a spring. When you stretch or compress a spring and release it, the spring exerts a restoring force described by Hooke's Law:

$$F = -kx$$

In this equation, $F$ is the restoring force (in newtons), $k$ is the spring constant (in N/m), and $x$ is the displacement from equilibrium (in meters). The negative sign indicates that the force always points in the opposite direction of the displacement—toward equilibrium.

Understanding Spring Constant

Spring constant (k): A measure of a spring's stiffness, measured in newtons per meter (N/m). A larger spring constant means a stiffer spring that requires more force to stretch or compress. A small spring constant means a softer, more easily stretched spring.

The period of oscillation for a mass-spring system depends on both the mass and the spring constant. Remarkably, it does not depend on the amplitude—whether you pull the mass a little or a lot, it takes the same time to complete one cycle. The formula for the period is:

$$T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{m}{k}}$$

where $m$ is the mass (in kg) and $k$ is the spring constant (in N/m). This formula tells us that heavier masses oscillate more slowly (longer period), while stiffer springs cause faster oscillations (shorter period). The factor $2\pi$ appears because the motion traces out a mathematical pattern related to circular motion.

Important: Mass vs. Spring Constant

From the period formula, we can see that increasing the mass increases the period (slower oscillation), while increasing the spring constant decreases the period (faster oscillation). A heavy mass on a soft spring oscillates very slowly, while a light mass on a stiff spring oscillates rapidly.

The Simple Pendulum

A simple pendulum consists of a mass (called a bob) suspended from a fixed point by a string or rod of negligible mass. When pulled to one side and released, gravity provides the restoring force that causes the pendulum to swing back and forth.

For small angles (less than about 15 degrees from vertical), the pendulum exhibits simple harmonic motion. The period of a simple pendulum depends only on its length and the acceleration due to gravity:

$$T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{L}{g}}$$

where $L$ is the length of the pendulum (in meters) and $g$ is the acceleration due to gravity ($9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$ on Earth). Notice that the period does not depend on the mass of the bob or the amplitude of the swing (for small angles). This means a heavy pendulum and a light pendulum of the same length swing with the same period.

Why Small Angles Matter

The simple pendulum formula $T = 2\pi\sqrt{L/g}$ is only accurate for small swings (less than 15° from vertical). For larger angles, the restoring force is no longer proportional to displacement, so the motion is not truly simple harmonic. However, for most practical applications like grandfather clocks, the small-angle approximation works very well.

This remarkable property—that the period depends only on length and not on mass—is what Galileo discovered centuries ago. It explains why pendulum clocks are so accurate: as long as the pendulum length stays constant, the clock keeps time reliably regardless of wear or slight changes in the bob's mass.

Energy in Simple Harmonic Motion

As an object undergoes simple harmonic motion, energy continuously transforms between kinetic energy and potential energy. At the equilibrium position, the object has maximum speed and therefore maximum kinetic energy. At the amplitude positions (maximum displacement), the object momentarily stops before changing direction, so it has zero kinetic energy but maximum potential energy.

For a mass-spring system, the potential energy is stored in the compressed or stretched spring. For a pendulum, the potential energy is gravitational—the bob is higher when displaced to the side than when at equilibrium. If we ignore friction and air resistance, the total mechanical energy remains constant throughout the oscillation.

Real-World Application: Earthquake Protection

Understanding simple harmonic motion is critical for designing buildings that can survive earthquakes. Engineers design structures with specific natural frequencies so they don't resonate with earthquake vibrations. Some modern skyscrapers use massive pendulums called tuned mass dampers at their tops. These pendulums oscillate in the opposite direction to building sway, reducing dangerous oscillations during earthquakes or strong winds. The Taipei 101 skyscraper in Taiwan contains a 730-ton steel pendulum that protects the building from typhoons and seismic activity.

Example Problems

Example 1: Calculating Frequency from Period

Problem: A mass on a spring completes one full oscillation in 0.40 seconds. What is the frequency of oscillation?

Given:

  • Period: $T = 0.40 \text{ s}$

Find: Frequency $f$

Solution:

Use the relationship between frequency and period:

$$f = \frac{1}{T}$$

Substitute the known value:

$$f = \frac{1}{0.40 \text{ s}} = 2.5 \text{ Hz}$$

Answer: The frequency is 2.5 Hz, meaning the mass completes 2.5 complete oscillations every second.

Example 2: Finding the Period of a Pendulum

Problem: A simple pendulum has a length of 2.0 m. What is its period of oscillation? (Use $g = 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$)

Given:

  • Length: $L = 2.0 \text{ m}$
  • Acceleration due to gravity: $g = 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$

Find: Period $T$

Solution:

Use the period formula for a simple pendulum:

$$T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{L}{g}}$$

Substitute the known values:

$$T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{2.0 \text{ m}}{9.8 \text{ m/s}^2}}$$ $$T = 2\pi\sqrt{0.204 \text{ s}^2}$$ $$T = 2\pi(0.452 \text{ s})$$ $$T = 2.84 \text{ s}$$

Answer: The period is 2.84 seconds. The pendulum takes 2.84 seconds to complete one full swing.

Example 3: Mass-Spring System Period Calculation

Problem: A 0.50 kg mass is attached to a spring with spring constant $k = 20 \text{ N/m}$. The mass is pulled 0.10 m from equilibrium and released. (a) What is the period of oscillation? (b) What is the frequency?

Given:

  • Mass: $m = 0.50 \text{ kg}$
  • Spring constant: $k = 20 \text{ N/m}$
  • Amplitude: $A = 0.10 \text{ m}$ (not needed for period)

Find: (a) Period $T$, (b) Frequency $f$

Solution:

Part (a): Use the period formula for a mass-spring system:

$$T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{m}{k}}$$

Substitute the known values:

$$T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{0.50 \text{ kg}}{20 \text{ N/m}}}$$ $$T = 2\pi\sqrt{0.025 \text{ s}^2}$$ $$T = 2\pi(0.158 \text{ s})$$ $$T = 0.99 \text{ s}$$

Part (b): Calculate frequency using the period:

$$f = \frac{1}{T} = \frac{1}{0.99 \text{ s}} = 1.01 \text{ Hz}$$

Answer: (a) The period is 0.99 seconds (approximately 1.0 second). (b) The frequency is 1.01 Hz, meaning the mass completes about one complete oscillation per second. Note that the amplitude does not affect the period or frequency.

Summary

  • Simple harmonic motion is repetitive oscillation through an equilibrium position with a restoring force proportional to displacement.
  • The equilibrium position is where the object would remain at rest; the restoring force always points toward equilibrium.
  • Amplitude is the maximum displacement from equilibrium; period is the time for one complete cycle; frequency is the number of cycles per second.
  • Frequency and period are inversely related: $f = 1/T$ and $T = 1/f$.
  • In a mass-spring system, the restoring force follows Hooke's Law: $F = -kx$, where $k$ is the spring constant.
  • The period of a mass-spring system is $T = 2\pi\sqrt{m/k}$; it depends on mass and spring stiffness but not amplitude.
  • A simple pendulum exhibits SHM for small angles, with period $T = 2\pi\sqrt{L/g}$ depending only on length and gravity.
  • The period of a pendulum does not depend on the mass of the bob or the amplitude (for small angles).
  • Energy in SHM continuously transforms between kinetic energy (at equilibrium) and potential energy (at maximum displacement).
  • Real-world applications include clock mechanisms, earthquake protection systems, and vibration isolation devices.

Answer Key

1. An object in simple harmonic motion oscillates because of a restoring force that always points toward the equilibrium position. When the object is displaced from equilibrium, this force pulls or pushes it back. As the object moves farther from equilibrium, the restoring force becomes stronger. When the object returns to equilibrium, it has gained speed and its momentum carries it past equilibrium to the other side, where the restoring force now acts in the opposite direction. This continuous push and pull creates the back-and-forth oscillation.

2. Yes, they will have the same period. According to the formula $T = 2\pi\sqrt{L/g}$, the period of a simple pendulum depends only on its length and the acceleration due to gravity. The mass of the bob does not appear in the formula, so pendulums of equal length but different masses will swing with identical periods. This is one of the remarkable properties that makes pendulums useful for timekeeping.

3. Amplitude is the maximum displacement from equilibrium—the farthest distance the object reaches from its resting position. Displacement, on the other hand, is the distance and direction from equilibrium at any given instant during the motion; it continuously changes as the object oscillates. No, changing the amplitude does not affect the period of a mass-spring system. The period formula $T = 2\pi\sqrt{m/k}$ does not include amplitude, so whether you pull the mass a little or a lot, it takes the same time to complete one cycle (this is called isochronism).

4. Both systems exhibit continuous energy transformation between kinetic and potential energy. In a mass-spring system, kinetic energy is maximum at equilibrium (maximum speed) and zero at maximum displacement. Potential energy is stored in the stretched or compressed spring and is maximum at the amplitude positions and zero at equilibrium. In a simple pendulum, kinetic energy is also maximum at equilibrium (lowest point) and zero at maximum displacement. However, the potential energy is gravitational—the bob is highest (maximum gravitational PE) at the amplitude positions and lowest (minimum gravitational PE) at equilibrium. In both cases, the total mechanical energy remains constant if friction is negligible.

7. (a) $T = 0.99 \text{ s}$, (b) $f = 1.01 \text{ Hz}$, (c) 5 complete oscillations

Part (a): Use $T = 2\pi\sqrt{m/k}$

$$T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{0.80 \text{ kg}}{32 \text{ N/m}}} = 2\pi\sqrt{0.025 \text{ s}^2} = 2\pi(0.158 \text{ s}) = 0.99 \text{ s}$$

Part (b): Use $f = 1/T$

$$f = \frac{1}{0.99 \text{ s}} = 1.01 \text{ Hz}$$

Part (c): Number of oscillations = time ÷ period

$$\text{Number} = \frac{5.0 \text{ s}}{0.99 \text{ s}} = 5.05 \approx 5 \text{ complete oscillations}$$

8. (a) $L = 0.99 \text{ m}$, (b) The clock will run slow; new period = $T = 4.95 \text{ s}$

Part (a): Rearrange $T = 2\pi\sqrt{L/g}$ to solve for $L$:

$$T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{L}{g}}$$ $$\frac{T}{2\pi} = \sqrt{\frac{L}{g}}$$ $$\left(\frac{T}{2\pi}\right)^2 = \frac{L}{g}$$ $$L = g\left(\frac{T}{2\pi}\right)^2 = 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2 \times \left(\frac{2.0 \text{ s}}{6.28}\right)^2 = 9.8 \times (0.318)^2 = 9.8 \times 0.101 = 0.99 \text{ m}$$

Part (b): On the Moon, with the same length but different gravity:

$$T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{0.99 \text{ m}}{1.6 \text{ m/s}^2}} = 2\pi\sqrt{0.619 \text{ s}^2} = 2\pi(0.787 \text{ s}) = 4.95 \text{ s}$$

The clock will run slow because the period is much longer on the Moon (4.95 s instead of 2.0 s). Each "tick" takes almost 5 seconds instead of 2 seconds, so the clock loses time compared to Earth time.
