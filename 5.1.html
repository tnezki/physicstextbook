Introduction: Oscillations Everywhere

Have you ever watched a guitar string vibrate after being plucked, bounced on a trampoline, or noticed how your car bounces when you hit a bump? All of these are examples of simple harmonic motion (SHM), one of the most common types of motion in the universe. From the atomic vibrations that generate heat to the oscillations of bridges and buildings during earthquakes, understanding SHM helps us design safer structures, create musical instruments, and even keep time with pendulum clocks.

The study of oscillatory motion dates back to ancient civilizations, but it was Galileo Galilei in the 1600s who first described the regular motion of pendulums. Later, Robert Hooke discovered the fundamental relationship between force and displacement in springs, now known as Hooke's Law. These discoveries laid the foundation for understanding everything from atomic physics to mechanical engineering.

What is Simple Harmonic Motion?

Simple harmonic motion (SHM) is the repetitive back-and-forth motion of an object over the same path. This motion occurs when an object is displaced from its equilibrium position and experiences a restoring force that is directly proportional to the displacement. The restoring force always points back toward equilibrium, which is why the object oscillates around that central position rather than flying away.

Common examples of simple harmonic motion include:

  • Mass on a spring
  • Pendulum swinging
  • Tuning fork vibrating
  • Guitar or piano strings
  • Atoms vibrating in solids
  • Car suspension systems
  • Swings on a playground
  • Balance wheel in watches

Key Vocabulary for SHM

Oscillation:
The repetitive back-and-forth motion of an object
Equilibrium position:
The middle position where no net force acts on the object
Displacement ($x$):
The distance the object is from its equilibrium position (in meters)
Restoring force ($F$):
The force that pulls or pushes the object back toward equilibrium (in newtons)
Spring constant ($k$):
A measure of the stiffness of a spring (units: N/m)
Amplitude ($A$):
The maximum displacement from equilibrium
Period ($T$):
The time required for one complete cycle (in seconds)
Frequency ($f$):
The number of complete cycles per second (in Hz or s$^{-1}$)

The Spring-Mass System

The simplest model for studying SHM is a mass attached to a horizontal spring on a frictionless surface. When the mass is at its equilibrium position, the spring exerts no force. If you pull the mass to stretch the spring or push it to compress the spring, the spring exerts a restoring force that tries to return the mass to equilibrium.

Equilibrium (F = 0) → Compressed (F ← ) → Stretched (F → )

Hooke's Law

The relationship between the restoring force and displacement is described by Hooke's Law, discovered by Robert Hooke in 1660. The magnitude of the restoring force is directly proportional to the displacement from equilibrium:

$$F = kx$$

where $F$ = restoring force (N), $k$ = spring constant (N/m), $x$ = displacement (m)

The spring constant ($k$) measures how stiff the spring is. A larger spring constant means a stiffer spring that requires more force to stretch or compress. The negative sign often included in the complete form ($F = -kx$) indicates that the force always opposes the displacement, pointing back toward equilibrium.

Key Concept: Hooke's Law tells us that the restoring force is proportional to displacement. A spring stretched twice as far experiences twice the restoring force. This linear relationship is fundamental to simple harmonic motion.

Motion Through One Complete Cycle

Let's follow a mass through one complete oscillation, starting when the spring is compressed:

1. Maximum Compression: Velocity = 0 (changing direction) | Restoring force = maximum (toward equilibrium) | Acceleration = maximum (toward equilibrium)

2. Moving Toward Equilibrium: Displacement decreases → Restoring force decreases → Acceleration decreases → Velocity increases

3. At Equilibrium: Displacement = 0 | Restoring force = 0 | Acceleration = 0 | Velocity = maximum

4. Moving Past Equilibrium: Mass continues due to inertia | Displacement increases (opposite direction) → Restoring force increases (opposite direction) → Acceleration increases (opposite direction) → Velocity decreases

5. Maximum Stretch: Velocity = 0 (changing direction) | Restoring force = maximum (toward equilibrium) | Acceleration = maximum (toward equilibrium)

6. Returns to Start: Process repeats in reverse, completing one full cycle

Important Pattern: When displacement is maximum (amplitude), velocity is ZERO and acceleration is maximum. When the mass is at equilibrium, displacement and acceleration are ZERO, but velocity is maximum. These quantities are always "out of phase" with each other.

Period and Frequency

One complete cycle of oscillation includes motion from the starting point, all the way to one side, all the way to the other side, and back to the start. The period ($T$) is the time for one complete cycle, while the frequency ($f$) is the number of cycles completed per second:

$$f = \frac{1}{T} \quad \text{and} \quad T = \frac{1}{f}$$

where $f$ = frequency (Hz or s$^{-1}$), $T$ = period (s)

A frequency of 1 Hz means the object completes one full oscillation per second. A period of 2 seconds means each complete oscillation takes 2 seconds. Note that a high frequency corresponds to a short period (oscillations happen quickly), while a low frequency corresponds to a long period (oscillations happen slowly).

Connection to Circular Motion

Simple harmonic motion is intimately related to uniform circular motion. If you observe an object moving in a circle from the side (edge-on view), its shadow appears to move back and forth in SHM. This relationship helps us understand why the mathematical descriptions of both types of motion are so similar. The projection of circular motion onto a line gives us simple harmonic motion.

Example Problems

Example 1: Finding the Spring Constant

Problem: When a 500 kg crate of cargo is placed in the bed of a pickup truck, the truck's springs compress 4.00 cm. What is the spring constant for the truck springs?

Given: $m = 500$ kg, $x = 4.00$ cm $= 0.0400$ m, $g = 9.81$ m/s$^2$ | Find: $k = ?$

Step 1: The weight of the crate provides the force compressing the spring.

$$F = mg = (500 \text{ kg})(9.81 \text{ m/s}^2) = 4905 \text{ N}$$

Step 2: Use Hooke's Law to find the spring constant: $F = kx$

$$k = \frac{F}{x} = \frac{4905 \text{ N}}{0.0400 \text{ m}} = 122,625 \text{ N/m} = 1.23 \times 10^5 \text{ N/m}$$

Answer: The spring constant is $1.23 \times 10^5$ N/m. This is a very stiff spring, as expected for truck suspension!

Example 2: Finding the Displacement

Problem: How far will the truck springs from Example 1 compress if 800 kg of cargo is placed in the truck bed?

Given: $m = 800$ kg, $k = 1.23 \times 10^5$ N/m, $g = 9.81$ m/s$^2$ | Find: $x = ?$

Step 1: Calculate the force from the heavier cargo.

$$F = mg = (800 \text{ kg})(9.81 \text{ m/s}^2) = 7848 \text{ N}$$

Step 2: Use Hooke's Law to find displacement: $F = kx$, so $x = F/k$

$$x = \frac{F}{k} = \frac{7848 \text{ N}}{1.23 \times 10^5 \text{ N/m}} = 0.0638 \text{ m} = 6.38 \text{ cm}$$

Answer: The springs compress 6.38 cm with the heavier load. Notice that more than 50% more cargo caused only 60% more compression—the strong spring resists large displacements.

Example 3: Multi-Step Problem

Problem: A 2.5 kg mass is attached to a spring with spring constant $k = 450$ N/m. The mass is pulled 15 cm from equilibrium and released. (a) What is the restoring force at the moment of release? (b) If the system oscillates with a frequency of 2.14 Hz, what is the period of oscillation?

Given: $m = 2.5$ kg, $k = 450$ N/m, $x = 15$ cm $= 0.15$ m, $f = 2.14$ Hz | Find: (a) $F = ?$ (b) $T = ?$

Part (a) - Step 1: At maximum displacement (the moment of release), use Hooke's Law.

$$F = kx = (450 \text{ N/m})(0.15 \text{ m}) = 67.5 \text{ N}$$

Part (a) - Answer: The restoring force is 67.5 N directed toward equilibrium. This force accelerates the mass from rest.

Part (b) - Step 2: Use the relationship between period and frequency.

$$T = \frac{1}{f} = \frac{1}{2.14 \text{ Hz}} = 0.467 \text{ s}$$

Part (b) - Answer: The period is 0.467 seconds. This means the mass completes one full oscillation (out and back) every 0.467 seconds, or about 2.14 times per second.

Application: Springs and oscillators are used in countless devices. Car shock absorbers use springs to cushion bumps. Digital watches use tuning forks (which oscillate) to keep precise time. MRI machines use oscillating magnetic fields. Understanding the period and frequency of oscillation is crucial for engineering all these systems.

Summary

  • Simple harmonic motion is repetitive back-and-forth motion that occurs in many natural and engineered systems.
  • The equilibrium position is where the net force is zero; displacement is measured from this position.
  • Hooke's Law ($F = kx$) describes the restoring force for springs, proportional to displacement.
  • The spring constant ($k$) measures spring stiffness with units of N/m. Stiffer springs have larger $k$ values.
  • During oscillation, when velocity is maximum, acceleration is zero (at equilibrium).
  • When displacement is maximum (amplitude), velocity is zero and acceleration is maximum.
  • Period ($T$) and frequency ($f$) are reciprocals: $f = 1/T$ and $T = 1/f$
  • SHM is mathematically related to uniform circular motion viewed from the side.
  • The restoring force always acts to return the mass toward equilibrium.
  • Without friction, an oscillating system will continue indefinitely with constant amplitude.

    Conceptual Questions (1-5)

    1. True. At the equilibrium position, the displacement is zero, so the restoring force ($F = kx$) is zero, which means acceleration is zero. However, the mass has maximum velocity because it has been accelerating from maximum displacement.

    2. True. At maximum displacement (amplitude), the restoring force is at its maximum value ($F = kA$), which produces maximum acceleration according to Newton's Second Law ($a = F/m$).

    3. The restoring force decreases as the mass moves toward equilibrium. Since the restoring force is proportional to displacement ($F = kx$), as displacement decreases, the force decreases proportionally. At equilibrium, both displacement and restoring force equal zero.

    4. The mass doesn't stop at equilibrium due to inertia. As the mass approaches equilibrium, it has been accelerating and has built up velocity. Even though the restoring force becomes zero at equilibrium, the mass still has momentum and continues moving past equilibrium until the restoring force on the opposite side slows it down to a stop.

    5. Calculate frequency: $f = 20 \text{ oscillations} / 30 \text{ s} = 0.67$ Hz. Calculate period: $T = 1/f = 1/0.67 = 1.5$ s. Frequency is in Hz (or s$^{-1}$), while period is in seconds (s).
