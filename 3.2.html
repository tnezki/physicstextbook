SECTION 3.2

Friction

Friction is the force that resists the sliding motion between two surfaces in contact. Whether you're pushing a box across the floor, braking your bicycle, or simply walking, friction plays a crucial role. Without friction, we couldn't walk, cars couldn't stop, and objects would slide endlessly.

Learning Objectives

I can...

  • Describe friction as a force that opposes motion between surfaces in contact.
  • Distinguish between static friction and kinetic friction and explain when each type acts.
  • Calculate friction forces using the coefficient of friction and the normal force ($f = \mu N$).
  • Analyze factors that affect friction, including surface texture and the normal force.

Introduction

Imagine trying to slide a heavy bookcase across a carpeted floor. You push and push, but it doesn't move—at least not at first. You need to push harder until suddenly the bookcase starts sliding. Once it's moving, you notice you don't need to push quite as hard to keep it moving. Why is that?

The answer is friction, a force that opposes the relative motion between two surfaces in contact. Friction is present everywhere in our daily lives. It allows us to walk without slipping, enables cars to accelerate and brake, lets us write with a pencil, and keeps pictures hanging on walls. While we often think of friction as an obstacle to overcome, it's actually essential for most activities we do.

Life Without Friction

Imagine a world without friction. You couldn't walk—your feet would slip with every step, like walking on perfectly smooth ice. Cars couldn't accelerate or brake. You couldn't hold anything because objects would slip through your fingers. Even sitting in a chair would be impossible as you'd slide right off. Friction, despite sometimes being inconvenient, is absolutely essential for everyday life.

The scientific study of friction dates back centuries, but it wasn't until the work of Guillaume Amontons in the late 1600s and Charles-Augustin de Coulomb in the 1700s that friction was properly understood and quantified. Their work established the fundamental laws of friction that we still use today.

What is Friction?

Friction is a contact force that opposes the sliding or attempted sliding of two surfaces past each other. It always acts parallel to the surfaces in contact and in the direction opposite to the motion (or attempted motion).

Friction: A force that opposes relative motion between two surfaces in contact. Friction acts parallel to the surfaces and opposite to the direction of motion or intended motion.

Friction arises from interactions between molecules and microscopic irregularities on the surfaces. Even surfaces that appear smooth to the eye have tiny bumps and valleys at the microscopic level. When two surfaces are pressed together, these microscopic bumps interlock, and molecular forces bind the surfaces together. When you try to slide one surface past another, these bonds must be broken, which requires force.

Types of Friction

There are two main types of friction that you'll encounter in physics problems:

Static Friction

Static friction is the friction force that acts on objects that are not moving relative to each other. It's the force that must be overcome to start an object moving. Static friction can vary in magnitude from zero up to a maximum value.

Static friction: The friction force between surfaces that are not sliding relative to each other. Static friction prevents motion from starting and adjusts its magnitude to match applied forces up to a maximum value.

When you push gently on a stationary box, static friction pushes back with equal force, keeping the box at rest. As you push harder, static friction increases to match your push—up to a limit. Once your applied force exceeds the maximum static friction, the box breaks free and starts moving.

The maximum static friction force is given by:

$$f_s \leq \mu_s N$$

where $\mu_s$ (pronounced "mew sub s") is the coefficient of static friction and $N$ is the normal force.

Kinetic Friction

Kinetic friction (also called sliding friction) is the friction force that acts on objects that are already sliding relative to each other. Once an object is moving, kinetic friction opposes its motion with a relatively constant force.

Kinetic friction: The friction force between surfaces that are sliding relative to each other. Kinetic friction has a constant magnitude for a given pair of surfaces.

Kinetic friction is calculated using:

$$f_k = \mu_k N$$

where $\mu_k$ (pronounced "mew sub k") is the coefficient of kinetic friction and $N$ is the normal force.

Key Relationship: For any pair of surfaces, the coefficient of static friction is always greater than the coefficient of kinetic friction: $\mu_s > \mu_k$. This is why it's harder to start something moving than to keep it moving once it's already in motion.

The Coefficient of Friction

The coefficient of friction ($\mu$) is a dimensionless number that represents how much friction exists between two surfaces. It depends on the materials and textures of both surfaces in contact.

Coefficient of friction ($\mu$): A dimensionless number representing the "stickiness" between two surfaces. Higher values mean more friction. It depends only on the materials and surface conditions, not on the area of contact or the object's mass.

Typical values of friction coefficients:

  • Steel on ice: $\mu_s \approx 0.03$ to $0.05$
  • Rubber on dry concrete: $\mu_s \approx 0.7$ to $1.0$
  • Wood on wood: $\mu_s \approx 0.4$ to $0.6$
  • Rubber on wet concrete: $\mu_s \approx 0.5$ to $0.7$
  • Teflon on Teflon: $\mu_s \approx 0.04$

Notice that coefficients of friction can be less than 1 or greater than 1. A coefficient greater than 1 means the maximum friction force can exceed the normal force.

The Normal Force and Friction

The normal force ($N$) is the perpendicular contact force exerted by a surface on an object. "Normal" means perpendicular—the normal force always acts perpendicular to the surface.

Normal force ($N$): The perpendicular force exerted by a surface on an object in contact with it. The normal force prevents objects from passing through surfaces.

For an object resting on a horizontal surface with no other vertical forces, the normal force equals the object's weight: $N = mg$. However, if the surface is tilted or if additional vertical forces act on the object, the normal force will differ from the weight.

The friction force is directly proportional to the normal force. If you press down harder on an object (increasing $N$), the friction force increases proportionally. This is why it's harder to slide a box when someone is sitting on top of it.

Factors Affecting Friction

Surface Texture: Rougher surfaces generally have higher friction coefficients. Smooth surfaces slide more easily past each other.

Normal Force: Greater normal force means greater friction. Pressing surfaces together more firmly increases friction.

Surface Area: Surprisingly, the area of contact does NOT affect friction force. A block lying flat has the same friction as the same block standing on its edge (assuming the normal force is the same). The friction depends only on the materials and the normal force.

Speed: For most materials, kinetic friction is relatively independent of speed, though at very high speeds, friction can decrease slightly.

Friction in Technology

Brakes: Car brakes use friction between brake pads and rotors to convert kinetic energy into heat, slowing the vehicle. Engineers select materials with high friction coefficients for effective braking.

Tires: Tire treads are designed to maximize friction with the road. Racing cars use soft rubber compounds for maximum grip. Winter tires have special patterns and rubber to maintain friction on ice and snow.

Lubricants: Oil and grease reduce friction in engines and machinery, allowing parts to move smoothly and reducing wear. Lubricants work by creating a thin film that separates surfaces.

Example Problems

Problem-Solving Strategy (5 Steps):

  1. List the Givens: Write down all known values with units
  2. Find the Formula: Identify the appropriate equation
  3. Substitute: Plug values into the formula
  4. Solve: Calculate the answer
  5. Check: Verify units and reasonableness

Example 1: Calculating Kinetic Friction

Problem: A 25 kg box is sliding across a floor. The coefficient of kinetic friction between the box and floor is 0.35. What is the kinetic friction force acting on the box?

Step 1 - List the Givens:

$m = 25 \text{ kg}$

$\mu_k = 0.35$

$g = 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$

Step 2 - Find the Formula:

First find normal force: $N = mg$

Then find friction: $f_k = \mu_k N$

Step 3 - Substitute:

$N = (25 \text{ kg})(9.8 \text{ m/s}^2) = 245 \text{ N}$

$f_k = (0.35)(245 \text{ N})$

Step 4 - Solve:

$f_k = 85.75 \text{ N} \approx 86 \text{ N}$

Step 5 - Check:

Units: (no units) × N = N ✓

Reasonableness: Friction is less than the weight (245 N), which makes sense ✓

Answer: The kinetic friction force is 86 N.

Example 2: Maximum Static Friction

Problem: A 50 kg crate rests on a floor where $\mu_s = 0.60$. What is the maximum static friction force that can act on the crate before it starts to move?

Step 1 - List the Givens:

$m = 50 \text{ kg}$

$\mu_s = 0.60$

$g = 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$

Step 2 - Find the Formula:

$N = mg$

$f_{s,max} = \mu_s N$

Step 3 - Substitute:

$N = (50 \text{ kg})(9.8 \text{ m/s}^2) = 490 \text{ N}$

$f_{s,max} = (0.60)(490 \text{ N})$

Step 4 - Solve:

$f_{s,max} = 294 \text{ N}$

Step 5 - Check:

Units correct ✓

The maximum friction (294 N) is less than the weight (490 N), which is reasonable ✓

Answer: The maximum static friction force is 294 N.

Example 3: Comparing Static and Kinetic Friction (Multi-step)

Problem: A 30 kg wooden crate sits on a wooden floor. The coefficient of static friction is $\mu_s = 0.50$ and the coefficient of kinetic friction is $\mu_k = 0.30$. (a) What is the maximum force you can apply before the crate starts to move? (b) Once moving, what friction force acts on the crate? (c) How much less is the kinetic friction compared to maximum static friction?

Part (a): Maximum Static Friction

Step 1 - List the Givens:

$m = 30 \text{ kg}$, $\mu_s = 0.50$, $g = 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$

Step 2 - Find the Formula:

$N = mg$, then $f_{s,max} = \mu_s N$

Step 3 - Substitute:

$N = (30)(9.8) = 294 \text{ N}$

$f_{s,max} = (0.50)(294)$

Step 4 - Solve:

$f_{s,max} = 147 \text{ N}$

Step 5 - Check:

Units correct ✓

Part (b): Kinetic Friction

Step 1 - List the Givens:

$\mu_k = 0.30$, $N = 294 \text{ N}$ (from part a)

Step 2 - Find the Formula:

$f_k = \mu_k N$

Step 3 - Substitute:

$f_k = (0.30)(294 \text{ N})$

Step 4 - Solve:

$f_k = 88.2 \text{ N}$

Step 5 - Check:

Kinetic friction (88.2 N) is less than static friction (147 N) ✓

Part (c): Difference

Difference $= f_{s,max} - f_k = 147 - 88.2 = 58.8 \text{ N}$

Answer: (a) Maximum static friction is 147 N. (b) Kinetic friction is 88.2 N. (c) Kinetic friction is 58.8 N less than maximum static friction.

Summary

  • Friction is a force that opposes the sliding or attempted sliding of surfaces in contact, acting parallel to the surfaces.
  • Static friction acts on objects at rest and can vary from zero up to a maximum value to prevent motion from starting.
  • Kinetic friction acts on objects already in motion and has a relatively constant magnitude.
  • The coefficient of static friction is always greater than the coefficient of kinetic friction: $\mu_s > \mu_k$.
  • Maximum static friction is calculated using $f_{s,max} = \mu_s N$, where $N$ is the normal force.
  • Kinetic friction is calculated using $f_k = \mu_k N$.
  • The coefficient of friction ($\mu$) is dimensionless and depends on the materials and surface conditions.
  • For horizontal surfaces, the normal force equals the weight: $N = mg$.
  • Friction increases with greater normal force but is independent of contact area.
  • Friction is essential for walking, driving, writing, and countless everyday activities.

Practice Problems

1. Explain why it is harder to start pushing a heavy box across the floor than it is to keep it moving once it's already sliding.

2. Why doesn't the area of contact between two surfaces affect the friction force?

3. A book is resting on a table. You push it gently to the right, but it doesn't move. Draw a free-body diagram showing all forces on the book, including friction.

4. Explain the relationship between the normal force and friction force. If you press down harder on an object, what happens to the friction?

5. A 15 kg box slides across a floor where $\mu_k = 0.25$. Calculate the kinetic friction force acting on the box.

6. A 40 kg crate rests on a warehouse floor where $\mu_s = 0.45$. What is the maximum static friction force before the crate starts to move?

7. A 60 kg sled sits on snow where $\mu_s = 0.12$ and $\mu_k = 0.08$. (a) What force is needed to start the sled moving? (b) Once moving, what friction force acts on the sled? (c) How much less force is needed to keep it moving compared to starting it?

8. A 20 kg box requires a horizontal force of 100 N to start it moving across a floor. (a) What is the coefficient of static friction? (b) If the coefficient of kinetic friction is 0.35, what is the kinetic friction force once the box is moving? (c) What applied force would be needed to keep the box moving at constant velocity?

Answer Key

1. It is harder to start the box moving because static friction ($\mu_s$) is greater than kinetic friction ($\mu_k$). When the box is at rest, you must overcome the maximum static friction force to break it free. Once moving, only kinetic friction opposes the motion, which requires less force. The microscopic bonds between surfaces are stronger when stationary than when sliding.

2. The friction force depends on how hard the surfaces are pressed together (normal force) and the materials involved (coefficient of friction), not on the contact area. When area increases, the force per unit area (pressure) decreases proportionally. These effects cancel out, making friction independent of area. This was first discovered experimentally by Amontons and Coulomb.

3. Free-body diagram should show:

  • Weight ($W$ or $mg$) pointing downward
  • Normal force ($N$) pointing upward (equal to weight)
  • Applied force ($F_{applied}$) pointing right
  • Static friction force ($f_s$) pointing left (equal to applied force since book isn't moving)

4. The friction force is directly proportional to the normal force through the equation $f = \mu N$. When you press down harder on an object, you increase the normal force, which increases the friction force by the same factor. For example, doubling the normal force doubles the friction force. This is why heavier objects are harder to push across a surface.

5. Given: $m = 15 \text{ kg}$, $\mu_k = 0.25$, $g = 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$
Normal force: $N = mg = (15)(9.8) = 147 \text{ N}$
Kinetic friction: $f_k = \mu_k N = (0.25)(147) = 36.75 \text{ N}$
Answer: The kinetic friction force is 36.75 N or approximately 37 N.

6. Given: $m = 40 \text{ kg}$, $\mu_s = 0.45$, $g = 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$
Normal force: $N = mg = (40)(9.8) = 392 \text{ N}$
Maximum static friction: $f_{s,max} = \mu_s N = (0.45)(392) = 176.4 \text{ N}$
Answer: The maximum static friction is 176.4 N or approximately 176 N.

7. Given: $m = 60 \text{ kg}$, $\mu_s = 0.12$, $\mu_k = 0.08$, $g = 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$

Normal force: $N = mg = (60)(9.8) = 588 \text{ N}$

(a) Force to start: $f_{s,max} = \mu_s N = (0.12)(588) = 70.56 \text{ N}$

(b) Friction while moving: $f_k = \mu_k N = (0.08)(588) = 47.04 \text{ N}$

(c) Difference: $70.56 - 47.04 = 23.52 \text{ N}$

Answer: (a) 70.6 N needed to start (b) 47.0 N friction while moving (c) 23.5 N less force needed to maintain motion

8. Given: $m = 20 \text{ kg}$, Force to start = $100 \text{ N}$, $\mu_k = 0.35$, $g = 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$

Normal force: $N = mg = (20)(9.8) = 196 \text{ N}$

(a) Since $f_{s,max} = \mu_s N$ and $f_{s,max} = 100 \text{ N}$:
$\mu_s = \frac{f_{s,max}}{N} = \frac{100}{196} = 0.51$

(b) Kinetic friction: $f_k = \mu_k N = (0.35)(196) = 68.6 \text{ N}$

(c) To move at constant velocity, net force must be zero, so applied force must equal kinetic friction: $68.6 \text{ N}$

Answer: (a) $\mu_s = 0.51$ (b) Kinetic friction = 68.6 N (c) Applied force needed = 68.6 N
