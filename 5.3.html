SECTION 5.3

Longitudinal Waves

A longitudinal wave travels through a medium when particles oscillate parallel to the direction of wave propagation. From the sound waves produced by musical instruments to the seismic waves that travel through Earth's interior, longitudinal waves are essential to understanding how energy moves through materials. Discover how compressions and rarefactions create the invisible motion that carries sound to your ears.

Learning Objectives

  • I can identify the characteristics of longitudinal waves and distinguish them from transverse waves.
  • I can explain how compressions and rarefactions form in a longitudinal wave.
  • I can use the wave equation to calculate frequency, wavelength, and wave speed for longitudinal waves.
  • I can apply longitudinal wave concepts to real-world examples such as sound waves and seismic waves.

Introduction

Have you ever watched a Slinky move down a set of stairs? While most people focus on the entertaining zigzag motion, few realize they are observing a fundamental type of wave motion. When you compress one end of a stretched Slinky and release it, the coils bunch up and spread out in a series of patterns that travel along the length of the spring. This motion demonstrates a longitudinal wave—a wave in which particles of the medium move parallel to the direction of the wave's propagation.

Unlike transverse waves, where particles move perpendicular to the wave direction, longitudinal waves require the medium to compress and expand. This makes them ideal for transmitting energy through media like air, water, and solids. Sound waves are the most familiar example of longitudinal waves; they carry music from a speaker to your ear by creating invisible pressure variations in the air. Understanding longitudinal waves is essential for explaining everything from how musical instruments produce different notes to how earthquake waves travel through Earth.

Real-World Connection: Ultrasound machines in hospitals use longitudinal waves to create images inside the human body. These sound waves travel through tissue and reflect off boundaries, creating detailed pictures of organs and developing babies. The same physics principles that explain a Slinky also explain this life-saving medical technology.

Longitudinal Waves: Key Characteristics

What Are Longitudinal Waves?

A longitudinal wave is a mechanical wave in which particles of the medium oscillate parallel to the direction of wave propagation. This is fundamentally different from a transverse wave, where particles move perpendicular to the wave direction. Both types are mechanical waves, meaning they require a medium to travel through—they cannot move through empty space.

The most common examples of longitudinal waves are sound waves. When a loudspeaker cone vibrates back and forth, it pushes air molecules forward and then allows them to spread apart. These molecules collide with neighboring molecules, transferring the disturbance forward. Each molecule oscillates only slightly, moving forward and backward along the direction the sound travels, but the disturbance itself propagates great distances.

Key Difference from Transverse Waves: In a transverse wave (like a wave on a rope), particles move up and down while the wave travels left or right. In a longitudinal wave, particles move left and right while the wave still travels left and right—all motion is parallel to the wave direction.

Compressions and Rarefactions

Longitudinal waves are characterized by two alternating regions: compressions and rarefactions.

A compression is a region where the particles of the medium are squeezed together, creating higher density and higher pressure. When a speaker cone moves forward, it compresses the air in front of it, creating a compression. This high-pressure region expands outward, pushing on neighboring air.

A rarefaction (also called an expansion) is a region where particles are spread farther apart than normal, creating lower density and lower pressure. When the speaker cone moves backward, it pulls the air behind it, creating a rarefaction—a low-pressure region where molecules are more spread out.

Together, compressions and rarefactions form the basic structure of a longitudinal wave. The pattern repeats: compression, rarefaction, compression, rarefaction, and so on. This alternating pattern carries energy through the medium.

Wavelength and Amplitude

Just like transverse waves, longitudinal waves have a wavelength and an amplitude.

Wavelength (represented by the Greek letter λ, pronounced "lambda") is the distance between two consecutive compressions (or two consecutive rarefactions). It represents the spatial length of one complete wave cycle. In the diagram above, you can see that the wavelength spans from the center of one compression to the center of the next compression.

Amplitude for a longitudinal wave is different from that of a transverse wave. Instead of measuring a vertical distance, the amplitude of a longitudinal wave is the maximum change in pressure or density compared to the undisturbed medium. A louder sound wave has greater amplitude (more extreme compressions and rarefactions), while a quieter sound has smaller amplitude.

Important Terms:

  • Frequency (f): The number of complete waves passing a point per second, measured in hertz (Hz)
  • Period (T): The time for one complete wave to pass a point, measured in seconds (s)
  • Wave Speed (v): How fast the wave propagates through the medium, measured in meters per second (m/s)

The Wave Equation

Longitudinal waves obey the same fundamental relationship as transverse waves. The wave equation connects frequency, wavelength, and wave speed:

$$v = f \lambda$$

Where:

  • v = wave speed (m/s)
  • f = frequency (Hz)
  • λ = wavelength (m)

This equation tells us that if you know any two of these quantities, you can always find the third. The speed of a longitudinal wave depends only on the properties of the medium it travels through. For example, sound travels at approximately 343 m/s in air at room temperature, 1,480 m/s in water, and 5,960 m/s in steel. The medium's density and elasticity determine how quickly waves propagate.

Why Speed Varies by Medium: Sound travels faster in denser, more rigid materials because particles are more tightly connected. In steel, atoms are bound strongly together, so a disturbance transmits quickly to the next atom. In air, molecules are far apart, so the disturbance takes longer to reach neighboring molecules. This is why you can "hear" a train coming by placing your ear on the rail—sound travels faster through the steel rail than through the air!

Example Problems

Example 1: Finding Wave Speed from Frequency and Wavelength

Given:

  • Frequency: f = 1.00 × 10⁶ Hz
  • Wavelength: λ = 1.50 mm = 1.50 × 10⁻³ m
  • Medium: Water

Find: What is the speed of this sonar signal traveling through water?

Solution:

Step 1: Identify the wave equation relating the known quantities to what we're finding.

We know frequency and wavelength, and need to find speed, so we use: v = fλ

Step 2: Substitute the values into the equation.

$$v = f \lambda = (1.00 \times 10^6 \text{ Hz})(1.50 \times 10^{-3} \text{ m})$$

Step 3: Perform the calculation.

$$v = 1.5 \times 10^3 \text{ m/s} = 1500 \text{ m/s}$$

Answer: The speed of the sonar signal in water is 1500 m/s or 1.5 × 10³ m/s.

Physical Insight: This speed is about 4 times faster than sound in air (343 m/s). This demonstrates that longitudinal waves (sound waves) travel much faster through denser media. Water's higher density and different elasticity allow compressions and rarefactions to propagate more rapidly than through air.

Example 2: Finding Frequency from Wavelength and Speed

Given:

  • Wavelength: λ = 0.70 m
  • Wave speed: v = 330 m/s
  • Time the wave is produced: t = 0.50 s
  • Medium: Air

Find:

  • a) What is the frequency of the wave?
  • b) How many complete waves are emitted in this time interval?
  • c) After 0.50 s, how far is the wave front from the source?

Solution:

Part a) Finding Frequency

Step 1: Rearrange the wave equation to solve for frequency.

$$v = f \lambda$$

$$f = \frac{v}{\lambda}$$

Step 2: Substitute values.

$$f = \frac{330 \text{ m/s}}{0.70 \text{ m}}$$

Step 3: Calculate.

$$f = 470 \text{ Hz} = 4.7 \times 10^2 \text{ Hz}$$

Answer (a): The frequency is 470 Hz.

Part b) Finding Number of Complete Waves

Step 1: Use the definition of frequency: frequency is the number of cycles per second.

$$\text{Number of waves} = f \times t$$

Step 2: Substitute values.

$$\text{Number of waves} = (470 \text{ Hz})(0.50 \text{ s})$$

Step 3: Calculate.

$$\text{Number of waves} = 235 \text{ cycles}$$

Answer (b): 235 complete waves are emitted in 0.50 s.

Part c) Finding Distance to Wave Front

Step 1: Use the relationship between distance, speed, and time.

$$d = v \times t$$

Step 2: Substitute values.

$$d = (330 \text{ m/s})(0.50 \text{ s})$$

Step 3: Calculate.

$$d = 165 \text{ m}$$

Answer (c): The wave front has traveled 165 m from the source.

Example 3: Multi-Step Problem - Analyzing a Seismic Wave

Given:

  • An earthquake generates a P-wave (longitudinal seismic wave) with frequency 2.0 Hz
  • The wave travels through rock at 6000 m/s
  • A seismograph station is 120 km away
  • Sound travels through air at 340 m/s for comparison

Find:

  • a) What is the wavelength of this seismic wave?
  • b) How long does it take the wave to reach the seismograph station?
  • c) How many complete wave cycles occur during this travel time?

Solution:

Part a) Finding Wavelength of Seismic Wave

Step 1: Rearrange the wave equation to solve for wavelength.

$$v = f \lambda$$

$$\lambda = \frac{v}{f}$$

Step 2: Substitute values.

$$\lambda = \frac{6000 \text{ m/s}}{2.0 \text{ Hz}}$$

Step 3: Calculate.

$$\lambda = 3000 \text{ m} = 3.0 \text{ km}$$

Answer (a): The wavelength is 3000 m or 3.0 km.

Part b) Finding Travel Time to Seismograph Station

Step 1: Convert distance to consistent units.

$$d = 120 \text{ km} = 120,000 \text{ m}$$

Step 2: Rearrange the distance equation to solve for time.

$$d = v \times t$$

$$t = \frac{d}{v}$$

Step 3: Substitute values.

$$t = \frac{120,000 \text{ m}}{6000 \text{ m/s}}$$

Step 4: Calculate.

$$t = 20 \text{ s}$$

Answer (b): It takes 20 seconds for the wave to reach the station.

Part c) Finding Number of Wave Cycles During Travel

Step 1: Use the definition of frequency to find the total number of cycles.

$$\text{Number of cycles} = f \times t$$

Step 2: Substitute values.

$$\text{Number of cycles} = (2.0 \text{ Hz})(20 \text{ s})$$

Step 3: Calculate.

$$\text{Number of cycles} = 40 \text{ cycles}$$

Answer (c): 40 complete wave cycles occur during the 20-second travel time.

Seismic Science: Earthquakes generate different types of waves. P-waves (primary waves) are longitudinal and travel quickly through Earth, arriving first at seismograph stations. S-waves (secondary waves) are transverse and travel slower. By measuring the time difference between P-wave and S-wave arrival, scientists can determine the distance to the earthquake epicenter and help locate where earthquakes occur.

Summary

  • Longitudinal waves are mechanical waves where particles oscillate parallel to the direction of wave propagation, unlike transverse waves where particles move perpendicular to the wave direction.
  • Longitudinal waves consist of alternating compressions (regions of high particle density) and rarefactions (regions of low particle density).
  • The wavelength of a longitudinal wave is the distance between consecutive compressions or consecutive rarefactions.
  • The amplitude of a longitudinal wave measures the maximum change in pressure or density from the undisturbed state.
  • The wave equation $v = f\lambda$ relates wave speed, frequency, and wavelength and applies to all types of waves.
  • Wave speed depends only on the properties of the medium; sound travels faster in denser materials like water and steel than in air.
  • Frequency is the number of complete waves passing a point per second, measured in hertz (Hz).
  • Sound waves are the most common example of longitudinal waves and are essential for communication, music, and perception of the world around us.
  • Longitudinal waves have applications in diverse fields including medicine (ultrasound), geology (seismic waves), acoustics (sound engineering), and technology (sonar).
  • Understanding longitudinal waves requires distinguishing between particle motion (oscillation parallel to wave direction) and wave motion (energy propagation through the medium).

Answer Key

Note to Teachers: This answer key provides complete solutions for all practice problems. Partial credit should be awarded based on correct method and reasoning, even if the final numerical answer contains minor errors.

Conceptual Questions (1-4)

Answer 1: Comparing Waves

Key Points for a Complete Answer:

  • In a longitudinal wave, particles move parallel (forward and backward) to the direction the wave travels.
  • In a transverse wave, particles move perpendicular (up and down) to the direction the wave travels.
  • When a Slinky is pushed along its length, coils compress and expand along the direction of motion → longitudinal wave.
  • When a Slinky is shaken side to side, coils move up and down while the wave pattern travels horizontally → transverse wave.

Answer 2: Compressions and Rarefactions

Correct Response:

As the tuning fork prong moves forward, it compresses the air molecules directly in front of it, creating a region of higher density and pressure called a compression. Behind the prong, a low-density, low-pressure region called a rarefaction forms as air molecules spread apart. As the prong moves backward, this pattern reverses. The compressions and rarefactions propagate outward through the air, carrying the sound energy away from the fork.

Answer 3: Wavelength Definition

Correct Response:

The wavelength of a longitudinal wave is the distance between two consecutive compressions (or two consecutive rarefactions). To measure wavelength in a spring, you would identify where two compressions are closest together, then measure the distance between the centers of these two regions. This distance equals one wavelength. Alternatively, you could measure from the center of one compression to the center of the next compression.

Answer 4: Real-World Application

Key Points for a Complete Answer:

  • Wave speed depends on medium properties: sound travels faster in water (1480 m/s) than in air (343 m/s).
  • In water, a compression or rarefaction reaches objects faster, delivering energy more quickly.
  • Water's higher density and elasticity transmit forces more efficiently than air.
  • The higher speed combined with water's incompressibility creates larger pressure changes.
  • These factors combine to make underwater explosions more destructive despite the same energy source.

Calculation Problems (5-6)

Answer 5: Radio Wave Speed

Given: f = 1.00 × 10⁸ Hz, λ = 3.00 m

Find: v = ?

Solution:

$$v = f\lambda$$

$$v = (1.00 \times 10^8 \text{ Hz})(3.00 \text{ m})$$

$$v = 3.00 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s}$$

Answer: $v = 3.00 \times 10^8$ m/s (This is the speed of light, which makes sense since radio waves are electromagnetic waves.)

Answer 6: Sound Wave Frequency

Given: v = 1480 m/s, λ = 0.740 m

Find: f = ?

Solution:

$$f = \frac{v}{\lambda}$$

$$f = \frac{1480 \text{ m/s}}{0.740 \text{ m}}$$

$$f = 2000 \text{ Hz} = 2.00 \times 10^3 \text{ Hz}$$

Answer: $f = 2000$ Hz or $2.00 \times 10^3$ Hz

Multi-Step Problems (7-8)

Answer 7: Sound Wave Analysis

Given: f = 262 Hz, v = 343 m/s, t = 2.5 s

Part a) Wavelength

$$\lambda = \frac{v}{f} = \frac{343 \text{ m/s}}{262 \text{ Hz}} = 1.31 \text{ m}$$

Answer (a): λ = 1.31 m

Part b) Distance Traveled in 2.5 s

$$d = vt = (343 \text{ m/s})(2.5 \text{ s}) = 857.5 \text{ m}$$

Answer (b): d = 857.5 m or 858 m

Part c) Number of Cycles in 2.5 s

$$n = ft = (262 \text{ Hz})(2.5 \text{ s}) = 655 \text{ cycles}$$

Answer (c): n = 655 complete cycles

Answer 8: Sonar Range Calculation

Given: Total time = 4.00 s, v = 1520 m/s, f = 50,000 Hz

Part a) Total Distance Traveled

Step 1: Use distance = speed × time

$$d_{\text{total}} = vt = (1520 \text{ m/s})(4.00 \text{ s}) = 6080 \text{ m}$$

Answer (a): Total distance = 6080 m or 6.08 km

Part b) Ocean Depth

Step 1: The total distance includes the round trip (down and back up), so divide by 2.

$$d_{\text{depth}} = \frac{d_{\text{total}}}{2} = \frac{6080 \text{ m}}{2} = 3040 \text{ m}$$

Answer (b): Ocean depth = 3040 m or 3.04 km

Part c) Wavelength of Sonar Pulse

Step 1: Use the wave equation to find wavelength.

$$\lambda = \frac{v}{f} = \frac{1520 \text{ m/s}}{50,000 \text{ Hz}} = \frac{1520}{50,000} = 0.0304 \text{ m}$$

Step 2: Convert to more convenient units.

$$\lambda = 0.0304 \text{ m} = 3.04 \text{ cm}$$

Answer (c): λ = 0.0304 m or 3.04 cm

Physical Interpretation: The short wavelength of high-frequency sonar allows detection of small underwater objects. Higher frequency waves create shorter wavelengths, which can resolve finer details.
