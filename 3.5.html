SECTION 3.5

Gravitational Force and Inclined Planes

Inclined planes—ramps—are everywhere in our world, from wheelchair ramps to ski slopes to highways climbing mountains. Understanding forces on inclined planes requires us to resolve weight into components parallel and perpendicular to the surface, combining our knowledge of trigonometry with Newton's laws.

Introduction

Why is it easier to push a heavy box up a ramp than to lift it straight up? How do mountain roads with gentle grades allow vehicles to climb steep mountains? Why do objects slide down ramps faster when the angle is steeper? The answers lie in understanding how forces act on inclined planes—surfaces tilted at an angle.

Inclined planes are one of the six simple machines, devices that make work easier. Ancient Egyptians used ramps to build the pyramids, moving massive stone blocks that would have been impossible to lift vertically. Modern applications include wheelchair ramps, loading docks, highway on-ramps, and even children's playground slides.

The Physics of Mountain Roads

Mountain highways don't go straight up mountains—they wind back and forth, creating a gentler incline. A road climbing 1000 meters over a horizontal distance of 10,000 meters has only a 6-degree angle. This gentle slope allows vehicles to climb with manageable force, though traveling a longer distance. The trade-off between force and distance is a fundamental principle of simple machines.

Understanding Inclined Planes

An inclined plane is a flat surface tilted at an angle to the horizontal. The angle of the incline, typically denoted by $\theta$ (theta), determines how steep the ramp is.

Inclined plane: A flat surface set at an angle to the horizontal, allowing objects to be raised or lowered over a sloped distance rather than vertically. Also called a ramp.

Angle of incline ($\theta$): The angle between the inclined surface and the horizontal. Measured in degrees, with $\theta = 0°$ being horizontal and $\theta = 90°$ being vertical.

Resolving Weight into Components

When an object sits on an inclined plane, its weight ($W = mg$) points straight down toward Earth's center. However, to analyze the object's motion, we need to understand how this weight affects the object relative to the ramp's surface.

We resolve weight into two components:

1. Perpendicular Component ($W_\perp$ or $W_{\text{perp}}$): The component of weight perpendicular (at right angles) to the ramp surface. This component presses the object into the ramp but doesn't cause sliding.

2. Parallel Component ($W_\parallel$ or $W_{\text{par}}$): The component of weight parallel to the ramp surface. This component pulls the object down the ramp and causes sliding.

Parallel component: The part of the weight force acting along the inclined surface, causing the object to accelerate down the ramp. Given by $W_\parallel = mg\sin\theta$

Perpendicular component: The part of the weight force acting perpendicular to the inclined surface, pressing the object into the ramp. Given by $W_\perp = mg\cos\theta$

Trigonometric Relationships

Using trigonometry, we can calculate these components:

$$W_\parallel = W \sin\theta = mg\sin\theta$$

$$W_\perp = W \cos\theta = mg\cos\theta$$

Where:

  • $W$ = total weight = $mg$
  • $\theta$ = angle of the incline
  • $W_\parallel$ = component parallel to ramp
  • $W_\perp$ = component perpendicular to ramp

Memory Tip: "Parallel uses sine, perpendicular uses cosine." Or remember: at $\theta = 0°$ (flat surface), $\cos(0°) = 1$ and $\sin(0°) = 0$, so all weight is perpendicular and none is parallel—which makes sense for a flat surface!

Why These Equations Work

The weight vector points straight down and has magnitude $W = mg$. When we tilt the coordinate system to align with the ramp, the weight vector forms a right triangle. The angle between the weight vector and the perpendicular to the ramp equals $\theta$ (the angle of the incline). Using trigonometry:

  • The side opposite to $\theta$ is $W_\parallel$, so $W_\parallel = W\sin\theta$
  • The side adjacent to $\theta$ is $W_\perp$, so $W_\perp = W\cos\theta$

Forces on an Inclined Plane

An object on an inclined plane typically experiences four forces:

1. Weight ($W$): Points straight down, magnitude $mg$. We resolve this into components.

2. Normal Force ($N$): Points perpendicular to the ramp surface, away from the ramp. On an incline, $N = W_\perp = mg\cos\theta$ (when there are no other vertical forces).

3. Friction ($f$): Points parallel to the ramp, opposing motion or potential motion. For kinetic friction, $f_k = \mu_k N = \mu_k mg\cos\theta$.

4. Applied Force (if present): Any external push or pull.

The Normal Force on Inclines

On a horizontal surface, the normal force equals the weight ($N = mg$). On an incline, the normal force equals only the perpendicular component of weight:

$$N = mg\cos\theta$$

As the angle increases, $\cos\theta$ decreases, so the normal force decreases. At $\theta = 90°$ (vertical wall), $\cos(90°) = 0$, so $N = 0$—there's no normal force from a vertical wall unless you're pushing into it.

Motion on Frictionless Inclines

On a frictionless incline, only the parallel component of weight causes acceleration down the ramp. Using Newton's Second Law along the ramp:

$$F_{net} = W_\parallel = ma$$

$$mg\sin\theta = ma$$

$$a = g\sin\theta$$

This equation tells us that acceleration down a frictionless ramp depends only on the angle, not on the object's mass. A feather and a bowling ball would accelerate equally down a frictionless ramp!

Motion on Inclines with Friction

When friction is present, it opposes the motion down the ramp. The net force becomes:

$$F_{net} = W_\parallel - f = ma$$

$$mg\sin\theta - \mu_k mg\cos\theta = ma$$

$$a = g(\sin\theta - \mu_k\cos\theta)$$

If $\sin\theta > \mu_k\cos\theta$, the object accelerates down the ramp.

If $\sin\theta = \mu_k\cos\theta$, the object moves at constant velocity (if already moving).

If $\sin\theta < \mu_k\cos\theta$, static friction prevents motion (object stays at rest).

Real-World Applications

Wheelchair Ramps: Building codes specify maximum ramp angles (typically 4.8° or 1:12 slope) to ensure wheelchair users can navigate safely. Steeper ramps require too much force to ascend and are difficult to control when descending.

Ski Slopes: Beginner slopes (green) have angles of 6-25°, intermediate (blue) 25-40°, and expert (black) 40°+. The steeper angle means greater $\sin\theta$, resulting in faster acceleration.

Truck Runaway Ramps: On mountain highways, special gravel ramps allow trucks with brake failure to decelerate safely. The upward incline and high-friction gravel maximize deceleration.

Example Problems

Problem-Solving Strategy (5 Steps):

  1. List the Givens: Write down all known values with units
  2. Find the Formula: Identify the appropriate equation
  3. Substitute: Plug values into the formula
  4. Solve: Calculate the answer
  5. Check: Verify units and reasonableness

Example 1: Resolving Weight Components

Problem: A 20 kg box sits on a ramp inclined at 30°. Calculate (a) the parallel component of weight and (b) the perpendicular component of weight.

Part (a): Parallel Component

Step 1 - List the Givens:

$m = 20 \text{ kg}$, $\theta = 30°$, $g = 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$

Step 2 - Find the Formula:

$W_\parallel = mg\sin\theta$

Step 3 - Substitute:

$W_\parallel = (20)(9.8)\sin(30°)$

$W_\parallel = (196)(0.5)$

Step 4 - Solve:

$W_\parallel = 98 \text{ N}$

Step 5 - Check:

Units correct ✓ Component less than total weight (196 N) ✓

Part (b): Perpendicular Component

Step 1 - List the Givens:

$m = 20 \text{ kg}$, $\theta = 30°$, $g = 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$

Step 2 - Find the Formula:

$W_\perp = mg\cos\theta$

Step 3 - Substitute:

$W_\perp = (20)(9.8)\cos(30°)$

$W_\perp = (196)(0.866)$

Step 4 - Solve:

$W_\perp = 169.7 \text{ N}$

Step 5 - Check:

Using Pythagorean theorem: $\sqrt{98^2 + 169.7^2} = 196$ N = total weight ✓

Answer: (a) Parallel component: 98 N (b) Perpendicular component: 169.7 N

Example 2: Acceleration on Frictionless Ramp

Problem: A 10 kg object slides down a frictionless ramp inclined at 25°. What is the object's acceleration?

Step 1 - List the Givens:

$m = 10 \text{ kg}$, $\theta = 25°$, $g = 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$, frictionless

Step 2 - Find the Formula:

$a = g\sin\theta$ (for frictionless ramp)

Step 3 - Substitute:

$a = (9.8)\sin(25°)$

$a = (9.8)(0.423)$

Step 4 - Solve:

$a = 4.14 \text{ m/s}^2$

Step 5 - Check:

Acceleration less than $g$ ✓ Independent of mass ✓

Answer: The acceleration is 4.14 m/s² down the ramp.

Example 3: Inclined Plane with Friction (Multi-step)

Problem: A 30 kg sled slides down a 20° snowy slope. The coefficient of kinetic friction is 0.10. (a) Calculate the parallel component of weight. (b) Calculate the normal force. (c) Calculate the friction force. (d) Find the net force. (e) Calculate the acceleration.

Part (a): Parallel Component

Step 1 - List the Givens:

$m = 30 \text{ kg}$, $\theta = 20°$, $g = 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$

Step 2 - Find the Formula:

$W_\parallel = mg\sin\theta$

Step 3 - Substitute:

$W_\parallel = (30)(9.8)\sin(20°) = (294)(0.342)$

Step 4 - Solve:

$W_\parallel = 100.5 \text{ N}$

Part (b): Normal Force

$N = mg\cos\theta = (30)(9.8)\cos(20°) = (294)(0.940) = 276.4 \text{ N}$

Part (c): Friction Force

$f_k = \mu_k N = (0.10)(276.4) = 27.6 \text{ N}$

Part (d): Net Force

Taking down the ramp as positive:

$F_{net} = W_\parallel - f_k = 100.5 - 27.6 = 72.9 \text{ N}$

Part (e): Acceleration

Step 1 - List the Givens:

$F_{net} = 72.9 \text{ N}$, $m = 30 \text{ kg}$

Step 2 - Find the Formula:

$a = F_{net}/m$

Step 3 - Substitute:

$a = 72.9/30$

Step 4 - Solve:

$a = 2.43 \text{ m/s}^2$

Step 5 - Check:

Acceleration less than frictionless case ($g\sin 20° = 3.35$ m/s²) ✓

Answer: (a) 100.5 N (b) 276.4 N (c) 27.6 N (d) 72.9 N down the ramp (e) 2.43 m/s²

Summary

  • Inclined planes are flat surfaces tilted at an angle, allowing objects to be raised or lowered over a sloped distance.
  • Weight on an incline resolves into parallel and perpendicular components using trigonometry.
  • Parallel component: $W_\parallel = mg\sin\theta$ causes the object to slide down the ramp.
  • Perpendicular component: $W_\perp = mg\cos\theta$ presses the object into the ramp.
  • The normal force on an incline equals the perpendicular component: $N = mg\cos\theta$.
  • On a frictionless incline, acceleration is $a = g\sin\theta$, independent of mass.
  • With friction, acceleration is $a = g(\sin\theta - \mu_k\cos\theta)$.
  • Friction force on incline: $f = \mu mg\cos\theta$ opposes motion down the ramp.
  • As angle increases, parallel component increases and perpendicular component decreases.
  • Inclined planes are used in wheelchair ramps, loading docks, highways, and countless applications to reduce required force.

Practice Problems

1. Explain why we need to resolve weight into components when analyzing motion on an inclined plane. Why can't we just use the total weight?

2. A box sits on a 40° ramp. Which is larger: the parallel component or the perpendicular component of weight? Explain your reasoning.

3. Why does the normal force on an inclined plane decrease as the angle of incline increases?

4. Draw a free-body diagram for a block sliding down a ramp with friction. Label all forces including weight components.

5. A 15 kg box sits on a ramp tilted at 35°. Calculate the parallel and perpendicular components of the box's weight.

6. A 50 kg person stands on a 15° ramp. What is the normal force the ramp exerts on the person?

7. A 25 kg sled slides down a frictionless 30° slope. (a) What is the sled's acceleration down the slope? (b) If the sled starts from rest, what is its velocity after sliding 20 meters down the slope? (Use $v^2 = u^2 + 2as$ where $u = 0$)

8. A 40 kg crate slides down a 25° ramp. The coefficient of kinetic friction is 0.20. (a) Calculate the parallel component of weight. (b) Find the normal force. (c) Calculate the friction force. (d) Determine the net force. (e) What is the crate's acceleration?

Answer Key

1. We resolve weight into components because motion on an inclined plane occurs along the ramp surface (parallel direction) and perpendicular to it. The total weight points straight down, but we need to know how much of that weight acts parallel to the ramp (causing sliding) and how much acts perpendicular (pressing into the ramp). The parallel component determines acceleration, while the perpendicular component determines the normal force and friction.

2. At 40°, we can calculate: $\sin(40°) = 0.643$ and $\cos(40°) = 0.766$. Since $\cos(40°) > \sin(40°)$, the perpendicular component is larger than the parallel component. This makes sense because at angles less than 45°, the ramp is closer to horizontal, so most of the weight presses into the ramp rather than pulling down along it. At exactly 45°, both components are equal.

3. The normal force equals $N = mg\cos\theta$. As the angle $\theta$ increases, $\cos\theta$ decreases (cosine decreases from 1 to 0 as angle goes from 0° to 90°). At 0° (horizontal), all weight is perpendicular to the surface. As the angle increases, more weight becomes parallel and less is perpendicular. At 90° (vertical wall), there's no weight perpendicular to the surface, so $N = 0$.

4. Free-body diagram should show:

  • Coordinate axes: one parallel to ramp (x), one perpendicular (y)
  • Weight components: $W_\parallel$ down the ramp, $W_\perp$ into the ramp
  • Normal force $N$ perpendicular to ramp, away from surface (equals $W_\perp$)
  • Friction force $f$ up the ramp (opposing motion)

5. Given: $m = 15 \text{ kg}$, $\theta = 35°$, $g = 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$
Weight: $W = mg = (15)(9.8) = 147 \text{ N}$
Parallel: $W_\parallel = mg\sin\theta = (147)\sin(35°) = (147)(0.574) = 84.4 \text{ N}$
Perpendicular: $W_\perp = mg\cos\theta = (147)\cos(35°) = (147)(0.819) = 120.4 \text{ N}$
Answer: Parallel: 84.4 N, Perpendicular: 120.4 N

6. Given: $m = 50 \text{ kg}$, $\theta = 15°$, $g = 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$
Normal force: $N = mg\cos\theta = (50)(9.8)\cos(15°)$
$N = (490)(0.966) = 473.3 \text{ N}$
Answer: The normal force is 473.3 N.

7. Given: $m = 25 \text{ kg}$, $\theta = 30°$, $g = 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$, frictionless, $s = 20 \text{ m}$

(a) Acceleration: $a = g\sin\theta = (9.8)\sin(30°) = (9.8)(0.5) = 4.9 \text{ m/s}^2$

(b) Using $v^2 = u^2 + 2as$ with $u = 0$:
$v^2 = 0 + 2(4.9)(20) = 196$
$v = 14 \text{ m/s}$

Answer: (a) 4.9 m/s² (b) 14 m/s

8. Given: $m = 40 \text{ kg}$, $\theta = 25°$, $\mu_k = 0.20$, $g = 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$

(a) Parallel component: $W_\parallel = mg\sin\theta = (40)(9.8)\sin(25°) = (392)(0.423) = 165.8 \text{ N}$

(b) Normal force: $N = mg\cos\theta = (40)(9.8)\cos(25°) = (392)(0.906) = 355.2 \text{ N}$

(c) Friction: $f_k = \mu_k N = (0.20)(355.2) = 71.0 \text{ N}$

(d) Net force: $F_{net} = W_\parallel - f_k = 165.8 - 71.0 = 94.8 \text{ N}$ (down the ramp)

(e) Acceleration: $a = F_{net}/m = 94.8/40 = 2.37 \text{ m/s}^2$

Answer: (a) 165.8 N (b) 355.2 N (c) 71.0 N (d) 94.8 N (e) 2.37 m/s²
