SECTION 5.6

Sound Waves

Sound is everywhere around us—from the gentle rustle of leaves to the roar of a concert stage. Every sound we hear begins with vibrations and travels through the materials around us. In this section, you'll discover how sound waves are created, how they travel through different materials, and why some materials transmit sound while others absorb it. Understanding sound waves is essential to acoustics, music, hearing, and countless applications in modern technology.

Learning Objectives

  • I can define sound and explain how sound waves are generated by vibrating objects.
  • I can describe the properties of sound waves, including that they are longitudinal waves that require a medium.
  • I can explain how sound waves travel through different materials and why some materials transmit sound better than others.
  • I can apply the relationship between vibration, energy transfer, and sound wave propagation to real-world situations.

Introduction

The Falling Tree Riddle. Imagine an old tree in a remote forest falls to the ground with a tremendous crash. No one is there to hear it. Did the tree make a sound? This classic riddle introduces an important scientific distinction. When we speak colloquially, we might say "no sound occurred" because no one heard it. But scientifically, sound was absolutely created by the falling tree. Understanding this difference requires us to think carefully about what sound actually is.

Real-World Relevance. Sound surrounds our daily lives in countless ways. Musicians depend on understanding sound waves to create music. Doctors use ultrasound technology—which relies on very high-frequency sound waves—to image inside the human body. Engineers designing concert halls must control how sound travels and reflects through spaces. Hearing protection specialists work to prevent sound from damaging our ears. The technology in your smartphone, headphones, and speakers all depends on precisely manipulating sound waves. Understanding sound waves empowers you to comprehend and control an invisible but powerful part of our world.

Historical Context. The scientific study of sound began in earnest during the 1600s when Robert Boyle, the famous physicist known for Boyle's Law, conducted a groundbreaking experiment. He placed a ticking clock inside a sealed glass jar and pumped the air out, creating a vacuum. The clock continued to tick, but no sound could be heard. This elegant demonstration proved that sound requires a medium through which to travel—it cannot propagate through empty space. This insight laid the foundation for all modern understanding of sound as a mechanical wave phenomenon.

Connection to Prior Knowledge. You have already learned about waves—disturbances that transfer energy from one location to another. You understand that transverse waves cause particles to vibrate perpendicular to the wave's direction of travel, like ripples on a pond. Sound waves, however, are different. They are longitudinal waves, where particles vibrate parallel to the direction the wave travels. In this section, you will expand your wave knowledge to understand this important category of waves that directly affects your life every moment.

What is Sound?

Sound is the transfer of energy from a vibrating object in waves that travel through matter. In everyday language, we often use "sound" to mean the sensation we experience when sound waves enter our ears and our brain interprets them. However, the scientific definition is more precise: sound is the energy transfer mechanism itself, not necessarily the perception.

This distinction matters for answering the falling tree riddle. The falling tree did create sound waves according to the scientific definition because energy was transferred in waves through matter (the air and ground). However, if no ear was present to receive those waves and translate them into the neural signals we call "hearing," then in the everyday sense, there was "no sound heard."

For the rest of this course, we will use the scientific definition: sound is the transfer of energy from vibrating objects in waves that travel through matter.

How Sound Waves Begin: Vibrations

Every sound begins with something vibrating. Consider a guitar string. When you pluck the string, you disturb it from its resting position. The string then moves back and forth repeatedly in a regular pattern—this is vibration. Each time the string moves, it pushes against the air particles next to it. These air particles are set into motion and begin vibrating themselves. They collide with neighboring air particles, transferring the vibrational motion outward in all directions away from the string.

Real-World Example: Musical Instruments

Every musical instrument creates sound through vibration. A piano string vibrates when struck by a hammer. A drum head vibrates when hit with a stick or hand. The vocal cords in your throat vibrate when air from your lungs passes through them, creating your voice. Even the speaker cone in your headphones vibrates to produce the music you hear. All of these instruments are transferring the energy of their vibrations to the surrounding medium (usually air) as waves.

Longitudinal Waves: The Nature of Sound

Longitudinal waves are waves in which particles of the medium vibrate back and forth parallel to the direction that the wave travels. This is different from transverse waves, where particles vibrate perpendicular to the wave direction.

To visualize a longitudinal wave, imagine a line of students standing in a row. If they all repeatedly step forward and backward in unison, with each student's motion triggering the next student to move, a disturbance travels along the row. However, each student moves forward and backward (parallel to the row's direction), not side-to-side. This forward-and-backward motion of particles parallel to the wave direction is what characterizes a longitudinal wave.

In sound waves specifically, the air particles vibrate back and forth along the direction the sound is traveling. When the vibrating source (like a guitar string) pushes forward, it compresses the nearby air particles together. When the source pulls back, it creates a region where air particles spread apart. These regions of compression (where particles are squeezed together) and rarefaction (where particles are spread apart) alternate as they propagate outward from the source.

Sound Requires a Medium

A medium is a material through which a wave travels. Sound is a mechanical wave—a wave that requires matter to propagate. Unlike light, which can travel through the vacuum of space, sound cannot travel through empty space.

Robert Boyle's 1660s experiment provides the clearest proof of this principle. When he pumped air from a sealed jar containing a ticking clock, the sound disappeared completely. The clock was still vibrating and creating sound waves, but without air particles to carry the vibrations, the sound energy had no way to propagate.

Sound travels best through materials with closely packed particles. Air is a common medium for sound, but sound also travels through liquids and solids. In fact, sound typically travels faster through solids and liquids than through air. For example, sound travels at approximately 343 m/s in air at room temperature, but at approximately 1,480 m/s in water and approximately 5,000 m/s in steel.

Real-World Application: Underwater Communication

Whales and dolphins communicate across vast distances underwater using sound waves. Because sound travels so much faster and more efficiently through water than through air, these marine mammals can hear each other from miles away. Humans have adapted this principle by developing sonar technology, which uses sound waves reflected off objects to detect submarines and map the ocean floor. Understanding that sound requires a medium and travels at different speeds through different materials made these technologies possible.

Sound Travels Through Different Materials

Most sounds we experience travel through air. When a friend speaks to you, sound waves propagate through the air between your friend's mouth and your ears. However, sound can also travel effectively through liquids and solids.

Transmission of sound refers to how well sound passes through a material. Some materials are excellent transmitters of sound. Glass, metals, and water all transmit sound very effectively. If you submerge your ears in a bathtub, you can hear sounds traveling through the water. You can hear people talking on the other side of a glass window because sound transmits through the glass.

Absorption of sound refers to how much sound energy a material takes in rather than transmitting or reflecting. Foam rubber, heavy fabrics, carpets, and other soft, porous materials absorb sound very effectively. This is why concert halls often have foam-lined walls and carpeted floors—to control unwanted echoes and reflections. Earplugs work by being inserted into the ear canal and absorbing sound energy before it reaches the delicate inner ear structures.

Example Problems

Example 1: Identifying the Medium for Sound

Problem: A person is swimming underwater at the beach. They hear the sound of a boat's motor vibrating in the water. Identify the medium through which the sound is traveling and explain why sound can travel through this medium.

Solution:

The medium is water. Sound travels through water because water is a liquid composed of closely packed particles that can vibrate and transfer energy. When the boat's motor vibrates, it disturbs the water particles next to it. These particles vibrate and collide with neighboring water particles, transferring the vibrational motion outward through the liquid in all directions. The closely packed structure of water particles makes it an excellent medium for sound propagation. This is why the swimmer can clearly hear the boat motor from underwater—the sound waves have a continuous path of water particles through which to travel.

Answer: The medium is water. Sound travels through water because water consists of closely packed particles that can vibrate back and forth in longitudinal waves, allowing energy transfer from the vibrating motor to the swimmer's ear.

Example 2: Sound Transmission Through Different Materials

Problem: You are in a house during a thunderstorm. You can clearly hear thunder through the glass windows, but when you step outside the house and stand near a closed wooden door, the sound from inside the house is much quieter. Explain why sound transmits differently through glass versus through solid wood.

Solution:

Glass and wood are both solid materials, but they differ significantly in how they transmit sound. Glass is an excellent transmitter of sound because its particles are tightly packed in a rigid structure that efficiently passes vibrations from one side to the other. When thunder creates sound waves in the air outside, these waves vibrate the glass pane, and the vibrations continue through the glass and emerge on the inside as sound waves again.

Wood, particularly solid wood doors, absorbs sound much more effectively than glass because it has a porous, fibrous structure. Sound waves cause the wood particles to vibrate, but the complex network of fibers and small air pockets within the wood converts much of the sound energy into heat through friction, rather than transmitting it through to the other side. Additionally, many doors are designed or treated to absorb sound for this reason.

Answer: Glass transmits sound well because its dense, rigid structure efficiently passes vibrations through. Wood absorbs sound because its porous, fibrous structure traps sound energy and converts it to heat through friction rather than transmitting it to the other side.

Example 3: Designing an Acoustic Environment

Problem: A school is constructing a new music practice room for the band program. The room will be built in the basement, adjacent to the library study area. The architects want to minimize sound transmission between the two spaces. Based on what you know about sound transmission and absorption, explain how you would design the wall between these two rooms and why your design would be effective.

Solution:

To minimize sound transmission from the band practice room to the library, the wall should be designed to absorb sound rather than transmit it. Here's why and how:

Why absorption is better than transmission: If the wall transmits sound, vibrations from the band instruments would pass through to the library side, disturbing students studying there. If the wall absorbs sound, the wave energy is trapped within the wall material and converted to heat, preventing vibrations from reaching the library.

Recommended wall design: The wall should include multiple layers: a solid outer barrier (like drywall), air gaps, and porous absorbing materials (like fiberglass insulation or foam). The porous materials have many small spaces that trap sound waves. When sound waves enter these spaces, the particles of the absorbing material vibrate repeatedly, causing friction that converts the sound energy to heat.

Why multiple layers work better than a single thick barrier: Different frequencies of sound may be absorbed or blocked differently by different materials. By using multiple layers at varying depths, the design can effectively reduce both high-frequency sounds (like cymbals) and low-frequency sounds (like drums). Air gaps between layers also allow for additional vibration and energy dissipation.

Answer: Design the wall with multiple layers: drywall, air gap, fiberglass/foam insulation, air gap, and inner drywall. This design absorbs rather than transmits sound because the porous materials trap sound waves and convert energy to heat through vibration and friction. Multiple layers at different depths absorb a wider range of sound frequencies more effectively than a single solid barrier would.

Summary

  • Sound is scientifically defined as the transfer of energy from a vibrating object in waves that travel through matter, distinct from the everyday perception of "hearing."
  • All sound waves originate from vibrating matter—objects moving back and forth at a regular frequency.
  • Longitudinal waves characterize sound, with particles vibrating parallel to the direction the wave travels, creating alternating regions of compression and rarefaction.
  • Sound is a mechanical wave that requires a medium (matter) through which to propagate; it cannot travel through empty space or a vacuum.
  • Sound travels through gases (air), liquids (water), and solids (glass, metal, wood), but travels at different speeds in each medium.
  • Transmission describes sound's ability to pass through a material; dense, solid materials like glass and metal transmit sound effectively.
  • Absorption describes a material's ability to take in sound energy and prevent transmission; porous materials like foam and fabric are excellent absorbers.
  • The behavior of sound in different materials explains everyday phenomena: hearing conversations through windows, muffled sound through walls, silent environments created by foam insulation, and the effectiveness of earplugs.
  • Understanding sound wave behavior is essential for applications including acoustics, music production, hearing protection, medical imaging (ultrasound), and sonar technology.
  • Robert Boyle's 1660s vacuum experiment provided the first clear scientific proof that sound requires a medium and cannot propagate through empty space.

Answer Key

Conceptual Questions - Answer Key

1. Answer:

Scientific definition: Sound is the transfer of energy from a vibrating object in waves that travel through matter. This is true regardless of whether anyone hears it.

Everyday usage: "Sound" often refers to what we hear—the perception and neural interpretation of sound waves.

Falling tree example: A falling tree in an empty forest DOES create sound according to the scientific definition because energy is transferred in waves through the air and ground. However, there is "no sound heard" in the everyday sense because no ear is present to detect and interpret those waves. The answer to the riddle is "yes and no" depending on which definition you use.

2. Answer:

Mechanical wave: Sound is mechanical because it is created by vibrating matter and requires matter to propagate. It's not electromagnetic like light.

Requires a medium: Sound cannot travel through empty space. It needs material particles (air, water, solid) that can vibrate and transfer energy through compressions and rarefactions.

In a perfect vacuum: Sound would not propagate at all because there are no particles to vibrate and transfer the vibrational energy. This was demonstrated by Robert Boyle's vacuum experiment in the 1600s.

3. Answer:

When a drum is struck, the drumhead vibrates up and down rapidly. As it moves upward, it compresses the air particles above it, creating a high-pressure region (compression). As it moves downward, it creates a low-pressure region (rarefaction) where air particles spread apart. These alternating compressions and rarefactions propagate outward from the drum in all directions as longitudinal waves through the air. The air particles vibrate parallel to the direction the wave travels. When these sound waves reach a person's ear, they cause the eardrum to vibrate, which the brain interprets as sound.

4. Answer:

Foam panels and carpet (absorption): These porous materials contain many tiny air spaces. When sound waves enter, the particles of the material vibrate repeatedly. This vibration in the complex, porous structure causes friction that converts sound energy into heat. The sound energy is "trapped" within the material and doesn't propagate further, which prevents echoes and reduces noise in concert halls.

Hard tile and concrete (transmission): These dense, smooth materials have fewer air spaces. Sound waves vibrate these rigid materials, but the vibrations pass efficiently through to the other side without much energy loss. This is why recording studios sometimes use hard surfaces to capture direct sound without absorption.

5. Answer:

When the window is open, sound waves from the thunderstorm travel directly through the air gap into your room with minimal obstruction. When you close the window, the glass creates a barrier. However, glass is an excellent transmitter of sound—the glass particles vibrate in response to the sound waves, and these vibrations continue through the glass material. The sound is quieter through a closed window because the glass is thick and some of the energy is lost as the waves pass through, but also because the transition from air to glass to air causes some reflection at the glass surfaces. Additionally, the small surface area of the window reduces the amount of sound that reaches you compared to the entire open window area.

6. Answer:

Robert Boyle's experiment demonstrated that sound requires a medium to propagate. Initially, the air in the jar carried sound waves from the vibrating clock to the outside where they could be heard. When Boyle pumped out the air, creating a vacuum, there were no particles present to vibrate and transfer the vibrational energy from the clock. Even though the clock was still ticking and creating vibrations, these vibrations had no medium through which to travel, so no sound reached the observer's ear. This experiment proved that sound is a mechanical wave and cannot travel through empty space—it must have a material medium composed of particles that can vibrate and pass the vibrations along.

7. Answer:

Earplugs reduce sound by absorbing sound wave energy rather than transmitting it. A good earplug material must be porous—containing many small air spaces and fibers. When sound waves enter the earplug, the particles within the porous material vibrate repeatedly. This vibration within the complex, fiber-filled structure causes friction that converts sound energy into heat. The more porous and thick the earplug material, the more energy it can absorb before sound reaches the sensitive inner ear. By absorbing the sound waves, earplugs prevent vibrations from transmitting to the ear canal and inner ear, effectively reducing the sound level that reaches the hearing structures.

8. Answer:

Sound travels much farther and more clearly underwater than through air because of the fundamental differences between liquids and gases. Water particles are much more densely packed than air particles. Because the particles are closer together, vibrations are transferred more efficiently from particle to particle in longitudinal waves. When sound waves travel through water, the compressions and rarefactions are transferred through this dense medium with minimal energy loss. In air, particles are much more spread out, so vibrations are less efficiently transferred, and sound energy dissipates more quickly over distance. Additionally, liquids do not have the same turbulence and convection currents that gases have, which can scatter and absorb sound. This is why whales can communicate across ocean distances of many miles, while the same frequency sounds in air would only travel a few hundred meters. The properties of the medium directly determine how far and how clearly sound can propagate.
