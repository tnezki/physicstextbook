SECTION 5.2

Transverse Waves

Discover how energy travels through space and matter while particles vibrate perpendicular to the direction of motion. From ocean waves crashing on beaches to light waves traveling through the universe, transverse waves are fundamental to understanding the physical world around us.

Learning Objectives

  • I can explain the difference between mechanical and electromagnetic waves and describe how energy transfer occurs in transverse waves.
  • I can identify and define the key characteristics of waves including amplitude, wavelength, frequency, period, and velocity.
  • I can calculate wave properties using the wave equation and frequency-period relationships.
  • I can apply wave concepts to real-world phenomena and explain how waves transmit energy without permanently displacing the medium.

Introduction

Hook: The Power of Waves

Imagine standing at the ocean shoreline during a massive storm. Waves tower above you, crashing with tremendous force that can move boulders and reshape coastlines. Yet if you watch a leaf floating on the water's surface, you'll notice something remarkable: as the waves pass by, the leaf bobs up and down but doesn't travel toward the shore with the waves. The energy in the wave moves across the ocean, but the water itself—and everything floating on it—only vibrates in place.

Real-World Relevance

Understanding transverse waves is essential for explaining countless phenomena in daily life. When you pluck a guitar string, you create transverse waves that produce sound. Light from the sun reaches Earth as transverse electromagnetic waves. The seismic waves from earthquakes are partially transverse, helping geologists study Earth's interior. Even the radio waves that carry music to your car are transverse waves. Medical ultrasound uses waves similar to those in the ocean to image babies and detect tumors. Mastering wave concepts opens doors to understanding energy transfer in the universe.

Historical Context

The study of waves has fascinated scientists for centuries. Christiaan Huygens, a Dutch mathematician and physicist in the 1600s, proposed that light travels as waves—a revolutionary idea at the time. His wave theory explained phenomena like refraction and diffraction that could not be explained by the competing particle theory. Later, Augustin-Jean Fresnel provided mathematical descriptions of wave behavior in the early 1800s, establishing the field of wave optics. Today, the wave equation discovered by these pioneers remains one of the most important equations in all of physics.

Connection to Prior Knowledge

You've already learned about motion, velocity, and energy. In this section, you'll apply those concepts to understand how waves transmit energy through different materials. Just as moving objects have kinetic energy and changing their speed requires force and acceleration, waves carry energy and change their properties based on the medium they travel through. The mathematical tools you've developed for analyzing motion will help you calculate wave properties with precision.

Understanding Waves and Energy Transfer

Types of Waves: Mechanical vs. Electromagnetic

Mechanical waves require a material medium (such as water, air, rope, or Earth material) to travel. When you create a wave in a bathtub, the water acts as the medium. When sound travels from a speaker to your ear, air is the medium. Without a medium, mechanical waves cannot exist.

Electromagnetic waves, on the other hand, do not require a material medium. Light, radio waves, X-rays, and microwaves are all electromagnetic waves that can travel through the vacuum of space. This is why we can see light from distant stars even though space contains almost no air.

This section focuses on mechanical waves, specifically a type called transverse waves. In all mechanical waves, energy moves from one location to another, but here's the key insight: the medium itself only vibrates back and forth in place and does not travel with the wave.

What are Transverse Waves?

In a transverse wave, the movement of the medium is perpendicular (at right angles) to the direction that energy travels. Imagine a person holding one end of a rope while the other end is tied to a tree. When the person jerks their hand up and down, they create waves that travel horizontally down the rope toward the tree. The rope itself moves up and down (perpendicular to the direction of wave travel). This is the defining characteristic of a transverse wave: medium motion is perpendicular to energy motion.

Testing with Tape

Here's a classic experiment to prove that the medium doesn't travel with the wave: stick a small piece of tape on a rope that's creating waves. Watch what happens as waves pass the tape. The tape bobs up and down but doesn't slide along the rope toward the tree. The energy travels down the rope, but the rope (and the tape) stays in the same horizontal location. This demonstrates that wave energy and medium displacement are different things.

Key Wave Characteristics

Scientists describe waves using five key measurements. Understanding these quantities allows you to completely characterize any transverse wave.

Amplitude

The amplitude of a wave is the maximum displacement of the medium from its undisturbed (equilibrium) position. Think of it as how far above or below the resting position the wave goes. For a rope wave, if the rope normally lies at position zero, and the highest point of the wave reaches 5 cm above that position, then the amplitude is 5 cm. A larger amplitude means more violent wave motion and more energy in the wave.

Wavelength

The wavelength ($\lambda$, the Greek letter lambda) is the distance between two consecutive points that are in the same state of vibration. You can measure wavelength from crest to crest (peak to peak), from trough to trough (valley to valley), or from any point on one wave to the identical point on the next wave. For ocean waves, wavelength might be 15 meters—meaning every 15 meters along the ocean, you find another crest. Shorter wavelengths mean more waves packed into the same distance.

Period

The period ($T$) is the time required for one complete wave to pass a given point. Imagine standing on a beach and timing how long it takes for one complete wave cycle: from the moment a crest passes you until the next crest reaches you. If that time is 10 seconds, the period is 10 seconds. Period is measured in seconds (s).

Frequency

The frequency ($f$) is the number of complete waves that pass a point in one second. If 5 waves pass your position in one second, the frequency is 5 waves per second. The unit of frequency is the Hertz (Hz), which means "waves per second" or simply $\text{s}^{-1}$. Frequency and period are reciprocals of each other:

$$f = \frac{1}{T} \quad \text{and} \quad T = \frac{1}{f}$$

If a wave has a period of 0.5 seconds, its frequency is $f = 1/0.5 = 2$ Hz, meaning 2 complete waves pass any point each second.

Wave Velocity

The wave velocity (sometimes called wave speed) is how fast the wave pattern travels through the medium. This is different from the velocity of the medium itself. The water particles in an ocean wave move up and down but don't travel with the wave. However, the wave pattern (the crests and troughs) moves across the ocean at a definite speed.

To find wave velocity, consider that in one complete period ($T$), one entire wavelength ($\lambda$) passes a given point. Since velocity equals distance divided by time:

$$v = \frac{\lambda}{T}$$

We can also express this using frequency, since $f = 1/T$:

$$v = \lambda f$$

This is the fundamental wave equation. It tells us that wave velocity equals wavelength times frequency. Importantly, for waves in a given medium, the velocity is constant. If you increase the frequency, the wavelength must decrease proportionally to keep velocity constant.

Common Misconception

Wrong: "Waves with higher frequency travel faster."

Correct: In a given medium, all waves travel at the same speed regardless of frequency. Higher frequency waves simply have shorter wavelengths. The product $\lambda f$ always equals the constant wave velocity for that medium.

Example Problems

Example 1: Finding Period from Frequency

Given: A wave has a frequency of 262 Hz (the frequency of the musical note Middle C)

Find: What is the period of this wave?

Solution:

Step 1: Identify the relationship between frequency and period.

The period and frequency are reciprocals, so $T = \frac{1}{f}$

Step 2: Substitute the given frequency value.

$$T = \frac{1}{f} = \frac{1}{262 \text{ Hz}}$$

Step 3: Calculate and simplify.

$$T = 0.00382 \text{ s} = 3.82 \times 10^{-3} \text{ s}$$

Answer: The period is 0.00382 s or 3.82 milliseconds. This means it takes about 3.8 thousandths of a second for one complete wave vibration.

Why this matters: Middle C vibrates very quickly—more than 260 times per second! Our ears perceive this rapid vibration as a specific musical pitch. Higher frequencies (like high notes) have shorter periods and vibrate even faster.

Example 2: Calculating Wave Velocity

Given: A water wave has a frequency of 0.5 Hz and a wavelength of 4.0 m

Find: What is the velocity of the wave?

Solution:

Step 1: Identify which wave equation to use.

Since we know frequency and wavelength, use: $v = \lambda f$

Step 2: Substitute the given values with proper units.

$$v = \lambda f = (4.0 \text{ m})(0.5 \text{ Hz})$$

Step 3: Calculate the result.

$$v = 2.0 \text{ m/s}$$

Answer: The wave velocity is 2.0 m/s. The wave pattern moves across the water surface at 2 meters every second.

Physical interpretation: This is a slow ocean wave (typical gentle waves move 1-3 m/s). The frequency is low (only half a wave per second), but each wave is long (4 meters), so the overall wave speed is moderate.

Example 3: Multi-Step Problem - Finding All Wave Properties

Given: A transverse wave traveling along a string has a wavelength of 0.60 m and a period of 0.40 s

Find: (a) frequency, (b) velocity, and (c) the amplitude if the maximum displacement is 0.08 m

Solution:

Part (a): Find the frequency

Step 1: Use the reciprocal relationship between period and frequency.

$$f = \frac{1}{T} = \frac{1}{0.40 \text{ s}}$$

Step 2: Calculate frequency.

$$f = 2.5 \text{ Hz}$$

Part (b): Find the velocity

Step 1: Use the wave equation with the known wavelength and period.

$$v = \frac{\lambda}{T} = \frac{0.60 \text{ m}}{0.40 \text{ s}}$$

Step 2: Calculate velocity.

$$v = 1.5 \text{ m/s}$$

Step 3: Verify using the alternative wave equation.

$$v = \lambda f = (0.60 \text{ m})(2.5 \text{ Hz}) = 1.5 \text{ m/s}$$ ✓

Part (c): Identify the amplitude

Step 1: Recall that amplitude is the maximum displacement from equilibrium.

The problem states the maximum displacement is 0.08 m, so the amplitude is 0.08 m.

Answer:
(a) Frequency = 2.5 Hz
(b) Velocity = 1.5 m/s
(c) Amplitude = 0.08 m or 8.0 cm

Note on verification: A good problem-solving strategy is to verify your answer using different approaches. Both methods of calculating velocity ($v = \lambda/T$ and $v = \lambda f$) gave the same answer, which confirms our calculations are correct.

Summary

  • Mechanical waves require a material medium to propagate; electromagnetic waves do not.
  • In a transverse wave, the medium vibrates perpendicular to the direction of energy transfer.
  • The medium does not travel with the wave; it only oscillates in place, as demonstrated by the tape experiment.
  • Amplitude is the maximum displacement of the medium from its equilibrium position and indicates wave energy.
  • Wavelength ($\lambda$) is the distance between consecutive identical points on adjacent waves, measured in meters.
  • Period ($T$) is the time for one complete wave cycle, measured in seconds.
  • Frequency ($f$) is the number of waves per second, measured in Hertz (Hz), and equals the reciprocal of period: $f = 1/T$.
  • The wave equation $v = \lambda f$ (or $v = \lambda/T$) relates wave speed, wavelength, and frequency; in a given medium, wave speed is constant.
  • Wave velocity describes energy transfer speed; it is different from the velocity of the medium particles.
  • Understanding wave properties is essential for explaining sound, light, seismic waves, and countless other physical phenomena.

Answer Key for Instructors

Conceptual Questions

Question 1: Why doesn't the tape move toward the fixed end?

Answer: The tape does not move toward the fixed end because in a transverse wave, the medium (rope) only vibrates perpendicular to the direction of wave travel. While the wave pattern (crests and troughs) propagates along the rope, the actual rope particles—including the tape—only move up and down. After the wave passes, the tape returns to its original position along the length of the rope. The wave energy travels down the rope, but the rope itself does not.

Question 2: Difference between wave velocity and particle velocity.

Answer: Wave velocity is how fast the wave pattern (crests and troughs) moves through the medium. Particle velocity is how fast the medium particles actually move as they vibrate up and down. For example, on the ocean, the wave pattern may travel at 5 m/s, but a water particle bobs up and down with a different velocity. The wave velocity is constant for a given medium, while particle velocity changes continuously as particles oscillate back and forth.

Question 3: Period of 8 seconds and waves in one minute.

Answer: A period of 8 seconds means one complete wave takes 8 seconds to pass any point. Therefore, the frequency is $f = 1/T = 1/8 = 0.125$ Hz. In one minute (60 seconds), the number of waves is: $(0.125 \text{ Hz})(60 \text{ s}) = 7.5$ waves, or approximately 8 waves reach the shore each minute.

Question 4: Which wave has longer wavelength?

Answer: Wave A (10 Hz) has the longer wavelength. From the wave equation $v = \lambda f$, we can rearrange to get $\lambda = v/f$. Since both waves travel through the same medium, $v$ is the same for both. Because Wave B has twice the frequency of Wave A, and wavelength is inversely proportional to frequency, Wave B must have half the wavelength of Wave A. Therefore, Wave A has the longer wavelength.

Calculation Problems

Question 5: Period and wave cycles

Given: $f = 5.0$ Hz

(a) Find the period:

$$T = \frac{1}{f} = \frac{1}{5.0 \text{ Hz}} = \boxed{0.20 \text{ s}}$$

(b) Number of cycles in 2.0 seconds:

$$\text{Number of cycles} = f \times t = (5.0 \text{ Hz})(2.0 \text{ s}) = \boxed{10 \text{ cycles}}$$

Question 6: Wave velocity

Given: $\lambda = 2.5$ m, $T = 0.50$ s

Solution:

$$v = \frac{\lambda}{T} = \frac{2.5 \text{ m}}{0.50 \text{ s}} = \boxed{5.0 \text{ m/s}}$$

Multi-Step Problems

Question 7: Sound wave properties in air and water

Given: $f = 440$ Hz, $\lambda_{\text{air}} = 0.78$ m, $v_{\text{water}} = 1480$ m/s

(a) Period in air:

$$T = \frac{1}{f} = \frac{1}{440 \text{ Hz}} = \boxed{2.27 \times 10^{-3} \text{ s or 2.27 ms}}$$

(b) Velocity in air:

$$v = \lambda f = (0.78 \text{ m})(440 \text{ Hz}) = \boxed{343.2 \text{ m/s} \approx 343 \text{ m/s}}$$

(c) Wavelength in water:

Since frequency remains constant when sound enters a different medium:

$$\lambda_{\text{water}} = \frac{v_{\text{water}}}{f} = \frac{1480 \text{ m/s}}{440 \text{ Hz}} = \boxed{3.36 \text{ m}}$$

The wavelength increases in water because sound travels faster there, while frequency stays constant.

Question 8: Complete wave analysis

Given: $A = 0.12$ m, $v = 2.0$ m/s, 4.0 cycles in 3.0 seconds

(a) Frequency:

$$f = \frac{\text{number of cycles}}{\text{time}} = \frac{4.0 \text{ cycles}}{3.0 \text{ s}} = \boxed{\frac{4}{3} \text{ Hz} \approx 1.33 \text{ Hz}}$$

(b) Period:

$$T = \frac{1}{f} = \frac{1}{4/3} = \boxed{\frac{3}{4} \text{ s} = 0.75 \text{ s}}$$

(c) Wavelength:

$$\lambda = \frac{v}{f} = \frac{2.0 \text{ m/s}}{4/3 \text{ Hz}} = 2.0 \times \frac{3}{4} = \boxed{1.5 \text{ m}}$$

(d) Why amplitude doesn't appear in calculations:

Amplitude does not affect frequency, period, or wavelength because these properties depend only on how the wave is created and the properties of the medium. Amplitude only describes how much the medium is displaced (the "intensity" of the wave). The wave equation $v = \lambda f$ is independent of amplitude. A louder sound has greater amplitude but the same frequency and wavelength as a quieter sound of the same pitch.
