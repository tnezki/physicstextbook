5.2 Transverse Waves

Understanding the Fundamental Properties of Wave Motion

Algebra-Based Physics

In this section, you will explore how waves transfer energy through matter without permanently displacing the medium. From ocean waves at legendary surf breaks to vibrating guitar strings, transverse waves are everywhere. You'll learn to quantify these waves using frequency, wavelength, and velocity—essential tools for understanding everything from light to sound.

Learning Objectives

Introduction: Waves All Around Us

Have you ever dropped a pebble into a pond and watched the ripples expand outward? Or felt a vibration travel through the ground during an earthquake? These are examples of waves—one of the most important phenomena in physics. Waves transfer energy from one place to another, but they do something remarkable: the medium carrying the wave (water, rope, earth) doesn't travel with the energy. Instead, it vibrates back and forth in place.

Consider a surfer riding a giant wave at "Jaws" in Maui. The ocean water itself doesn't travel across the Pacific Ocean—if it did, all the water would pile up on beaches! Instead, the wave's energy propagates forward while individual water molecules bob up and down. This is the essence of a transverse wave: energy moves in one direction while the medium vibrates perpendicular to that direction.

In this section, you'll learn the language of waves—terms like frequency, wavelength, and amplitude—and discover the mathematical relationships that govern all wave motion. These concepts apply to the waves you can see and feel, as well as invisible waves like light and radio signals.

Types of Waves: Mechanical vs. Electromagnetic

Mechanical waves require a material medium (such as water, air, or rope) to travel. Examples include water waves, sound waves, and waves on a vibrating string. In contrast, electromagnetic waves like light can travel through a vacuum without any medium.

In this section, we focus on mechanical waves. The key property of all mechanical waves is that energy moves from place to place while the medium only vibrates back and forth. This is a fundamental distinction: the wave propagates; the medium oscillates.

Understanding Transverse Waves

A transverse wave is a mechanical wave where the particles of the medium vibrate perpendicular to the direction the wave travels. Imagine a boy jerking a rope up and down while the other end is tied to a tree. The energy he puts in travels down the rope toward the tree, but the rope itself only moves up and down. A piece of tape stuck to the rope would bob vertically but wouldn't move horizontally—it stays in the same place.

Key Insight: In transverse waves, the direction of particle motion ⊥ direction of wave motion. The medium vibrates, but the wave propagates.

Characteristics of Transverse Waves

Every transverse wave has distinct features we use to describe and measure it:

  • Undisturbed position: The center line where the medium would be with no wave present (equilibrium).
  • Crest: The highest point of displacement above the undisturbed position.
  • Trough: The lowest point of displacement below the undisturbed position.
  • Amplitude ($A$): The maximum displacement from equilibrium—the distance from the center line to the crest (or trough).
  • Wavelength ($\lambda$): The distance between equivalent points on successive waves (crest to crest, or trough to trough).

Frequency, Period, and Wave Velocity

To fully describe a wave, we need to know how often it oscillates and how fast it travels.

Period and Frequency

Period ($T$) is the time it takes for one complete wave cycle to pass a given point. During one period, one complete wavelength passes the observer. Period is measured in seconds (s).

Frequency ($f$) is the number of complete waves passing a point per second. If many waves pass per second, the frequency is high; if few waves pass, the frequency is low. Frequency is measured in hertz (Hz) or $\text{s}^{-1}$, where 1 Hz = 1 wave per second.

Period and frequency are reciprocals of each other:

$$T = \frac{1}{f} \quad \text{or} \quad f = \frac{1}{T}$$

Wave Velocity

Wave velocity ($v$) is the speed at which the wave propagates through the medium. This is different from the velocity of the medium itself! In one period $T$, the wave travels one wavelength $\lambda$. Using the definition of velocity as distance divided by time:

$$v = \frac{\lambda}{T}$$

Since $T = 1/f$, we can also write:

$$v = \lambda f$$

This is the wave equation—one of the most important relationships in wave physics. It tells us that wave velocity equals wavelength times frequency.

Real-World Connection: The speed of light in vacuum is approximately $3 \times 10^8$ m/s. Using $v = \lambda f$, we can determine that red light has a longer wavelength than violet light because they have the same speed but different frequencies.

Example Problems

Example 1: Finding Period from Frequency

Problem: A wave has a frequency of 262 Hz. What is the time interval between successive wave crests?

Analysis: The time between successive crests is one period. We're given frequency and need to find period.

Given: $f = 262 \text{ Hz}$     Find: $T = ?$

Step 1: Use the frequency-period relationship: $\displaystyle T = \frac{1}{f}$

Step 2: Substitute the known value:

$$T = \frac{1}{262 \text{ Hz}} = 0.00382 \text{ s} = 3.82 \text{ ms}$$

Answer: The time between successive crests is 0.00382 seconds or 3.82 milliseconds.

Example 2: Finding Wave Velocity

Problem: A wave with a frequency of 262 Hz has a wavelength of 1.29 m. What is the velocity of the wave?

Analysis: We have frequency and wavelength, and need to find velocity. Use the wave equation.

Given: $f = 262 \text{ Hz}$, $\lambda = 1.29 \text{ m}$     Find: $v = ?$

Step 1: Use the wave equation: $v = \lambda f$

Step 2: Substitute values:

$$v = (1.29 \text{ m})(262 \text{ Hz}) = 338 \text{ m/s}$$

Answer: The wave velocity is 338 m/s. (This is approximately the speed of sound in air!)

Example 3: Multi-Step Wave Problem

Problem: A light wave travels through a transparent material with a speed of $2.0 \times 10^8$ m/s. If the wavelength of the light in this material is $5.0 \times 10^{-7}$ m, find: (a) the frequency of the light, and (b) the period of the wave.

Analysis: This problem requires us to use the wave equation and the frequency-period relationship in sequence.

Given: $v = 2.0 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s}$, $\lambda = 5.0 \times 10^{-7} \text{ m}$     Find: (a) $f = ?$   (b) $T = ?$

Step 1: Find frequency using the wave equation. Rearrange $v = \lambda f$ to solve for $f$:

$$f = \frac{v}{\lambda}$$

Step 2: Substitute the known values:

$$f = \frac{2.0 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s}}{5.0 \times 10^{-7} \text{ m}} = 4.0 \times 10^{14} \text{ Hz}$$

Step 3: Find the period using $T = 1/f$:

$$T = \frac{1}{4.0 \times 10^{14} \text{ s}^{-1}} = 2.5 \times 10^{-15} \text{ s}$$

Answers: (a) Frequency = $4.0 \times 10^{14}$ Hz   (b) Period = $2.5 \times 10^{-15}$ s

Historical Note: The study of light as a wave was revolutionary. In the 1800s, scientists like Thomas Young and Augustin-Jean Fresnel demonstrated that light exhibits wave-like properties. This understanding helped establish that light is both a wave and a particle—a concept that remained puzzling until quantum mechanics emerged in the 20th century.

Summary

  • Mechanical waves require a material medium. Electromagnetic waves (light) do not.
  • In a transverse wave, the medium vibrates perpendicular to the direction of wave propagation.
  • The amplitude ($A$) is the maximum displacement from equilibrium (in meters).
  • The wavelength ($\lambda$) is the distance between equivalent points on successive waves (in meters).
  • The period ($T$) is the time for one complete oscillation (in seconds).
  • The frequency ($f$) is the number of oscillations per second (in hertz or s$^{-1}$).
  • Period and frequency are reciprocals: $T = 1/f$ and $f = 1/T$
  • The wave equation: $v = \lambda f$ or $v = \lambda/T$, where $v$ is wave velocity.
  • Wave velocity depends on the medium, not on frequency or wavelength.
  • The medium is not permanently displaced—it returns to its original position after the wave passes.

Relationship Equation Variables

Practice Problems

Conceptual & Vocabulary Questions (1-5)

  • 1. Conceptual: When a guitar string is plucked, in what direction does the wave travel along the string? In what direction does the string itself vibrate? Explain how these two directions differ for a transverse wave.
  • 2. Vocabulary: Define the following terms and explain the relationship between them: period, frequency, and hertz. If a wave has a period of 0.5 seconds, what is its frequency?
  • 3. Conceptual: Consider a floating buoy in the ocean. As waves pass beneath it, does the buoy move toward the shore? Why or why not? What does the buoy's motion tell us about the difference between medium motion and wave propagation?
  • 4. Short Answer: If you increase the frequency of a wave on a rope while keeping the wave speed constant, what must happen to the wavelength? Explain using the wave equation.
  • 5. Short Answer: Two waves travel through the same medium with different frequencies. Will they have different speeds? Why or why not?

  • 6. Find the Period: A radio wave has a frequency of 101.5 MHz (101.5 × 106 Hz). What is the period of this wave?
  • 7. Find the Frequency: An ocean wave has a period of 8.0 seconds. What is the frequency of this wave?
  • 8. Find the Velocity: A sound wave in water has a wavelength of 3.7 m and a frequency of 400 Hz. What is the speed of the sound in this water?

  • 9. Multi-Step: A vibrating tuning fork produces a sound wave with a frequency of 440 Hz. In air at 20°C, sound travels at 343 m/s.
    (a) Calculate the wavelength of the sound wave.
    (b) If the same tuning fork were used underwater where sound travels at 1,480 m/s, what would be the new wavelength? (The frequency remains the same.)
  • 10. Multi-Step: A student observes that waves on a pond have a wavelength of 0.45 m. She counts 12 wave crests passing a particular point in 8.0 seconds.
    (a) Calculate the frequency of the waves.
    (b) Calculate the period.
    (c) Calculate the wave velocity.

    • Answer Key

    Conceptual Questions (1-5)

    1. The wave travels along the length of the string, but the string vibrates up and down (perpendicular). This is the defining characteristic of a transverse wave.

    2. Period = time for one complete cycle (seconds); Frequency = number of cycles per second (hertz); Hertz is the unit of frequency. They are reciprocals: $f = 1/T$. If $T = 0.5$ s, then $f = 2$ Hz.

    3. No, the buoy oscillates up and down but doesn't move toward shore. This shows that energy propagates while the medium oscillates—the water doesn't move toward shore.

    4. The wavelength must decrease. Since $v = \lambda f$ and $v$ is constant, if $f$ increases, then $\lambda$ must decrease proportionally.

    5. No, they will have the same speed if traveling in the same medium. Wave velocity depends only on the medium's properties, not on frequency or wavelength.

    Calculation Problems (6-10)

    6. $T = 1/f = 1/(101.5 \times 10^6 \text{ Hz}) = 9.85 \times 10^{-9} \text{ s} = 9.85 \text{ ns}$

    7. $f = 1/T = 1/(8.0 \text{ s}) = 0.125 \text{ Hz}$

    8. $v = \lambda f = (3.7 \text{ m})(400 \text{ Hz}) = 1,480 \text{ m/s}$

    9. (a) $\lambda = v/f = (343 \text{ m/s})/(440 \text{ Hz}) = 0.78 \text{ m}$
    (b) In water: $\lambda = (1,480 \text{ m/s})/(440 \text{ Hz}) = 3.36 \text{ m}$

    10. (a) 12 crests in 8.0 s means $f = 12/(8.0 \text{ s}) = 1.5 \text{ Hz}$
    (b) $T = 1/f = 1/(1.5 \text{ Hz}) = 0.67 \text{ s}$
    (c) $v = \lambda f = (0.45 \text{ m})(1.5 \text{ Hz}) = 0.68 \text{ m/s}$
