SECTION 6.5 – Lenses: Double Convex and Double Concave

Explore Notes

Opening

Lenses bend light through refraction instead of reflection. Like mirrors, different lens shapes produce different types of images. Understanding lens behavior allows us to predict image location and size.

Objectives

  • I can identify the properties of converging and diverging lenses
  • I can use the lens equation to calculate image location and size
  • I can determine whether a lens produces a real or virtual image
  • I can apply magnification to determine image height

Introduction

A double convex lens is thicker in the middle and thinner at the edges. It converges parallel light rays to a focal point. A double concave lens is thinner in the middle and thicker at the edges. It diverges parallel light rays.

Real-World

Converging lenses are used in cameras, microscopes, and the human eye. Diverging lenses are used in eyeglasses for nearsightedness.

Historical

The study of lenses advanced during the development of telescopes and microscopes in the 1600s. Understanding refraction allowed scientists to design optical instruments that expanded human observation.

Vocabulary

Double Convex Lenses: Lenses that are thicker in the middle and thinner at the edges; also called converging lenses because they bend light rays toward each other.

Converging Lenses: Lenses that bend light rays toward each other, causing them to converge at a focal point; have positive focal length.

Double Concave Lenses: Lenses that are thinner in the middle and thicker at the edges; also called diverging lenses because they bend light rays away from each other.

Diverging Lenses: Lenses that bend light rays away from each other, causing them to diverge; have negative focal length and always produce virtual images.

Formula

Lens Equation:

$$\frac{1}{f} = \frac{1}{d_o} + \frac{1}{d_i}$$

Magnification (Lenses):

$$M = \frac{d_i}{d_o} = \frac{h_i}{h_o}$$

Core Strategies

1. Identify the Lens Type

Converging lenses have positive focal length. Diverging lenses have negative focal length.

2. Apply the Lens Equation Carefully

Use the lens equation to determine image distance: $$\frac{1}{f} = \frac{1}{d_o} + \frac{1}{d_i}$$

3. Use Magnification to Determine Image Size and Orientation

Magnification is: $$M = \frac{d_i}{d_o} = \frac{h_i}{h_o}$$ A negative magnification indicates an inverted image.

Summary of Key Concepts

  • Converging lenses have positive focal length and can produce real or virtual images.
  • Diverging lenses have negative focal length and always produce virtual images.
  • The lens equation relates focal length, object distance, and image distance.
  • Magnification determines image size and orientation.
  • Real images have positive image distance and can be projected.
  • Virtual images have negative image distance and cannot be projected.

Common Mistakes

  • Forgetting that diverging lenses have negative focal length.
  • Using incorrect sign conventions for image distance.
  • Confusing mirror and lens magnification formulas.
  • Failing to check whether the image is real or virtual after solving.
  • Incorrectly interpreting magnification sign.
  • Using inconsistent units for distances.

1–6 Vocabulary

1. A converging lens bends light rays toward each other and has positive focal length.

2. A diverging lens bends light rays away from each other and has negative focal length.

3. Positive focal length indicates a converging lens.

4. Negative focal length indicates a diverging lens.

5. $$\frac{1}{f} = \frac{1}{d_o} + \frac{1}{d_i}$$

6. Magnification represents the ratio of image height to object height and indicates image orientation.
