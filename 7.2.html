7.2 Electric Fields and Voltage

You do not have to touch a charged object to feel its effect. Electric forces can act across space, and that invisible influence is described by an electric field.
This section builds from force to field and then to voltage, helping students connect diagrams, equations, and energy ideas into a single picture of electric interaction.
“The important thing is to never stop questioning.” — Albert Einstein
Why does this matter?

Electric fields and voltage help explain how charges move in everything from thunderstorms to batteries and circuits. These ideas are central to modern electronics, medical equipment, communication systems, and power distribution.

Learning Targets

Students will analyze electric fields and relate voltage to force, energy, charge, and distance.

  • I can interpret electric field lines and describe the direction of the field around positive and negative charges.
  • I can calculate electric field strength using $E = \frac{F}{q}$ or $E = \frac{V}{d}$ in a uniform field.
  • I can use $F = qE$ to determine the force on a charged particle in an electric field.
  • I can explain voltage as energy per charge and connect voltage to work done on a charge.

Introduction

When a charged comb attracts small bits of paper, the paper responds before it ever touches the comb. Something about the space around the comb is causing the effect. Physicists describe that region of influence as an electric field.

An electric field tells us how a positive test charge would be affected at a point in space. Instead of focusing only on one pair of charges at a time, the field model lets us describe what is happening all around a charged object.

Voltage adds another layer to this picture. If electric field describes how charges are pushed or pulled, voltage describes how energy changes as a charge moves from one location to another. In this section, we connect force, field, and energy into one framework.

Key Idea: Electric field describes force per charge, and voltage describes energy per charge. Both ideas help explain how charges interact and move.

History

In the early study of electricity, scientists described electric interactions mainly in terms of forces between charged objects. That was useful, but it did not fully explain how space around a charge could influence other charges.

Michael Faraday introduced the idea of a field in the 1800s. He imagined the space around a charged object as physically important, not empty. His field-line pictures gave scientists a visual way to understand how electric influence spreads through space.

Later, James Clerk Maxwell developed mathematical equations that described electric and magnetic fields more completely. Faraday’s visual model and Maxwell’s mathematics together created the foundation of modern electromagnetism.

The idea of electric potential also became essential because it connected electric interactions to energy. Today, voltage is one of the most practical ways scientists and engineers describe how electricity works in devices and circuits.

Key Idea: Electric field lines show the direction of force on a positive test charge and become denser where the field is stronger.

Key Concepts

Electric Field

An electric field is the region around a charged object where other charges experience an electric force. Instead of describing force between just two charges, the field model describes how a charge influences the space around it.

$$E = \frac{F_e}{q}$$

$E$ = electric field $\left(\text{N/C}\right)$
$F_e$ = electric force $\left(\text{N}\right)$
$q$ = charge $\left(\text{C}\right)$

A stronger electric field means a larger force would act on the same charge.

Electric Field Lines

Electric field lines provide a visual model of electric fields. They show both the direction and relative strength of the field.

  • Field lines point away from positive charges and toward negative charges.
  • The direction of the field is the direction a positive test charge would move.
  • Closer lines indicate a stronger electric field.
  • Field lines never cross.

Force in an Electric Field

Once the electric field is known, the force on any charge placed in that field can be found.

$$F_e = qE$$

A positive charge experiences force in the direction of the field, while a negative charge experiences force in the opposite direction.

Uniform Electric Field

A uniform electric field has the same strength and direction everywhere. This often occurs between two large, parallel charged plates.

$$E = \frac{V}{d}$$

$E$ = electric field $\left(\text{N/C or V/m}\right)$
$V$ = voltage $\left(\text{V}\right)$
$d$ = distance $\left(\text{m}\right)$

In a uniform field, field lines are parallel and evenly spaced.

Electric Potential Energy

Electric potential energy is the energy a charge has because of its position in an electric field. Just like an object held above the ground has gravitational potential energy, a charge placed in an electric field has electric potential energy.

The amount of electric potential energy depends on both the size of the charge and where it is located in the field. Regions closer to strong charges or regions with higher electric potential correspond to higher or lower energy states depending on the situation.

Moving a charge against the electric field requires work and increases its electric potential energy. Moving a charge with the electric field allows the field to do work, decreasing its potential energy.

Key Idea: Electric potential energy depends on both the charge and its position in the field. Changes in position within the field result in energy transfer.

This idea is important because it connects directly to voltage. Voltage tells how much electric potential energy changes per unit charge, which allows us to predict how charges will move and how energy is transferred in circuits.

Voltage - Electric Potential

Voltage describes how much electric potential energy changes per unit charge.

$$V = \frac{U_e}{q}$$

$V$ = voltage $\left(\text{V}\right)$
$U_e$ = electric potential energy $\left(\text{J}\right)$
$q$ = charge $\left(\text{C}\right)$

A higher voltage means more energy is transferred for each coulomb of charge.

Work and Voltage

When a charge moves through a potential difference, work is done on the charge.

$$W = qV$$

$W$ = work $\left(\text{J}\right)$
$q$ = charge $\left(\text{C}\right)$
$V$ = voltage $\left(\text{V}\right)$

This equation connects energy transfer directly to voltage and charge.

Dipoles and Field Patterns

A dipole consists of two equal and opposite charges separated by a distance. The electric field pattern around a dipole is more complex than the field around a single charge.

Field lines begin on the positive charge and end on the negative charge, forming curved patterns that represent how electric influence spreads through space.

Key Idea: Field diagrams are models that help visualize invisible electric interactions.

Worked Derivation

The definition of electric field starts from the idea that field strength tells how much force would act on each unit of charge. That means:

$$E = \frac{F_e}{q}$$

If we want to solve for force instead, multiply both sides by $q$:

$$qE = F_e$$

So we get: $$F_e = qE$$

This algebra shows that electric field and force are closely connected. Field describes the environment. Force describes what happens when a particular charge is placed in that environment.

Why this matters: Students should see $E = \frac{F_e}{q}$ and $F_e = qE$ as the same relationship written for different purposes.

Vocabulary

Electric field:

The region around a charged object where electric force is exerted on other charges.

Electric field lines:

Lines that show the direction and relative strength of an electric field.

Dipole:

A pair of equal and opposite charges separated by a distance.

Uniform electric field:

An electric field with the same strength and direction at every point.

Electric potential energy:

Energy stored due to the position of a charge in an electric field.

Voltage:

The electric potential difference per unit charge; energy per coulomb.

Volt (V):

The SI unit of voltage; $1\,\text{V} = 1\,\text{J/C}$.

Potential difference:

The difference in electric potential between two points.

  3. Equation: Choose the equation that matches the relationship in the problem.
  4. Solve: Substitute carefully and isolate the variable if needed.
  5. Check Units: Confirm the answer has the correct physics unit.

Diagram Tips

  • Use arrows to show field direction.
  • Remember field lines point away from positive and toward negative.
  • For a negative charge, force is opposite the field direction.
  • Sketching the situation first often prevents sign mistakes.

Unit Analysis Tips

  • $\text{N/C}$ and $\text{V/m}$ are equivalent units for electric field.
  • $1\,\text{V} = 1\,\text{J/C}$ helps connect energy and voltage.
  • Use meters, not centimeters, in $E = \frac{V}{d}$.
Key Idea: In field and voltage problems, units often reveal which equation should be used.

Common Mistakes

  • Confusing force and field: Force depends on the specific charge present; field describes the location itself.
  • Ignoring charge sign: A negative charge feels force opposite the field direction.
  • Mixing up voltage and electric field: Voltage is energy per charge; electric field is force per charge.
  • Unit mismatch: Distances must be in meters for $E = \frac{V}{d}$.
  • Using the wrong equation: Students often use $W = qV$ when the problem is asking for field, not work.
  • Treating field lines as motion paths: Field lines represent direction of force on a positive test charge, not necessarily the actual path a particle follows.

Summary

  • Electric field describes force per unit charge.
  • Field lines point away from positive charges and toward negative charges.
  • Electric force can be found from field using $F_e = qE$.
  • Voltage describes energy per unit charge.
  • Work and voltage are related by $W = qV$.
  • In a uniform field, $E = \frac{V}{d}$ connects field strength, voltage, and distance.

Calculation

5.

$$E = \frac{F}{q} = \frac{0.40}{2.0 \times 10^{-6}} = 2.0 \times 10^5\,\text{N/C}$$

Answer: $\boxed{2.0 \times 10^5\,\text{N/C}}$

6.

Original field: $$E = \frac{V}{d} = \frac{60}{0.30} = 200\,\text{N/C}$$ New distance is half → field doubles: $$E = \frac{60}{0.15} = 400\,\text{N/C}$$

Answer: $\boxed{400\,\text{N/C}}$

Analyze & Explain

7. The statement is incorrect because electric field and voltage describe different relationships.

Electric field is force per charge: $$E = \frac{F}{q}$$

Voltage is energy per charge: $$V = \frac{U}{q}$$

Both involve charge, but one describes force and the other describes energy.

Detailed Example Solutions

Example 1 — Electric Field

Question: A force of $0.60\,\text{N}$ acts on a charge of $3.0 \times 10^{-6}\,\text{C}$. What is the electric field strength?

$$E = \frac{F}{q} = \frac{0.60}{3.0 \times 10^{-6}} = 2.0 \times 10^5\,\text{N/C}$$

Answer: $\boxed{2.0 \times 10^5\,\text{N/C}}$

Example 2 — Force from Field

Question: What force acts on a charge of $2.0 \times 10^{-6}\,\text{C}$ in an electric field of $4.0 \times 10^4\,\text{N/C}$?

$$F = qE = (2.0 \times 10^{-6})(4.0 \times 10^4) = 0.080\,\text{N}$$

Answer: $\boxed{0.080\,\text{N}}$

Example 3 — Voltage

Question: A charge of $4.0\,\text{C}$ has $20\,\text{J}$ of electric potential energy. What is the voltage?

$$V = \frac{U}{q} = \frac{20}{4.0} = 5.0\,\text{V}$$

Answer: $\boxed{5.0\,\text{V}}$

Example 4 — Work

Question: How much work is done when a charge of $3.0\,\text{C}$ moves through a potential difference of $8.0\,\text{V}$?

$$W = qV = (3.0)(8.0) = 24\,\text{J}$$

Answer: $\boxed{24\,\text{J}}$

Example 5 — Uniform Electric Field

Question: A potential difference of $15\,\text{V}$ exists across plates separated by $0.050\,\text{m}$. What is the electric field strength?

$$E = \frac{V}{d} = \frac{15}{0.050} = 300\,\text{N/C}$$

Answer: $\boxed{300\,\text{N/C}}$

Example 6 — Multi-Step Scenario

(a) $E = \frac{V}{d} = \frac{100}{0.20} = 500\,\text{N/C}$

(b) $F = qE = (2.0 \times 10^{-6})(500) = 1.0 \times 10^{-3}\,\text{N}$

Answer: $\boxed{500\,\text{N/C},\; 1.0 \times 10^{-3}\,\text{N}}$

Example 7 — Multi-Step Scenario

(a) $q = \frac{W}{V} = \frac{36}{12} = 3.0\,\text{C}$

(b) $W = qV = (3.0)(6.0) = 18\,\text{J}$

Answer: $\boxed{3.0\,\text{C},\; 18\,\text{J}}$
