Conceptual Questions  |  Section 7.2
Teacher copy — do not distribute to students. Accept equivalent reasoning.
1.
The field line shows the direction of force a positive test charge would feel at that point. A positive test charge would be pushed in the direction the field line points (away from positive sources, toward negative sources).
2.
Same magnitude — both have the same magnitude of charge (same $|q|$), and $F = qE$, so the force magnitude is equal. Different direction — the proton is pushed with the field; the electron is pushed opposite the field direction. (The sign of $q$ flips the direction of force.)
3.
The force doubles. From $F = qE$, if $E$ doubles and $q$ stays the same, then $F$ is twice as large. The relationship is directly proportional.
4.
Work must be done against the electric force, which acts on the positive charge in the direction of decreasing voltage. Moving the charge "uphill" in voltage increases its electric potential energy, just as lifting an object increases gravitational PE. Energy must be supplied from outside.
5.
Zero work is done. From $W = qV$, the potential difference (voltage) between the two points is zero ($\Delta V = 0$), so no energy change occurs and the electric field does no work on the charge.

✍ Answer Key — Station 5
Analysis & Reasoning (DoK 3)  |  Section 7.2
Teacher copy — accept equivalent reasoning. Award full credit for complete justification.
1.
Disagree. The electric field describes force per unit charge ($E = F/q$). Doubling the charge on a test particle doubles the force felt by that particle, but the field itself (defined as force divided by charge) remains the same at that location in space. The field is a property of the source charges and the surrounding space — not of the test charge placed there. A larger test charge does not create a stronger field; it simply experiences a larger force.
2.
Region A has a stronger field (lines closer together → denser spacing = greater field strength). The same charge placed in Region A would experience a larger force ($F = qE$), so it would accelerate faster. In Region B, the force is smaller, producing smaller acceleration. Over time, the particle in A would gain speed more rapidly. Both particles move in the direction of the field (if positive) or opposite (if negative), but particle A speeds up much faster.
3.
Decrease the separation distance between the plates. From $E = V/d$, if voltage stays the same and $d$ decreases, $E$ increases — halving the distance doubles the field. The force on a charged particle between the plates would also double ($F = qE$), since the charge is unchanged. The particle would accelerate at twice the original rate in the stronger field.

Mixed Review  |  Section 7.2
Teacher copy — do not distribute to students.
Part A — Vocabulary
1.
Parallel (straight) and equally spaced from each other. (Accept: uniform, evenly spaced, same distance apart.)
Part B — Conceptual
2.
The negative charge will move opposite the direction of the electric field. The electric field is defined by the force on a positive test charge, so field lines show where positive charges would go. Because $F = qE$ and a negative charge has a negative $q$, the force is in the direction opposite the field. The particle accelerates opposite to the field lines.
Part C — Single Formula
3.
$V = \dfrac{U}{q} = \dfrac{48}{6.0} = 8.0\,\text{V}$
Part D — Multi-Step
4a.
$E = \dfrac{V}{d} = \dfrac{100}{0.025} = 4000\,\text{N/C}$
4b.
$F = qE = (1.5 \times 10^{-6})(4000) = 6.0 \times 10^{-3}\,\text{N}$
Part E — Analysis
5.
Electric field and voltage are not the same. Electric field ($E = F/q$) is force per unit charge and describes how strongly and in what direction a charge is pushed at a point in space. Voltage ($V = U/q$) is energy per unit charge and describes the electric potential energy change as a charge moves between two points. They are related by $E = V/d$ in a uniform field, but they have different units (N/C vs. V = J/C) and represent different physical quantities.
