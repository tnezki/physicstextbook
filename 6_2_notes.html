SECTION 6.2 – Refraction and Snell's Law

Explore Notes

Opening

When light passes from one material into another, it changes speed. This change in speed causes the light to bend. This bending of light is called refraction.

Objectives

  • I can explain why light bends when it passes between materials with different optical densities
  • I can use Snell's Law to calculate angles of refraction
  • I can predict the direction light will bend based on refractive index values
  • I can describe real-world examples of refraction

Introduction

Refraction occurs when light passes from one medium to another and changes speed. The change in speed causes the direction of the light ray to change.

Real-World

Refraction explains why objects underwater appear bent, how lenses focus light, and how fiber optic cables transmit signals.

Historical

Snell's Law was developed in the 1600s and became fundamental in the study of optics. It allowed scientists to design lenses and optical instruments.

Vocabulary

Refraction: The bending of light when it passes from one material to another.

Refractive Index (n): A measure of how much light slows down in a material compared to vacuum, defined as $n = \frac{c}{v}$.

Optical Density: A measure of how strongly a material slows down light; materials with higher refractive index are more optically dense.

Angle of Refraction: The angle between the refracted ray and the normal.

Snell's Law: The relationship $n_1 \sin \theta_1 = n_2 \sin \theta_2$ that describes how light bends between two materials.

Formula

Refractive Index:

$$n = \frac{c}{v}$$

Where:

  • $n$ = refractive index
  • $c$ = speed of light in vacuum
  • $v$ = speed of light in the material

Snell's Law:

$$n_1 \sin \theta_1 = n_2 \sin \theta_2$$

Where:

  • $n_1$ = refractive index of first medium
  • $n_2$ = refractive index of second medium
  • $\theta_1$ = angle of incidence
  • $\theta_2$ = angle of refraction

Core Strategies

1. Identify the Media First

Determine which material light is leaving and which material it is entering before applying Snell's Law.

2. Measure Angles from the Normal

Both $\theta_1$ and $\theta_2$ must be measured from the normal.

3. Predict the Direction of Bending

Light bends toward the normal when entering a more optically dense material. Light bends away from the normal when entering a less optically dense material.

4. Isolate the Unknown Carefully

Rearrange $n_1 \sin \theta_1 = n_2 \sin \theta_2$ before using inverse sine.

Summary of Key Concepts

  • Refraction occurs when light changes speed between materials.
  • The refractive index describes how much light slows down in a material.
  • Snell's Law relates angles and refractive indices.
  • Angles must be measured from the normal.
  • Light bends toward the normal when entering a more optically dense material.
  • Light bends away from the normal when entering a less optically dense material.

Common Mistakes

  • Measuring angles from the surface instead of the normal.
  • Switching $n_1$ and $n_2$ incorrectly.
  • Forgetting to use inverse sine when solving for an angle.
  • Using incorrect units for speed.
  • Failing to predict direction before calculating.
