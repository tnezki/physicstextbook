SECTION 1.4

Vectors and Vector Components

Many physical quantities have both size and direction—like velocity, force, and displacement. Vectors are the mathematical tools we use to represent these quantities. In this section, you'll learn to work with vectors, break them into components, and use trigonometry to solve two-dimensional problems.

Learning Objectives

  • I can distinguish between scalar and vector quantities and provide examples of each.
  • I can represent vectors graphically using arrows showing magnitude and direction.
  • I can resolve vectors into perpendicular components using trigonometry.
  • I can calculate the magnitude and direction of a vector from its components.

Introduction

Imagine you're giving someone directions. You could say, "Walk 500 meters." But that's not enough—your friend would ask, "In which direction?" To fully describe the motion, you need to specify both the distance (500 m) and the direction (north, east, at 30° from the x-axis, etc.). This is exactly what vectors do in physics—they represent quantities that have both magnitude and direction.

Understanding vectors is essential in physics because many important quantities are vectors: velocity, acceleration, force, momentum, and electric and magnetic fields. In this section, you'll learn the mathematical tools to work with these quantities confidently.

Scalars vs. Vectors

Physical quantities fall into two categories:

Scalar: A quantity that has only magnitude (size). Examples: mass, time, temperature, speed, energy, volume.

Vector: A quantity that has both magnitude and direction. Examples: displacement, velocity, acceleration, force, momentum.

Scalar Quantities Vector Quantities
Distance (50 m) Displacement (50 m north)
Speed (30 m/s) Velocity (30 m/s east)
Mass (5 kg) Weight (49 N downward)
Temperature (25°C) Force (100 N at 45°)
Time (10 s) Acceleration (9.8 m/s² down)

Distance vs. Displacement

Distance is a scalar—it's the total length of path traveled. Displacement is a vector—it's the straight-line distance from start to finish with direction. If you walk 10 m east then 10 m west, your distance is 20 m, but your displacement is 0 m (you're back where you started).

Representing Vectors Graphically

We represent vectors as arrows:

  • Length of arrow: Represents the magnitude (size)
  • Direction of arrow: Represents the direction
  • Arrowhead: Points in the direction of the vector

We denote vectors with boldface letters ($\mathbf{A}$, $\mathbf{B}$) or letters with arrows ($\vec{A}$, $\vec{B}$). The magnitude (size only) is written as $|\mathbf{A}|$ or simply $A$.

Specifying Direction

Directions can be specified in several ways:

  • Cardinal directions: "50 m north" or "30 m/s east"
  • Angles from an axis: "100 N at 30° above the positive x-axis"
  • Compass bearings: "N 30° E" means 30° east of north
  • Standard position: Angle measured counterclockwise from the positive x-axis

Vector Components

Any vector can be broken down into components—perpendicular parts that add up to give the original vector. Usually, we use horizontal (x) and vertical (y) components.

Think of it like this: Imagine pushing a lawnmower. Your force on the handle is at an angle. Part of your force pushes forward (horizontal component) and part pushes downward (vertical component). The components work together to create the total force.

Finding Components Using Trigonometry

Given a vector $\mathbf{A}$ with magnitude $A$ at angle $\theta$ from the positive x-axis:

Horizontal (x) component: $A_x = A \cos(\theta)$

Vertical (y) component: $A_y = A \sin(\theta)$

Trigonometry Review

For a right triangle with angle $\theta$:

  • $\sin(\theta) = \frac{\text{opposite}}{\text{hypotenuse}}$
  • $\cos(\theta) = \frac{\text{adjacent}}{\text{hypotenuse}}$
  • $\tan(\theta) = \frac{\text{opposite}}{\text{adjacent}}$

SOH-CAH-TOA: Sine = Opposite/Hypotenuse, Cosine = Adjacent/Hypotenuse, Tangent = Opposite/Adjacent

Finding Magnitude and Direction from Components

If you know the components $A_x$ and $A_y$, you can find the magnitude and direction:

Magnitude: $A = \sqrt{A_x^2 + A_y^2}$ (Pythagorean theorem)

Direction: $\theta = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{A_y}{A_x}\right)$ (inverse tangent)

Important: Signs Matter!

Components can be positive or negative:

  • $A_x > 0$: vector points right (positive x-direction)
  • $A_x < 0$: vector points left (negative x-direction)
  • $A_y > 0$: vector points up (positive y-direction)
  • $A_y < 0$: vector points down (negative y-direction)

When using $\tan^{-1}$, pay attention to which quadrant your vector is in!

Example Problems

Problem-Solving Strategy

  1. List the Givens: Write down all known values with units.
  2. Find the Formula: Identify which formula to use.
  3. Substitute: Plug values into the formula.
  4. Solve: Calculate the answer.
  5. Check: Verify units and reasonableness.

Example 1: Finding X-Component

Problem: A force vector has a magnitude of 50 N and makes an angle of 30° with the positive x-axis. What is the x-component of this force?

Step 1 - List the Givens:

  • Magnitude: $F = 50$ N
  • Angle: $\theta = 30°$
  • Find: $F_x$

Step 2 - Find the Formula:

$$F_x = F \cos(\theta)$$

Step 3 - Substitute:

$$F_x = (50 \text{ N}) \cos(30°)$$

Step 4 - Solve:

Using $\cos(30°) = 0.866$:

$$F_x = 50 \times 0.866 = 43.3 \text{ N}$$

Answer: The x-component is 43.3 N.

Example 2: Finding Y-Component

Problem: A displacement vector has a magnitude of 75 m at an angle of 60° above the positive x-axis. What is the y-component of the displacement?

Step 1 - List the Givens:

  • Magnitude: $d = 75$ m
  • Angle: $\theta = 60°$
  • Find: $d_y$

Step 2 - Find the Formula:

$$d_y = d \sin(\theta)$$

Step 3 - Substitute:

$$d_y = (75 \text{ m}) \sin(60°)$$

Step 4 - Solve:

Using $\sin(60°) = 0.866$:

$$d_y = 75 \times 0.866 = 65.0 \text{ m}$$

Answer: The y-component is 65.0 m.

Example 3: Multi-Step - Finding Magnitude and Direction

Problem: A velocity vector has an x-component of 24 m/s and a y-component of 18 m/s. Find (a) the magnitude of the velocity and (b) the direction of the velocity.

Part (a): Find magnitude

Step 1 - List the Givens:

  • $v_x = 24$ m/s
  • $v_y = 18$ m/s
  • Find: magnitude $v$

Step 2 - Find the Formula:

$$v = \sqrt{v_x^2 + v_y^2}$$

Step 3 - Substitute:

$$v = \sqrt{(24)^2 + (18)^2} = \sqrt{576 + 324}$$

Step 4 - Solve:

$$v = \sqrt{900} = 30 \text{ m/s}$$

Answer: The magnitude is 30 m/s.

Part (b): Find direction

Step 1 - List the Givens:

  • $v_x = 24$ m/s
  • $v_y = 18$ m/s
  • Find: angle $\theta$

Step 2 - Find the Formula:

$$\theta = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{v_y}{v_x}\right)$$

Step 3 - Substitute:

$$\theta = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{18}{24}\right) = \tan^{-1}(0.75)$$

Step 4 - Solve:

$$\theta = 36.9°$$

Answer: The direction is 36.9° above the positive x-axis.

Summary

  • Scalars have only magnitude; vectors have both magnitude and direction.
  • Examples of scalars: mass, time, temperature, speed, distance.
  • Examples of vectors: displacement, velocity, acceleration, force.
  • Vectors are represented graphically as arrows where length = magnitude and direction = direction.
  • Any vector can be broken into perpendicular components (usually x and y).
  • The x-component is found using: $A_x = A \cos(\theta)$.
  • The y-component is found using: $A_y = A \sin(\theta)$.
  • The magnitude from components uses the Pythagorean theorem: $A = \sqrt{A_x^2 + A_y^2}$.
  • The direction from components uses inverse tangent: $\theta = \tan^{-1}(A_y/A_x)$.
  • Components can be positive or negative depending on direction; pay attention to signs!

Practice Problems

1. Explain the difference between a scalar and a vector. Give two examples of each from everyday life.

2. A student walks 50 meters east, then 50 meters west, returning to the starting point. What is the distance traveled? What is the displacement?

3. Why do we break vectors into components? How does this help us solve physics problems?

4. A vector has a magnitude of 100 N at an angle of 0° (pointing along the positive x-axis). What are the x and y components? What if the angle is 90°?

5. A force vector has a magnitude of 120 N at an angle of 40° above the positive x-axis. Calculate the x-component of the force. (Use $\cos(40°) = 0.766$)

6. A displacement vector has a magnitude of 85 m at an angle of 55° above the horizontal. Calculate the y-component of the displacement. (Use $\sin(55°) = 0.819$)

7. A velocity vector has components $v_x = 15$ m/s and $v_y = 20$ m/s. Calculate the magnitude of the velocity.

8. Multi-step problem: An acceleration vector has components $a_x = 3.0$ m/s² and $a_y = 4.0$ m/s².
(a) Calculate the magnitude of the acceleration.
(b) Calculate the direction of the acceleration (angle from the positive x-axis).

Answer Key

1. A scalar is a quantity with only magnitude (size), while a vector has both magnitude and direction. Scalar examples: temperature (75°F), time (30 seconds), speed (50 mph), mass (10 kg). Vector examples: displacement (100 m north), velocity (30 m/s east), force (50 N downward), wind (15 mph from the southwest).

2. Distance is the total path length = 50 m + 50 m = 100 m (scalar). Displacement is the straight-line distance from start to finish with direction. Since the student returns to the starting point, displacement = 0 m (vector). This shows the key difference: distance is always positive, but displacement can be zero if you return to where you started.

3. We break vectors into components to work with each direction independently. This is useful because: (1) It's easier to work with perpendicular directions separately. (2) When adding vectors, we can add all x-components together and all y-components together. (3) Many physics laws and equations work separately in x and y directions. (4) Components allow us to use algebra instead of complex geometry.

4. At 0°: $F_x = 100 \cos(0°) = 100 \times 1 = 100$ N; $F_y = 100 \sin(0°) = 100 \times 0 = 0$ N. The vector points entirely in the x-direction.
At 90°: $F_x = 100 \cos(90°) = 100 \times 0 = 0$ N; $F_y = 100 \sin(90°) = 100 \times 1 = 100$ N. The vector points entirely in the y-direction.

5.

Given: $F = 120$ N, $\theta = 40°$

Find: $F_x$

Formula: $F_x = F \cos(\theta)$

Substitute: $F_x = (120 \text{ N}) \cos(40°) = 120 \times 0.766$

Solve: $F_x = 91.9$ N

Answer: The x-component is 91.9 N.

6.

Given: $d = 85$ m, $\theta = 55°$

Find: $d_y$

Formula: $d_y = d \sin(\theta)$

Substitute: $d_y = (85 \text{ m}) \sin(55°) = 85 \times 0.819$

Solve: $d_y = 69.6$ m

Answer: The y-component is 69.6 m.

7.

Given: $v_x = 15$ m/s, $v_y = 20$ m/s

Find: magnitude $v$

Formula: $v = \sqrt{v_x^2 + v_y^2}$

Substitute: $v = \sqrt{(15)^2 + (20)^2} = \sqrt{225 + 400} = \sqrt{625}$

Solve: $v = 25$ m/s

Answer: The magnitude is 25 m/s.

8.

Part (a): Magnitude

Given: $a_x = 3.0$ m/s², $a_y = 4.0$ m/s²

Formula: $a = \sqrt{a_x^2 + a_y^2}$

Substitute: $a = \sqrt{(3.0)^2 + (4.0)^2} = \sqrt{9.0 + 16.0} = \sqrt{25.0}$

Solve: $a = 5.0$ m/s²

Answer: The magnitude is 5.0 m/s².

Part (b): Direction

Formula: $\theta = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{a_y}{a_x}\right)$

Substitute: $\theta = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{4.0}{3.0}\right) = \tan^{-1}(1.333)$

Solve: $\theta = 53.1°$

Answer: The direction is 53.1° above the positive x-axis.
