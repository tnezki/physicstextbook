7.4 Ohm's Law and Circuit Analysis

A phone charger, a flashlight, and a string of holiday lights all depend on the same basic circuit relationships. Voltage pushes charge, resistance opposes charge flow, and current responds to both.
This section develops Ohm’s Law as the core algebraic model for simple circuit behavior and introduces the reasoning needed to analyze basic electrical systems.
“Study hard what interests you the most in the most undisciplined, irreverent, and original manner possible.” — Richard Feynman
Why does this matter?

Ohm’s Law is one of the most useful tools in introductory electricity. It helps explain why some devices draw more current than others, why wires heat up, why batteries drain, and how engineers design circuits that are both functional and safe.

Introduction

Imagine two bulbs connected to identical batteries, but one bulb glows more brightly than the other. Something about the circuit is allowing more current to pass through one bulb than through the other. To understand that difference, physicists relate three quantities: voltage, current, and resistance.

Voltage provides energy per charge. Current tells how much charge flows each second. Resistance describes how strongly a material or component opposes that flow. Ohm’s Law connects these ideas with a simple equation that becomes one of the main tools for circuit analysis.

In this section, we move from general ideas about charge flow to the algebraic structure of circuits. Students begin to analyze what happens when voltage increases, when resistance changes, and when current is measured in different parts of a circuit.

Key Idea: Ohm’s Law connects voltage, current, and resistance. In many simple circuits, increasing voltage increases current, while increasing resistance decreases current.

History

In the early 1800s, Georg Simon Ohm studied how electrical current behaved in wires and circuits. Through careful experiments, he found a consistent mathematical relationship between voltage, current, and resistance.

At first, Ohm’s work was not widely accepted. Some scientists thought the relationship was too simple or did not apply broadly enough. Over time, however, experiments confirmed that his law was extremely useful for many electrical systems.

Ohm’s name is now attached both to the law itself and to the SI unit of resistance, the ohm $(\Omega)$. His work helped transform electricity from a collection of observations into a quantitative science that engineers could use.

Today, Ohm’s Law is taught in nearly every introductory electricity course because it gives students a practical, algebra-based entry point into circuit analysis.

Key Concepts

Ohm’s Law

Ohm’s Law states that the voltage across a component equals the current through the component multiplied by its resistance.

$$V = IR$$

This equation can also be rearranged as $$I = \frac{V}{R} \qquad \text{and} \qquad R = \frac{V}{I}$$

Ohm’s Law is often used to analyze resistors and simple circuits where temperature and other conditions remain approximately constant.

Voltage, Current, and Resistance

Voltage is the push that gives energy to charges. Current is the flow of charge. Resistance is the opposition to that flow. These three quantities are linked.

Relationship summary:
  • If voltage increases while resistance stays the same, current increases.
  • If resistance increases while voltage stays the same, current decreases.
  • If current increases while resistance stays the same, voltage must also increase.

Resistance

Resistance measures how much a material or device opposes current. Some materials allow charge to flow easily and have low resistance. Others oppose charge more strongly and have high resistance.

Resistance Unit:

Resistance is measured in ohms, written as $$\Omega$$ where $$1\,\Omega = 1\,\text{V/A}$$

Resistors are common components used to control current in circuits.

Simple Circuit Analysis

In simple one-loop circuits, Ohm’s Law can be used directly to find an unknown quantity if the other two are known. This makes it possible to analyze current through a resistor, voltage across a resistor, or the resistance of a component.

A circuit diagram helps students organize the known values and identify what quantity needs to be calculated.

Comparing Circuit Scenarios

Often, students do not need to calculate exact values. Instead, they compare how a circuit changes when one variable changes.

Examples of proportional reasoning:

  • If voltage doubles and resistance stays the same, current doubles.
  • If resistance doubles and voltage stays the same, current is cut in half.
  • If current doubles and resistance stays the same, voltage doubles.

Series Thinking in Simple Circuits

In introductory circuit analysis, students often begin with one resistor and one source. This helps isolate the relationship between $V$, $I$, and $R$ before moving to more complex series and parallel circuits.

Even in larger circuits, many questions can be understood by first analyzing one part at a time and asking how the voltage, current, and resistance are connected.

Units as a Check

Units help confirm whether a solution makes sense. For example, if current is found by dividing volts by ohms, $$I = \frac{V}{R}$$ then the result should be in amperes.

This is one reason unit analysis is a valuable circuit-analysis tool, especially in multi-step problems.

Worked Derivation

Ohm’s Law is commonly written as $$V = IR$$ but circuit problems may ask for current or resistance instead. To solve for current, divide both sides by $R$:

$$\frac{V}{R} = I$$

So: $$I = \frac{V}{R}$$

To solve for resistance, divide both sides of $$V = IR$$ by $I$:

$$\frac{V}{I} = R$$

So: $$R = \frac{V}{I}$$

Why this matters: Students should recognize all three forms as the same relationship, not three unrelated formulas.

Vocabulary

Ohm’s Law:

The relationship between voltage, current, and resistance: $V = IR$.

Resistance:

The opposition to current flow in a material or component.

Ohm $(\Omega)$:

The SI unit of resistance.

Resistor:

A circuit component designed to provide a specific resistance.

Circuit diagram:

A symbolic drawing that represents the parts of a circuit and how they are connected.

Series circuit:

A circuit in which charge has a single path to follow.

Equivalent resistance:

The single resistance that could replace part or all of a circuit while keeping the same electrical behavior.

Strategies

Given → Find → Equation → Solve → Check Units

  1. Given: List the known values and their units.
  2. Find: Decide whether you are solving for voltage, current, or resistance.
  3. Equation: Start with $V = IR$ and rearrange if needed.
  4. Solve: Substitute carefully and isolate the unknown.
  5. Check Units: Confirm that volts, amperes, and ohms match the requested quantity.

Diagram Tips

  • Sketch the battery, resistor, and current direction.
  • Label known values directly on the circuit diagram.
  • Circle the unknown before solving.
  • Use the diagram to check that your answer makes physical sense.

Unit Analysis Tips

  • $\text{V} = \text{A}\cdot\Omega$
  • $\text{A} = \frac{\text{V}}{\Omega}$
  • $\Omega = \frac{\text{V}}{\text{A}}$
Key Idea: In circuit analysis, the equation is only part of the work. A labeled diagram and unit check often reveal mistakes before the problem is finished.

Examples

Example 1 — Conceptual

A battery is connected to two different resistors one at a time. Explain why the circuit with the larger resistance has the smaller current if the battery voltage stays the same.

Example 2 — Single-Formula

A resistor has a voltage of $12\,\text{V}$ across it and carries a current of $3.0\,\text{A}$. Find the resistance.

Example 3 — Multi-Step

A $9.0\,\text{V}$ battery is connected to a resistor of $4.5\,\Omega$. Find the current in the circuit.

Example 4 — Unit Conversion Embedded

A circuit has a voltage of $6.0\,\text{V}$ and a current of $250\,\text{mA}$. Convert the current to amperes and find the resistance.

Example 5 — Graph / Relationship Interpretation

If the resistance in a circuit doubles while the voltage stays the same, what happens to the current? Explain using Ohm’s Law.

Example 6 — Circuit Analysis

A circuit diagram shows a $24\,\text{V}$ source connected to a resistor. The current is measured as $2.0\,\text{A}$. Find the resistance and label the circuit.

Example 7 — Draw It

Draw a simple circuit with a battery and resistor. Label the battery voltage, the resistor, and the current direction. Then write the Ohm’s Law equation that would relate the three quantities.

Common Mistakes

  • Using the wrong form of Ohm’s Law: Students sometimes solve for current but use $R = \frac{V}{I}$ instead of $I = \frac{V}{R}$.
  • Confusing resistance and current: Higher resistance does not mean higher current when voltage stays constant.
  • Unit mismatch: Students may forget to convert milliamperes to amperes.
  • Dropping units: Circuit answers should include volts, amperes, or ohms as appropriate.
  • Ignoring proportional reasoning: Students may recalculate from scratch when a comparison could be made directly.
  • Misreading a circuit diagram: Values must be attached to the correct component or source.

Summary

  • Ohm’s Law is $V = IR$.
  • Voltage, current, and resistance are connected in predictable ways.
  • Increasing voltage increases current if resistance stays the same.
  • Increasing resistance decreases current if voltage stays the same.
  • Resistance is measured in ohms $(\Omega)$.
  • Simple circuits can often be analyzed directly with algebra and unit reasoning.

Detailed Example Solutions

Example 1 — Conceptual

From $$I = \frac{V}{R}$$ if the battery voltage stays the same and resistance becomes larger, the current becomes smaller. The larger resistance opposes charge flow more strongly.

Example 2 — Single-Formula

Given: $V = 12\,\text{V}$, $I = 3.0\,\text{A}$

Equation:

$$R = \frac{V}{I}$$

Solve:

$$R = \frac{12}{3.0} = 4.0\,\Omega$$

Example 3 — Multi-Step

Given: $V = 9.0\,\text{V}$, $R = 4.5\,\Omega$

Equation:

$$I = \frac{V}{R}$$

Solve:

$$I = \frac{9.0}{4.5} = 2.0\,\text{A}$$

Example 4 — Unit Conversion Embedded

Convert current: $$250\,\text{mA} = 0.250\,\text{A}$$

Now use $$R = \frac{V}{I} = \frac{6.0}{0.250} = 24\,\Omega$$

Example 5 — Graph / Relationship Interpretation

From $$I = \frac{V}{R}$$ if resistance doubles while voltage stays constant, the current becomes half as large.

Example 6 — Circuit Analysis

Given: $V = 24\,\text{V}$, $I = 2.0\,\text{A}$

Find resistance: $$R = \frac{V}{I} = \frac{24}{2.0} = 12\,\Omega$$

Example 7 — Draw It

A correct sketch shows a battery, a resistor, and a labeled current direction. The equation relating the quantities is $$V = IR$$
