SECTION 6.3 – Total Internal Reflection and the Critical Angle

Explore Notes

Opening

What happens when light tries to leave a material but does not refract out of it? Under certain conditions, light reflects entirely inside the material. This phenomenon is called total internal reflection.

Objectives

  • I can explain the conditions required for total internal reflection to occur
  • I can define the critical angle
  • I can calculate the critical angle using Snell's Law
  • I can determine when total internal reflection will occur

Introduction

When light travels from a more optically dense material to a less optically dense material, the angle of refraction increases as the angle of incidence increases.

At a certain angle of incidence, the refracted ray travels along the boundary. This angle is called the critical angle.

Using Snell's Law:

$$n_1 \sin \theta_1 = n_2 \sin \theta_2$$

When the angle of refraction equals 90°, the equation becomes:

$$\sin \theta_c = \frac{n_2}{n_1}$$

Real-World

Total internal reflection is used in fiber optic cables, where light signals are transmitted through long distances without escaping.

Historical

The relationship describing total internal reflection follows directly from Snell's Law, developed in the 1600s during the advancement of geometric optics.

Vocabulary

Critical Angle ($\theta_c$): The angle of incidence at which the angle of refraction equals 90°.

Total Internal Reflection: The complete reflection of light within a material when the angle of incidence exceeds the critical angle.

Optically Dense Material: A material with a higher refractive index in which light travels more slowly.

Optically Less Dense Material: A material with a lower refractive index in which light travels more quickly.

Formula

Snell's Law:

$$n_1 \sin \theta_1 = n_2 \sin \theta_2$$

Critical Angle:

$$\sin \theta_c = \frac{n_2}{n_1}$$

Where:

  • $n_1$ = refractive index of first medium
  • $n_2$ = refractive index of second medium
  • $\theta_1$ = angle of incidence
  • $\theta_c$ = critical angle

Core Strategies

1. Confirm Direction of Travel

Total internal reflection only occurs when light travels from a higher refractive index to a lower refractive index.

2. Identify the Critical Condition

The critical angle occurs when the refracted angle equals 90°.

3. Compare Angles

If the angle of incidence is greater than the critical angle, total internal reflection occurs.

Summary of Key Concepts

  • Total internal reflection occurs when light travels from a higher refractive index to a lower refractive index.
  • The critical angle is the angle of incidence that produces a refracted angle of 90°.
  • The critical angle equation is $\sin \theta_c = \frac{n_2}{n_1}$.
  • If the angle of incidence exceeds the critical angle, no refraction occurs.
  • Snell's Law must be used to calculate the critical angle.
  • All angles are measured from the normal.

Common Mistakes

  • Trying to calculate a critical angle when $n_1 < n_2$.
  • Switching $n_1$ and $n_2$ in the critical angle equation.
  • Forgetting that total internal reflection only occurs when $n_1 > n_2$.
  • Using degrees and radians inconsistently on a calculator.
  • Measuring angles from the surface instead of the normal.

Solutions

1–6 Vocabulary

1. The angle of incidence that produces a refracted angle of 90°.

2. The complete reflection of light within a material when the angle of incidence exceeds the critical angle.

3. Light must travel from a higher refractive index to a lower refractive index and the angle must exceed the critical angle.

4. A higher refractive index means light travels more slowly.

5. $\sin \theta_c = \frac{n_2}{n_1}$

6. $n_1$ must be greater than $n_2$.

7–12 Conceptual

7. Because only then can the refracted angle increase to 90°.

8. The refracted ray travels along the boundary.

9. A larger difference changes the value of $\frac{n_2}{n_1}$ and therefore the critical angle.

10. Because light is entering a more optically dense material.

11. Light reflects repeatedly inside the core due to total internal reflection.

12. Refraction occurs and the ray exits the material.

13–17 Single-Step

13. $\sin \theta_c = \frac{1.00}{1.50} = 0.667$ → $\theta_c = 41.8°$

14. $\sin \theta_c = \frac{1.00}{1.70} = 0.588$ → $\theta_c = 36.0°$

15. $\sin \theta_c = \frac{1.00}{1.33} = 0.752$ → $\theta_c = 48.8°$; 60° > 48.8° → TIR occurs.

16. $\sin 42° = \frac{1.00}{n_1}$ → $n_1 = 1.49$

17. $\sin \theta_c = \frac{1.33}{1.80} = 0.739$ → $\theta_c = 47.6°$

18–20 Multi-Step

18. $\theta_c = 41.8°$; 50° > 41.8° → total internal reflection occurs.

19. $\sin \theta_c = \frac{1.00}{1.65} = 0.606$ → $\theta_c = 37.3°$; 35° < 37.3° → refraction occurs.

20. $\sin \theta_c = \frac{1.40}{1.60} = 0.875$ → $\theta_c = 61.0°$; light remains trapped because incident angles exceed 61°.
