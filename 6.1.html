SECTION 6.1

Reflection and the Law of Reflection

Every day, light bounces off mirrors, water, and other shiny surfaces to reach our eyes. In this section, we will explore how light reflects from surfaces and discover the simple rule that explains this behavior. Understanding reflection is fundamental to understanding how mirrors work and how light behaves in optical instruments.

Learning Objectives

  • I can describe how light travels in straight lines and explain what happens when light hits a surface
  • I can state and apply the law of reflection to predict how light bounces from mirrors
  • I can draw accurate ray diagrams showing incident rays, reflected rays, and angles of incidence and reflection
  • I can distinguish between different types of mirrors based on their shape and how they reflect light

Introduction

When you look in a mirror, you see your reflection staring back at you. But have you ever wondered how the light from your face gets to the mirror and then back to your eyes? The answer lies in a fundamental principle of physics called reflection.

Light is a form of electromagnetic radiation that travels through space in straight lines. When light hits a surface, something interesting happens: some of the light bounces off the surface. This bouncing of light is called reflection. The law that governs this reflection is simple and elegant, and it applies to all smooth, shiny surfaces—from bathroom mirrors to puddles of water.

Real-World Connection: Why Mirrors Work

Mirrors have been used by humans for thousands of years. The ancient Egyptians made mirrors from polished copper, and the Romans created mirrors from bronze. Today, most mirrors are made by coating the back of glass with a reflective material like aluminum or silver. Understanding the law of reflection helps engineers design better mirrors for telescopes, cars, and even laser systems used in surgery.

In this section, you will learn how light behaves when it strikes a surface. You'll discover that light doesn't bounce randomly—it follows a precise mathematical relationship. This relationship, called the law of reflection, is one of the most important principles in optics.

What Is Light?

Before we study reflection, we need to understand what light is. Light is a form of energy that travels in straight lines through empty space and through transparent materials. Light travels at an incredibly fast speed: approximately $3 \times 10^8$ meters per second (m/s) in empty space, which we often call the speed of light. This speed is so fast that if light traveled around Earth's equator, it would circle the planet seven times in just one second!

For studying reflection and refraction in this unit, we will use the ray model of light. In this model, we represent light as straight arrows called rays that show the direction in which light is traveling. This simple model works very well for understanding mirrors, lenses, and many other optical devices.

Key Terms: Light and Rays

Light: A form of electromagnetic energy that travels through space and allows us to see objects.

Ray: A straight line with an arrow that represents the direction light is traveling.

Speed of light (c): The speed at which light travels through empty space, approximately $3 \times 10^8$ m/s.

Ray model: A simplified way of representing light as straight lines (rays) traveling in specific directions.

Understanding Reflection

When light strikes a smooth surface—like a mirror or calm water—most of the light bounces off. This bouncing of light is called reflection. The surface that the light bounces off is called the reflecting surface.

To describe reflection accurately, we need to define several important angles and lines:

  • Normal: An imaginary line perpendicular (at a 90-degree angle) to the reflecting surface at the point where light hits it
  • Angle of incidence (θ₁): The angle between the incoming light ray and the normal
  • Angle of reflection (θ₂): The angle between the reflected light ray and the normal
  • Incident ray: The light ray traveling toward the reflecting surface
  • Reflected ray: The light ray bouncing away from the reflecting surface

Important: Angles Are Measured from the Normal

A common mistake is to measure angles from the surface itself. Always measure angles of incidence and reflection from the normal, not from the surface. The normal is the imaginary line perpendicular to the surface.

The Law of Reflection

After centuries of observing how light bounces off surfaces, scientists discovered a remarkably simple pattern. The law of reflection states:

The Law of Reflection

The angle of incidence equals the angle of reflection.

In mathematical form: θ₁ = θ₂

This law is true for all smooth, shiny surfaces. It doesn't matter what material the surface is made of—glass, metal, water, or anything else. As long as the surface is smooth, the angles will be equal.

Historical Note: Discovery of the Law of Reflection

The law of reflection was first described scientifically by the ancient Greek mathematician Euclid around 300 BCE. However, it wasn't until the 1600s that scientists like René Descartes and Isaac Newton helped us fully understand why light reflects this way. The law of reflection is so fundamental that it applies not only to light but also to other waves, like sound waves bouncing off canyon walls (creating echoes).

Drawing Ray Diagrams

To solve reflection problems, we draw diagrams called ray diagrams. Here's how to construct an accurate ray diagram:

Steps for Drawing a Ray Diagram

  1. Draw the reflecting surface as a straight line
  2. At the point where light hits the surface, draw a normal (perpendicular) line
  3. Draw the incident ray approaching the surface, making an angle with the normal
  4. Using the law of reflection, draw the reflected ray at an equal angle on the opposite side of the normal
  5. Label all angles, rays, and the normal clearly

  • The incident ray (red) hits the mirror at an angle
  • The normal (dashed line) is perpendicular to the mirror
  • The reflected ray (blue) leaves at an equal angle on the opposite side
  • Both angles are measured from the normal, not from the mirror surface

Types of Reflection

Not all surfaces reflect light the same way. There are two main types of reflection:

Specular Reflection (Regular Reflection)

Specular reflection occurs when light reflects off a smooth, shiny surface like a mirror or calm water. All the light rays reflect at the same angle, creating a clear, bright reflection. This is what allows you to see a sharp image when you look in a mirror.

Diffuse Reflection (Irregular Reflection)

Diffuse reflection occurs when light reflects off a rough, bumpy surface like paper, a wall, or the pages of this book. Even though the law of reflection still applies at each tiny spot on the surface, the normals point in different directions. This causes the reflected rays to scatter in many different directions. This is why you cannot see a clear reflection in a rough surface.

Why We Can See Most Objects

Most objects in the world are not mirrors—they don't produce clear reflections. Instead, light bounces off them through diffuse reflection, scattering in all directions. This scattered light allows our eyes to see the object from any angle. For example, when you look at a book, light bounces diffusely off the pages and into your eyes. If the pages were mirrors, you could only see a reflection from one specific angle!

Example Problems

Example 1: Finding the Angle of Reflection

Given: A light ray hits a mirror at an angle of incidence of 35° from the normal.

Find: The angle of reflection.

Solution:

Step 1: Identify what we know and what we need to find.

  • Angle of incidence (θ₁) = 35°
  • Angle of reflection (θ₂) = ?

Step 2: Apply the law of reflection.

By the law of reflection: θ₁ = θ₂

Step 3: Calculate the answer.

θ₂ = θ₁ = 35°

Answer: The angle of reflection is 35°.

Example 2: Identifying Types of Reflection

Given: You shine a flashlight on three different surfaces: (A) a smooth mirror, (B) a window, and (C) a wall.

Find: Which surface produces specular reflection and which produces diffuse reflection?

Solution:

Step 1: Recall the definition of specular reflection.

Specular reflection occurs on smooth, shiny surfaces where all rays reflect in the same direction, creating a bright, clear reflection.

Step 2: Recall the definition of diffuse reflection.

Diffuse reflection occurs on rough surfaces where rays scatter in different directions, and you cannot see a clear reflection.

Step 3: Classify each surface.

  • (A) Mirror: smooth and shiny → Specular reflection
  • (B) Window: smooth and shiny → Specular reflection
  • (C) Wall: rough and bumpy → Diffuse reflection

Answer: Mirror and window produce specular reflection. Wall produces diffuse reflection.

Example 3: Drawing and Analyzing a Ray Diagram

Given: Light strikes a mirror at an angle of 55° from the surface (not the normal).

Find: Draw an accurate ray diagram and find the angle of incidence and angle of reflection.

Solution:

Step 1: Convert the angle from the surface to an angle from the normal.

If the angle from the surface is 55°, then the angle from the normal = 90° − 55° = 35°

Therefore: θ₁ = 35°

Step 2: Use the law of reflection to find the angle of reflection.

θ₂ = θ₁ = 35°

Step 3: Sketch the ray diagram (shown below).

Answer: Angle of incidence = 35°, Angle of reflection = 35°. Ray diagram shows incident and reflected rays at equal angles from the normal.

Summary

Key Concepts

  • Light travels in straight lines in a uniform medium and can be represented by rays
  • Reflection occurs when light bounces off a surface
  • The normal is an imaginary line perpendicular to the reflecting surface
  • The angle of incidence is the angle between the incident ray and the normal
  • The angle of reflection is the angle between the reflected ray and the normal
  • The law of reflection states: angle of incidence = angle of reflection (θ₁ = θ₂)
  • Specular reflection occurs on smooth surfaces and creates clear images
  • Diffuse reflection occurs on rough surfaces and scatters light in many directions
  • Both specular and diffuse reflection obey the law of reflection at each point on the surface
  • Ray diagrams are a useful tool for visualizing how light reflects from surfaces

Answer Key

Conceptual Questions

1. What is the difference between specular and diffuse reflection?

Answer: Specular reflection occurs on smooth, shiny surfaces where all light rays reflect in the same direction, creating a clear, bright image or reflection. Diffuse reflection occurs on rough, bumpy surfaces where light rays reflect in many different directions, scattering the light. As a result, specular reflection produces a sharp image (like in a mirror), while diffuse reflection does not.

2. Why must we always measure angles of incidence and reflection from the normal, not from the reflecting surface?

Answer: The law of reflection states that angle of incidence = angle of reflection, but this relationship only holds true when both angles are measured from the normal. The normal is perpendicular (90°) to the surface, so it provides a consistent, fixed reference line. If we measured from the surface itself, the relationship would not work correctly, and we could not reliably predict how light reflects.

3. If light strikes a mirror perpendicularly (straight on), what is the angle of incidence? What is the angle of reflection? Explain your answer.

Answer: The angle of incidence is 0°, and the angle of reflection is also 0°. When light hits the mirror straight on (perpendicular to the surface), it is traveling along the normal. Since the angle is measured from the normal, the angle is 0°. By the law of reflection, the reflected ray also travels at 0° from the normal, meaning it bounces straight back along the same path it came from.

4. Why can you see your reflection in a still lake but not in a rough, rocky surface, even though the law of reflection applies to both?

Answer: A still lake is smooth, so its surface acts like a mirror and produces specular reflection. All the light rays from your body reflect at the same angle and travel together to your eyes, creating a clear image. A rough, rocky surface produces diffuse reflection because the surface is uneven with many small bumps and slopes. Light rays hit different parts at different angles, so they reflect in many directions. The scattered light reaches your eyes from many points, creating a scrambled, unclear image rather than a clean reflection.

Calculation Problems

5. A light ray hits a mirror at an angle of incidence of 42° from the normal. What is the angle of reflection?

Answer: By the law of reflection, θ₁ = θ₂. Therefore, the angle of reflection = 42°.

6. Light strikes a mirror surface at an angle of 60° from the mirror surface itself (not from the normal). Convert this to an angle measured from the normal. Then determine the angle of reflection.

Solution:

Step 1: Convert angle from surface to angle from normal:

θ_normal = 90° − 60° = 30°

Step 2: Apply the law of reflection:

θ_reflection = θ_incidence = 30°

Answer: Angle of incidence from normal = 30°; Angle of reflection = 30°.

Multi-Step Problems

7. Two mirrors are placed at a 90° angle to each other, forming a corner. A light ray strikes the first mirror at an angle of incidence of 30°.

Solution:

a) Angle of reflection from the first mirror = 30° (by law of reflection)

b) The reflected ray travels at 30° from the normal of the first mirror. When it hits the second mirror (which is perpendicular to the first), the angle of incidence at the second mirror = 30°

c) Angle of reflection at the second mirror = 30° (by law of reflection)

d) The final ray travels back in a direction parallel to (but opposite from) the original incident ray. This is because each reflection changes the ray's direction by the same amount, and the perpendicular mirrors combine to redirect the light back along its original path.

8. A person stands 2 meters in front of a plane mirror and looks at their reflection.

Answer:

a) Ray diagram should show:

  • A person on the left side
  • A mirror on the right
  • A light ray leaving the person's eye, hitting the mirror, and reflecting back to the eye

b) The angle of incidence and angle of reflection should be equal and measured from the normal (perpendicular to the mirror).

c) The person sees their reflection behind the mirror because our brain traces the reflected rays backward in a straight line. The reflected rays appear to come from behind the mirror at a distance equal to the distance between the person and the mirror. This creates the illusion of an image behind the glass surface, even though no actual light rays come from that location. The image is virtual, meaning it appears to be there but no light actually originates from it.
