SECTION 5.4

Wave Speed

When you create a disturbance in a rope or water, the wave travels along the medium at a specific speed determined by the medium's properties. Understanding wave speed—how far a wave travels in a given time—is essential for predicting wave behavior, from ripples on a pond to light traveling through space. Discover how frequency, wavelength, and the medium work together to determine how fast waves move.

Learning Objectives

  • I can calculate wave speed using the relationship between distance and time.
  • I can apply the wave equation to find wave speed, frequency, or wavelength when given the other two variables.
  • I can explain how the medium affects wave speed and predict how waves behave in different materials.
  • I can solve multi-step problems involving wave speed, wavelength, and frequency in real-world contexts.

Introduction

Imagine dropping a pebble into a still pond. Ripples spread outward in concentric circles, but how quickly do they travel? The answer depends on wave speed—the distance the wave travels in a given amount of time. Wave speed is a fundamental property that determines when and where a wave will arrive. A surfer must understand ocean wave speed to predict when waves will reach the beach. A geologist uses wave speed to locate earthquakes. A musician depends on sound wave speed to produce the right frequencies. In every case, understanding wave speed is critical to predicting and controlling wave behavior.

Wave speed is unique because it depends not on how the wave is created, but on the properties of the medium through which it travels. A loud sound and a soft sound travel at the same speed through air, but they travel at different speeds through water or steel. This fundamental principle underlies much of wave physics and helps explain why different materials behave so differently when waves pass through them.

Real-World Connection: Earthquake scientists use the different wave speeds of P-waves and S-waves traveling through Earth's interior to locate earthquake epicenters. P-waves travel faster through rock and arrive first at seismograph stations, while slower S-waves arrive later. By measuring the time delay between these arrivals at multiple stations, scientists can pinpoint where the earthquake occurred—a technique that has saved countless lives.

The Speed of a Wave

Defining Wave Speed

The speed of a wave is the distance a wave travels in a given amount of time. Like all speeds, wave speed can be calculated using the fundamental relationship between distance and time:

$$\text{Speed} = \frac{\text{Distance}}{\text{Time}}$$

In physics, we typically denote wave speed with the variable v (for velocity), distance with d, and time with t:

$$v = \frac{d}{t}$$

When measuring wave speed, distance is expressed in meters (m) and time in seconds (s), so wave speed is expressed in meters per second (m/s). This is the standard SI unit for speed.

The key insight is that wave speed depends only on the medium. No matter how the wave is created—whether you shake a rope gently or vigorously—the wave travels at the same speed through that rope. A whispered sound and a shout travel at the same speed through air (approximately 343 m/s at room temperature). The difference in loudness affects the wave's amplitude, not its speed.

Important Distinction: Don't confuse wave speed with particle velocity. In a water wave, the water molecules move up and down (or back and forth) relatively slowly, but the wave pattern itself propagates much faster across the water's surface. The wave speed describes how fast the disturbance pattern travels, not how fast individual particles move.

The Wave Equation: Connecting Speed, Frequency, and Wavelength

Wave speed is related to two other fundamental wave properties: wavelength and frequency. The wave equation connects these three quantities:

$$v = f \lambda$$

Where:

  • v = wave speed (m/s)
  • f = frequency (Hz or cycles/s)
  • λ = wavelength (m)

This equation reveals a crucial relationship: for a given medium, if frequency increases, wavelength must decrease, and vice versa. The product of these two quantities—frequency multiplied by wavelength—always equals the wave speed, which depends only on the medium.

To understand this physically, consider a loudspeaker playing different notes. A high-frequency note (like a soprano singing high C) creates waves that oscillate rapidly, producing shorter wavelengths. A low-frequency note (like a bass drum) creates waves that oscillate slowly, producing longer wavelengths. But both travel at the same speed through the air because they're in the same medium.

Rearrangements of the Wave Equation:

Depending on which quantity you need to find, the wave equation can be rearranged:

  • For speed: $$v = f\lambda$$
  • For frequency: $$f = \frac{v}{\lambda}$$
  • For wavelength: $$\lambda = \frac{v}{f}$$

Calculating Wave Speed and Related Quantities

Finding Wave Speed from Wavelength and Frequency

The most straightforward application of the wave equation is calculating wave speed when you know wavelength and frequency. Here's the process:

Step 1: Identify the known quantities and what you need to find.

Step 2: Select the appropriate equation (in this case, $v = f\lambda$).

Step 3: Substitute the values and calculate.

Step 4: Include units in your answer.

Example: Ocean Wave Speed

Given:

  • Wavelength: λ = 3.0 m
  • Frequency: f = 1.0 Hz

Find: What is the speed of this ocean wave?

Solution:

$$v = f\lambda = (1.0 \text{ Hz})(3.0 \text{ m}) = 3.0 \text{ m/s}$$

Answer: The wave speed is 3.0 m/s.

Finding Frequency or Wavelength from Wave Speed

Often you know the wave speed (determined by the medium) and either the frequency or wavelength, and need to find the other. In these cases, rearrange the wave equation:

Example: Finding Frequency

Given:

  • Wave speed: v = 2.0 m/s
  • Wavelength: λ = 1.0 m

Find: What is the frequency of this wave?

Solution:

Step 1: Rearrange the wave equation to solve for frequency.

$$f = \frac{v}{\lambda}$$

Step 2: Substitute values.

$$f = \frac{2.0 \text{ m/s}}{1.0 \text{ m}} = 2.0 \text{ Hz}$$

Answer: The frequency is 2.0 Hz.

Example: Finding Wavelength

Given:

  • Wave speed in air: v = 343 m/s
  • Frequency of sound: f = 440 Hz (musical note A)

Find: What is the wavelength of this sound wave?

Solution:

Step 1: Rearrange the wave equation to solve for wavelength.

$$\lambda = \frac{v}{f}$$

Step 2: Substitute values.

$$\lambda = \frac{343 \text{ m/s}}{440 \text{ Hz}} = 0.780 \text{ m}$$

Answer: The wavelength is 0.780 m or 78.0 cm.

Musical Note Context: The note A (440 Hz) is the standard tuning frequency for orchestral instruments. Knowing its wavelength in air (78 cm) helps engineers design concert halls and recording studios that work well with these frequencies.

The Medium Matters: How Wave Speed Depends on Properties

Why Wave Speed Varies by Medium

The most important principle in wave physics is this: wave speed depends on the medium through which the wave travels. This is fundamentally different from mechanical motion, where speed is determined by forces applied to an object. Waves don't "care" how they were created; they propagate at a speed determined entirely by the medium's properties.

Specifically, wave speed depends on:

  • Particle density: How close together particles are in the medium
  • Elasticity: How easily the medium deforms and returns to its original shape
  • Temperature: How the kinetic energy of particles affects wave propagation

Generally, waves travel fastest through solids and slowest through gases. This is because solids have particles packed closely together and tightly bonded. When a disturbance occurs, it quickly transmits from one particle to the next. In gases, particles are far apart, so the disturbance takes much longer to propagate.

Wave Speeds in Different Media

Here are typical wave speeds for sound in various media:

Medium Type Speed (m/s) Relative Speed
Air Gas 343 1× (reference)
Water Liquid 1,480 4.3×
Concrete Solid 3,800 11×
Steel Solid 5,960 17×
Diamond Solid 12,000 35×

Notice that sound travels about 4 times faster in water than in air, and about 17 times faster in steel. This explains why you can hear a train coming from far away by placing your ear on the rail—the sound travels much faster through the steel rail than through the air.

Whales and Ocean Communication: Whales use sound to communicate across enormous distances in the ocean. Because sound travels at 1,480 m/s in seawater (4.3 times faster than in air), whales can hear each other across distances of hundreds of kilometers. A whale call at 20 Hz can travel thousands of kilometers because the long wavelength (λ = v/f = 1,480/20 = 74 m) experiences minimal absorption in water. This is why marine biologists worry that ship noise disrupts whale communication—louder ship sounds mask the whales' natural signals.

Temperature Effects on Wave Speed

For a given medium, temperature also affects wave speed. In air, sound speed increases with temperature because warmer molecules move faster and transmit vibrations more quickly:

$$v_{\text{sound}} = 331 + 0.6T$$

Where v is speed in m/s and T is temperature in degrees Celsius.

At 0°C (freezing): v = 331 + 0.6(0) = 331 m/s

At 20°C (room temperature): v = 331 + 0.6(20) = 343 m/s

At 40°C (warm day): v = 331 + 0.6(40) = 355 m/s

Why Temperature Matters for Musicians: Musicians must account for temperature when performing outdoors. An instrument tuned at room temperature will sound slightly flat (lower frequency) in cold weather and sharp (higher frequency) in warm weather, because the sound speed changes and thus the frequency heard by the audience changes. Professional orchestras warm up instruments before outdoor concerts.

Example Problems

Example 1: Single-Step Wave Speed Calculation

Given:

  • A wave on a spring has wavelength λ = 0.10 m
  • Frequency: f = 2.0 Hz

Find: What is the speed of the wave?

Solution:

Step 1: Identify the wave equation to use.

We have wavelength and frequency, and need speed, so: $v = f\lambda$

Step 2: Substitute values.

$$v = (2.0 \text{ Hz})(0.10 \text{ m})$$

Step 3: Calculate.

$$v = 0.20 \text{ m/s}$$

Answer: The wave speed is 0.20 m/s.

Example 2: Finding Wavelength from Speed and Frequency

Given:

  • Speed of sound in water: v = 1,480 m/s
  • Frequency of a whale call: f = 20 Hz

Find: What is the wavelength of this whale call?

Solution:

Step 1: Identify the known quantities and write the appropriate equation.

We have speed and frequency, and need wavelength, so rearrange: $\lambda = \frac{v}{f}$

Step 2: Substitute values.

$$\lambda = \frac{1480 \text{ m/s}}{20 \text{ Hz}}$$

Step 3: Calculate.

$$\lambda = 74 \text{ m}$$

Answer: The wavelength is 74 m—nearly the length of three school buses end-to-end! This long wavelength allows whale songs to travel across ocean basins.

Example 3: Multi-Step Problem - Comparing Waves in Different Media

Given:

  • A loudspeaker emits a sound at frequency 1,000 Hz
  • Speed of sound in air: v_air = 343 m/s
  • Speed of sound in water: v_water = 1,480 m/s

Find:

  • a) What is the wavelength in air?
  • b) What is the wavelength in water?
  • c) How many times longer is the wavelength in water compared to air?
  • d) If the sound travels 100 m in air, how long does it take?

Solution:

Part a) Wavelength in Air

Step 1: Write the equation for wavelength.

$$\lambda = \frac{v}{f}$$

Step 2: Substitute values for air.

$$\lambda_{\text{air}} = \frac{343 \text{ m/s}}{1000 \text{ Hz}} = 0.343 \text{ m}$$

Answer (a): λ_air = 0.343 m or 34.3 cm

Part b) Wavelength in Water

Step 1: Use the same equation with water's sound speed.

$$\lambda_{\text{water}} = \frac{1480 \text{ m/s}}{1000 \text{ Hz}} = 1.48 \text{ m}$$

Answer (b): λ_water = 1.48 m

Part c) Ratio of Wavelengths

Step 1: Divide the wavelength in water by the wavelength in air.

$$\text{Ratio} = \frac{\lambda_{\text{water}}}{\lambda_{\text{air}}} = \frac{1.48 \text{ m}}{0.343 \text{ m}} = 4.3$$

Answer (c): The wavelength in water is 4.3 times longer than in air (which equals the speed ratio: 1,480/343 = 4.3).

Part d) Time for Sound to Travel 100 m in Air

Step 1: Rearrange the distance equation to solve for time.

$$d = vt$$

$$t = \frac{d}{v}$$

Step 2: Substitute values.

$$t = \frac{100 \text{ m}}{343 \text{ m/s}} = 0.292 \text{ s}$$

Answer (d): t = 0.292 s or about 0.3 seconds

Physical Insight: Notice that even though the frequency stays the same (1,000 Hz), the wavelength changes dramatically when the medium changes. This is because the wave speed is determined by the medium, not by the source. The relationship $v = f\lambda$ always holds: when speed increases, wavelength must increase proportionally if frequency stays constant.

Summary

  • Wave speed is the distance a wave travels in a given amount of time and is calculated using $v = \frac{d}{t}$, with units of meters per second (m/s).
  • The wave equation $v = f\lambda$ relates wave speed, frequency, and wavelength, allowing calculation of any one quantity when the other two are known.
  • For a given medium, wave speed is constant. Increasing frequency causes wavelength to decrease proportionally, and vice versa, keeping the product constant.
  • Wave speed depends only on the medium, not on how the wave is created. A loud sound and a soft sound travel at the same speed through air.
  • Waves travel fastest through solids and slowest through gases because particle density and elasticity affect how quickly disturbances propagate.
  • Typical sound speeds: air (343 m/s), water (1,480 m/s), steel (5,960 m/s)—showing that sound travels about 4 times faster in water and 17 times faster in steel compared to air.
  • Temperature affects wave speed. For sound in air: $v = 331 + 0.6T$ (°C), showing speed increases by 0.6 m/s for each degree Celsius increase.
  • Understanding wave speed is essential for applications including sonar detection, earthquake location, musical instrument design, and animal communication systems.
  • The wave equation can be rearranged to solve for any variable: $f = \frac{v}{\lambda}$ or $\lambda = \frac{v}{f}$.
  • Particle motion and wave motion are different. Individual particles oscillate slowly while the wave pattern propagates at the medium's characteristic speed.

Answer Key

Note to Teachers: This answer key provides complete solutions for all practice problems. Partial credit should be awarded based on correct method and reasoning, even if numerical answers contain minor rounding errors. Accept equivalent forms of correct answers.

Conceptual Questions (1-4)

Answer 1: Wave Speed and Medium

Key Points for a Complete Answer:

  • In solids, particles are packed closely together with strong bonds between them.
  • When a disturbance occurs, it quickly transfers from one particle to the next because they are close and strongly connected.
  • In gases, particles are far apart with weak interactions between them.
  • Disturbances take longer to propagate because particles must travel greater distances to collide with neighbors.
  • The density and elasticity (how quickly bonds restore the medium to its original shape) both contribute to faster propagation in solids.

Answer 2: Frequency and Wavelength Changes

Correct Response:

The speed of sound does NOT change when the source moves closer or farther away. Sound speed depends only on the medium (air), not on the source. However, both frequency and wavelength DO change due to the Doppler effect. When the source moves toward you, the frequency increases (sound is higher pitched) and the wavelength decreases because the sound waves are compressed. When the source moves away, the frequency decreases (sound is lower pitched) and the wavelength increases because the waves are stretched out. Despite these changes in frequency and wavelength, their product ($f \times \lambda$) remains constant and equal to the unchanging wave speed.

Answer 3: Wave Speed vs. Particle Speed

Correct Response:

Wave speed describes how fast the disturbance pattern travels through the medium. Particle speed describes how fast individual medium particles move back and forth (or up and down). In a water ripple, the wave might travel across the surface at 0.5 m/s, but individual water molecules move up and down at much slower speeds. The wave pattern propagates forward while the water molecules oscillate in place. Think of a crowd doing "the wave" at a stadium—the wave pattern races around the stadium while individual people simply move up and down in place.

Answer 4: Temperature Effects

Solution and Key Points:

At 30°C: $$v = 331 + 0.6(30) = 331 + 18 = 349 \text{ m/s}$$

Speed at 30°C = 349 m/s

Effect on Wavelength: For a fixed-frequency sound source, the wave equation ($v = f\lambda$) shows that if speed increases while frequency stays constant, wavelength must increase. As temperature increases, the sound travels faster, so longer wavelengths are produced. Conversely, as temperature decreases, sound travels slower and wavelengths decrease.

Calculation Problems (5-6)

Answer 5: Ripple Wave Speed

Given: λ = 0.50 m, f = 2.0 Hz

Find: v = ?

Solution:

$$v = f\lambda = (2.0 \text{ Hz})(0.50 \text{ m}) = 1.0 \text{ m/s}$$

Answer: v = 1.0 m/s

Answer 6: Radio Wave Wavelength

Given: f = 1.00 × 10⁸ Hz, v = 3.00 × 10⁸ m/s

Find: λ = ?

Solution:

$$\lambda = \frac{v}{f} = \frac{3.00 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s}}{1.00 \times 10^8 \text{ Hz}} = 3.0 \text{ m}$$

Answer: λ = 3.0 m (This is a typical FM radio wave wavelength.)

Multi-Step Problems (7-8)

Answer 7: Comparing Sound Wavelengths in Different Media

Given: f = 500 Hz, v_air = 343 m/s, v_water = 1,480 m/s

Part a) Wavelength in Air

$$\lambda_{\text{air}} = \frac{v}{f} = \frac{343 \text{ m/s}}{500 \text{ Hz}} = 0.686 \text{ m}$$

Answer (a): λ_air = 0.686 m or 68.6 cm

Part b) Wavelength in Water

$$\lambda_{\text{water}} = \frac{v}{f} = \frac{1480 \text{ m/s}}{500 \text{ Hz}} = 2.96 \text{ m}$$

Answer (b): λ_water = 2.96 m or approximately 3.0 m

Part c) Ratio of Wavelengths

$$\text{Ratio} = \frac{\lambda_{\text{water}}}{\lambda_{\text{air}}} = \frac{2.96}{0.686} = 4.3$$

Answer (c): The wavelength in water is 4.3 times larger (This matches the speed ratio: 1,480/343 = 4.3)

Answer 8: Thunder and Lightning

Given: λ = 0.686 m, v_air = 343 m/s, d = 1000 m, v_water = 1,480 m/s

Part a) Frequency of Thunder

$$f = \frac{v}{\lambda} = \frac{343 \text{ m/s}}{0.686 \text{ m}} = 500 \text{ Hz}$$

Answer (a): f = 500 Hz

Part b) Time for Thunder to Travel in Air

$$t = \frac{d}{v} = \frac{1000 \text{ m}}{343 \text{ m/s}} = 2.92 \text{ s}$$

Answer (b): t = 2.92 s or approximately 3 seconds

Physical Context: This is why we count seconds between lightning and thunder to estimate distance. Each 3-second delay represents approximately 1 km of distance.

Part c) Time for Sound to Travel in Water

$$t_{\text{water}} = \frac{d}{v} = \frac{1000 \text{ m}}{1480 \text{ m/s}} = 0.676 \text{ s}$$

Answer (c): t_water = 0.676 s or approximately 0.68 seconds

Comparison: Sound reaches you about 4.3 times faster in water than in air, because it travels 4.3 times faster. The faster speed means the sound covers the same distance in much less time.
