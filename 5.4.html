SECTION 5.4

Wave Properties and Behavior

Waves exhibit fascinating behaviors when they encounter boundaries, pass through openings, or meet other waves. Reflection bounces waves back, refraction bends their path, diffraction spreads them around obstacles, and interference creates patterns of reinforcement and cancellation. Understanding these wave behaviors explains everything from echoes to rainbows, from noise-cancelling headphones to the colors in soap bubbles.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this section, you will be able to:

  • I can explain and predict how waves reflect, refract, diffract, and interfere when they encounter boundaries, different media, or other waves.
  • I can distinguish between constructive and destructive interference and predict the resulting amplitude changes.
  • I can analyze data from wave interference patterns to determine whether constructive or destructive interference occurred.
  • I can provide real-world examples of wave behaviors and explain the underlying physics principles.

Introduction

Have you ever shouted in a canyon and heard your voice echo back, or noticed how a pencil looks bent when partially submerged in water? These everyday observations reveal fundamental wave behaviors. Waves don't simply travel in straight lines—they interact with their environment in predictable and often spectacular ways.

When waves encounter obstacles, boundaries, or other waves, four key behaviors can occur: reflection (bouncing back), refraction (bending when entering a new medium), diffraction (spreading around obstacles), and interference (combining with other waves). These behaviors are not just scientific curiosities—they underlie technologies from ultrasound imaging to wireless communication, from concert hall acoustics to telescope design.

Historical Context: Young's Double-Slit Experiment

In 1801, Thomas Young performed one of the most important experiments in physics history. He passed light through two narrow, closely-spaced slits and observed an interference pattern of bright and dark bands on a screen. This pattern could only be explained if light behaved as a wave, with the two light waves from the slits interfering constructively (bright bands) and destructively (dark bands). This experiment settled a centuries-long debate about whether light was made of particles or waves (in favor of waves at the time), and demonstrated that light exhibits the same interference behavior as water waves and sound waves. Young's experiment revolutionized our understanding of light and established wave interference as a fundamental phenomenon.

This section builds on everything you've learned about waves in Sections 5.1-5.3. You understand that waves transfer energy, have characteristic properties like wavelength and frequency, and come in transverse and longitudinal forms. Now you'll discover what happens when these waves interact with the world around them—knowledge essential for understanding both natural phenomena and modern technology.

Reflection of Waves

Reflection occurs when a wave encounters a boundary or obstacle and bounces back into the original medium. You experience wave reflection every time you see your image in a mirror (light waves reflecting) or hear an echo (sound waves reflecting off a distant surface).

Key Terms for Reflection

Incident wave: The wave approaching a boundary or surface.

Reflected wave: The wave that bounces back from the boundary.

Normal line: An imaginary line perpendicular to the reflecting surface at the point where the wave hits.

Angle of incidence: The angle between the incident wave and the normal line.

Angle of reflection: The angle between the reflected wave and the normal line.

The fundamental principle governing reflection is the law of reflection: the angle of incidence equals the angle of reflection. This law applies to all types of waves—light, sound, water waves, and more. When a wave hits a flat surface at a 30-degree angle from the perpendicular, it reflects at a 30-degree angle on the other side.

$$\theta_i = \theta_r$$

where $\theta_i$ is the angle of incidence and $\theta_r$ is the angle of reflection

The nature of reflection depends on whether the reflecting surface is smooth or rough. Smooth surfaces produce specular reflection where parallel incoming waves reflect as parallel outgoing waves, creating clear images (like mirrors). Rough surfaces produce diffuse reflection where waves scatter in many directions, preventing clear image formation but allowing us to see objects from various angles.

Real-World Application: Echoes and Echolocation

Echoes are reflected sound waves. When you shout toward a distant wall or cliff, sound waves travel to the surface, reflect, and return to your ears. Bats and dolphins use echolocation—they emit high-frequency sound waves and listen to the reflections to navigate and hunt in darkness or murky water. The time delay between emission and echo, combined with the speed of sound, tells them the distance to objects. This same principle is used in medical ultrasound and sonar technology.

Refraction of Waves

Refraction is the bending of waves as they pass from one medium into another where their speed changes. When light passes from air into water, or sound moves from cold air into warm air, the waves change direction at the boundary.

Understanding Refraction

Refraction: The bending of a wave's path as it enters a medium where it travels at a different speed.

Index of refraction (n): A measure of how much a medium slows down light compared to its speed in vacuum; $n = c/v$ where $c$ is the speed of light in vacuum and $v$ is the speed in the medium.

Refraction occurs because different parts of a wave front enter the new medium at different times. If a wave enters a slower medium at an angle, the part that enters first slows down while the rest is still traveling at the original speed. This causes the wave to bend toward the normal (perpendicular) line. Conversely, when a wave enters a faster medium, it bends away from the normal.

The relationship between angles and speeds in different media is described by Snell's Law:

$$n_1 \sin\theta_1 = n_2 \sin\theta_2$$

where $n_1$ and $n_2$ are indices of refraction, and $\theta_1$ and $\theta_2$ are angles from the normal

Refraction explains many familiar phenomena. A pencil appears bent when placed in a glass of water because light rays from the submerged portion refract as they exit the water, making the pencil appear in a different location. Mirages in deserts occur because light refracts through layers of air at different temperatures. Lenses in eyeglasses, cameras, and telescopes work by carefully controlling refraction to focus light.

Real-World Example: Fiber Optic Cables

Modern internet and telecommunications rely on fiber optic cables, which use refraction to trap and guide light signals over long distances. These cables have a glass core with high refractive index surrounded by a cladding with lower refractive index. When light traveling through the core hits the boundary at a steep angle, it undergoes total internal reflection—it can't escape into the cladding and bounces back into the core. By repeatedly reflecting off the walls, light travels through the fiber even when the cable bends. This allows data to travel at nearly the speed of light with minimal loss over thousands of miles.

Diffraction of Waves

Diffraction is the spreading and bending of waves as they pass through openings or around obstacles. You've experienced diffraction if you've ever heard someone talking in the next room even though you can't see them—sound waves diffract around the doorway.

Diffraction Concepts

Diffraction: The bending and spreading of waves as they encounter edges, openings, or obstacles.

Diffraction pattern: The characteristic distribution of wave intensity that results from diffraction, often showing alternating regions of high and low amplitude.

The amount of diffraction depends on the relationship between wavelength and the size of the opening or obstacle. When the wavelength is comparable to or larger than the obstacle/opening size, significant diffraction occurs. When the wavelength is much smaller than the obstacle/opening, diffraction is minimal and waves travel in relatively straight lines.

This explains why we can hear around corners but not see around them easily. Sound waves have wavelengths ranging from centimeters to meters, comparable to doorways and building corners, so they diffract significantly. Visible light has wavelengths of only a few hundred nanometers, much smaller than most everyday objects, so it travels in straighter lines and diffraction is less noticeable in daily life.

Diffraction and Wave Size

Key principle: Significant diffraction occurs when wavelength ≈ opening size. Large diffraction: wavelength > opening. Small diffraction: wavelength << opening. This is why radio waves (long wavelength) easily diffract around buildings and through doorways, while light waves (short wavelength) do not. It's also why "bass" sounds (long wavelength) from a neighbor's music seem to come through walls more than high-pitched sounds (short wavelength).

Diffraction sets fundamental limits on the resolution of optical instruments like telescopes and microscopes. Larger apertures (openings) reduce diffraction effects and allow finer details to be resolved. This is one reason why astronomical telescopes have very large mirrors or lenses—to minimize diffraction and see finer details in distant objects.

Interference of Waves

Interference occurs when two or more waves occupy the same space at the same time. The waves combine according to the principle of superposition, and the resulting wave amplitude can be larger or smaller than the individual waves.

Interference Vocabulary

Superposition: When two or more waves overlap, the displacement at any point is the sum of the displacements of the individual waves at that point.

Constructive interference: When waves combine to produce a larger amplitude; occurs when crests align with crests and troughs with troughs.

Destructive interference: When waves combine to produce a smaller amplitude; occurs when crests align with troughs.

Phase: The position within a wave cycle; waves "in phase" have aligned crests and troughs, while waves "out of phase" have misaligned features.

Constructive interference happens when two waves arrive at the same location with their crests and troughs aligned (in phase). The amplitudes add together, creating a wave with larger amplitude than either individual wave. If two identical waves with amplitude $A$ interfere constructively, the resulting amplitude is $2A$.

Destructive interference occurs when waves arrive with crests aligned with troughs (out of phase by 180 degrees or half a wavelength). The amplitudes subtract from each other. If two identical waves with amplitude $A$ interfere destructively, they can completely cancel, resulting in zero amplitude at that location.

Conditions for Interference:

Constructive: Path difference = $n\lambda$ (where $n = 0, 1, 2, 3...$)

Destructive: Path difference = $(n + \frac{1}{2})\lambda$ (where $n = 0, 1, 2, 3...$)

Interference creates distinctive patterns. In Young's double-slit experiment, light passing through two slits creates an interference pattern of bright and dark bands. Bright bands occur where waves from both slits arrive in phase (constructive interference), and dark bands occur where they arrive out of phase (destructive interference).

Understanding Path Difference

Path difference is the difference in distance traveled by two waves to reach a common point. If one wave travels exactly one wavelength farther than another to reach a point, they arrive in phase and interfere constructively. If one wave travels exactly half a wavelength (λ/2) farther, they arrive out of phase and interfere destructively. This concept is crucial for understanding interference patterns.

Standing Waves

A special case of interference produces standing waves. When a wave reflects back on itself—such as a wave on a guitar string reflecting from both fixed ends—the incident and reflected waves interfere to create a pattern that appears to stand still rather than propagate.

Standing waves have characteristic features:

  • Nodes: Points of zero amplitude where destructive interference always occurs; particles at nodes don't move.
  • Antinodes: Points of maximum amplitude where constructive interference occurs; particles at antinodes oscillate with maximum displacement.

Musical instruments rely on standing waves. A guitar string vibrates in standing wave patterns, with nodes at both ends (where the string is held fixed) and antinodes in between. Different musical notes correspond to different standing wave patterns—the fundamental frequency has one antinode in the middle, while higher harmonics have multiple antinodes.

Real-World Application: Noise-Cancelling Headphones

Noise-cancelling headphones use destructive interference to reduce unwanted sound. Microphones on the headphones detect incoming sound waves. The headphones then generate sound waves with the same frequency and amplitude but opposite phase (180 degrees out of phase). When these "anti-noise" waves combine with the environmental noise, destructive interference occurs, significantly reducing the sound you hear. This technology is particularly effective for constant low-frequency noise like airplane engines, train rumble, or air conditioning hum.

Applications of Wave Behaviors

Understanding wave behaviors has led to countless technologies and explains numerous natural phenomena:

Architectural Acoustics: Concert halls use reflection to distribute sound evenly throughout the space. Carefully designed surfaces reflect sound waves so every audience member hears clearly without echoes or dead zones.

Medical Imaging: Ultrasound uses reflection of sound waves from boundaries between different tissues to create images inside the body. Different tissues reflect ultrasound differently, allowing doctors to see organs, blood vessels, and developing babies.

Optical Coatings: Anti-reflective coatings on eyeglasses and camera lenses use thin films that create destructive interference for reflected light, reducing glare and improving light transmission.

Rainbow Formation: Rainbows form through refraction and reflection of sunlight in water droplets. Light refracts entering the droplet, reflects off the back surface, and refracts again while exiting. Different wavelengths (colors) refract by different amounts, separating white light into its spectrum.

Example Problems

Example 1: Reflection Angle Calculation

Problem: A light ray strikes a flat mirror at an angle of 35° from the normal line. At what angle does the light reflect?

Given:

  • Angle of incidence: $\theta_i = 35°$

Find: Angle of reflection $\theta_r$

Solution:

Use the law of reflection:

$$\theta_i = \theta_r$$

Therefore:

$$\theta_r = 35°$$

Answer: The light reflects at 35° from the normal, on the opposite side of the normal from the incident ray.

Example 2: Determining Interference Type

Problem: Two identical sound waves with amplitude 0.50 mm meet at a point. One wave has traveled 3.0 m to reach this point, and the other has traveled 3.6 m. If the wavelength is 1.2 m, will the waves interfere constructively or destructively?

Given:

  • Amplitude of each wave: $A = 0.50 \text{ mm}$
  • Distance traveled by wave 1: $d_1 = 3.0 \text{ m}$
  • Distance traveled by wave 2: $d_2 = 3.6 \text{ m}$
  • Wavelength: $\lambda = 1.2 \text{ m}$

Find: Type of interference and resulting amplitude

Solution:

First, calculate the path difference:

$$\Delta d = d_2 - d_1 = 3.6 \text{ m} - 3.0 \text{ m} = 0.6 \text{ m}$$

Compare path difference to wavelength:

$$\frac{\Delta d}{\lambda} = \frac{0.6 \text{ m}}{1.2 \text{ m}} = 0.5 = \frac{1}{2}$$

Since the path difference is exactly half a wavelength ($\lambda/2$), the waves arrive out of phase and will interfere destructively.

For complete destructive interference of identical waves:

$$A_{result} = 0 \text{ mm}$$

Answer: The waves interfere destructively. Since they have equal amplitudes and the path difference is exactly λ/2, they completely cancel, resulting in zero amplitude at this point.

Example 3: Multi-Step Wave Interference Analysis

Problem: Two speakers are placed 4.0 m apart, both emitting sound waves with frequency 1,715 Hz. A listener stands 5.0 m from speaker A and 7.0 m from speaker B. The speed of sound is 343 m/s.

(a) Calculate the wavelength of the sound.

(b) Calculate the path difference between waves from the two speakers reaching the listener.

(c) Will the listener hear constructive or destructive interference? Explain.

Given:

  • Speaker separation: 4.0 m
  • Distance from speaker A: $d_A = 5.0 \text{ m}$
  • Distance from speaker B: $d_B = 7.0 \text{ m}$
  • Frequency: $f = 1,715 \text{ Hz}$
  • Speed of sound: $v = 343 \text{ m/s}$

Find: (a) Wavelength, (b) Path difference, (c) Type of interference

Solution:

Part (a): Use the wave equation to find wavelength:

$$\lambda = \frac{v}{f} = \frac{343 \text{ m/s}}{1,715 \text{ Hz}} = 0.20 \text{ m}$$

Part (b): Calculate path difference:

$$\Delta d = d_B - d_A = 7.0 \text{ m} - 5.0 \text{ m} = 2.0 \text{ m}$$

Part (c): Compare path difference to wavelength:

$$\frac{\Delta d}{\lambda} = \frac{2.0 \text{ m}}{0.20 \text{ m}} = 10$$

The path difference equals exactly 10 wavelengths, which is an integer multiple of λ. This means the waves arrive in phase and will interfere constructively. The listener will hear a louder sound at this location.

Answer: (a) The wavelength is 0.20 m (20 cm). (b) The path difference is 2.0 m. (c) The listener experiences constructive interference because the path difference equals exactly 10 complete wavelengths, causing the waves to arrive in phase and reinforce each other, producing a loud sound.

Summary

  • Reflection occurs when waves bounce off boundaries; the law of reflection states that angle of incidence equals angle of reflection.
  • Specular reflection from smooth surfaces creates clear images, while diffuse reflection from rough surfaces scatters waves in all directions.
  • Refraction is the bending of waves as they enter a medium where they travel at different speeds; waves bend toward the normal when entering slower media.
  • Snell's Law ($n_1 \sin\theta_1 = n_2 \sin\theta_2$) mathematically describes refraction at the boundary between two media.
  • Diffraction is the spreading of waves around obstacles or through openings; significant diffraction occurs when wavelength is comparable to obstacle/opening size.
  • Sound diffracts more than light because sound wavelengths are much larger, explaining why we can hear but not see around corners.
  • Interference occurs when waves overlap; the principle of superposition states that displacements add algebraically.
  • Constructive interference (waves in phase) produces larger amplitude; occurs when path difference is an integer multiple of wavelength ($n\lambda$).
  • Destructive interference (waves out of phase) produces smaller amplitude; occurs when path difference is a half-integer multiple of wavelength ($(n+1/2)\lambda$).
  • Standing waves form when waves reflect and interfere with themselves, creating stationary patterns with nodes (zero amplitude) and antinodes (maximum amplitude).
  • Applications include architectural acoustics, noise-cancelling headphones, fiber optic cables, ultrasound imaging, and anti-reflective coatings.

Answer Key

1. You can hear someone in the next room because of diffraction—sound waves spread around obstacles and through openings like doorways. Sound has much longer wavelengths (centimeters to meters) compared to doorway sizes, so significant diffraction occurs, allowing sound to bend around corners and spread into adjacent rooms. Light has extremely short wavelengths (hundreds of nanometers), much smaller than doorways, so it diffracts very little and travels in straighter lines. This is why sound easily reaches you around corners while light does not.

2. During constructive interference, two waves arrive at a point with their crests and troughs aligned (in phase). According to the principle of superposition, the displacements add together. If two identical waves each have amplitude $A$, constructive interference produces a resulting amplitude of $2A$—twice as large as either individual wave. The waves reinforce each other, creating a stronger combined disturbance. This is heard as louder sound or seen as brighter light.

3. This effect is caused by refraction—the bending of light waves as they pass from one medium (water) to another (air). Light travels at different speeds in water and air. When light from the submerged part of the pencil exits the water at an angle, it bends away from the normal (perpendicular) line because light travels faster in air than water. This bending causes the light rays to appear to come from a different location, making the pencil look bent or broken at the water's surface. The pencil itself is straight; it's the light path that bends.

4. In a standing wave, nodes are points of zero amplitude where destructive interference always occurs—particles at nodes remain stationary. Antinodes are points of maximum amplitude where constructive interference occurs—particles at antinodes oscillate with the largest displacement. On a vibrating guitar string, the nodes are located at the fixed ends where the string is held (bridge and fret/nut), and possibly at intermediate points for higher harmonics. Antinodes occur at points between nodes, where the string moves with maximum displacement. For the fundamental frequency, there is one antinode in the middle of the string.

This is a fundamental property of wave behavior called the principle of superposition.
