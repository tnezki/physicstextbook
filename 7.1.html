7.1 Electric Charge and Electric Force

Electric forces are everywhere in daily life. A balloon clings to a wall, clothes stick together in the dryer, and lightning jumps across the sky because electric charge creates forces that can attract or repel objects.
This section begins with familiar static-electricity experiences and then builds toward Coulomb’s Law, the algebraic model that describes how strongly charged objects push or pull on each other.
“Nothing happens until something moves.” — Albert Einstein
Why does this matter?

Every circuit, screen, battery, and lightning strike depends on electric charge. Understanding how charges interact helps explain both tiny particle behavior and large-scale technologies that power modern life.

Learning Targets

Students will describe electric charge and apply Coulomb's Law to analyze attraction, repulsion, and electric force between charged objects.

  • I can identify positive charge, negative charge, elementary charge, and the coulomb as the unit of charge.
  • I can determine whether two charged objects will attract or repel and explain why.

  • I can explain how changing charge magnitude or distance changes electric force.

Introduction

If you have ever rubbed a balloon on your hair and watched it stick to a wall, you have observed electric charge in action. At first, the effect seems small and almost like a trick. But the same type of interaction helps explain sparks from a doorknob, static cling in clothing, and even lightning in a thunderstorm.

Before physicists studied full electric circuits, they first had to understand a more basic question: why do some objects pull together while others push apart? The answer begins with charge. Objects can have positive charge, negative charge, or no net charge at all. When charges interact, they exert electric forces on each other.

In this section, we begin with the observable phenomenon of attraction and repulsion and then build toward the mathematical model that describes it. That model is Coulomb’s Law, $$F_e = k\frac{|q_1 q_2|}{r^2}$$ which shows that electric force depends on the size of the charges and the distance between them.

Key Idea: Electric charge is a property of matter that causes objects to experience electric forces. Like charges repel, opposite charges attract, and the strength of the force changes with both charge and distance.

History

Humans have noticed electric effects for thousands of years. The ancient Greeks observed that amber could attract small bits of straw after being rubbed. In fact, the word electric comes from the Greek word elektron, meaning amber.

In the 1700s, scientists began studying electric phenomena more carefully. Benjamin Franklin helped popularize ideas about positive and negative charge and famously investigated lightning as an electrical event. Although some details of his model were incomplete, his work helped connect natural events to scientific explanation.

Later, Charles-Augustin de Coulomb developed the mathematical relationship for electric force using careful experiments with charged objects. Coulomb’s Law showed that electric force becomes stronger when charges increase and weaker when distance increases. This law gave physics a way to move from observation to precise calculation.

Today, Coulomb’s Law remains one of the foundational ideas of electricity. It helps scientists and engineers understand how charged particles interact in everything from atoms to electronic devices.

Key Concepts

Electric Charge

Electric charge is a fundamental property of matter. Some particles carry positive charge, some carry negative charge, and some carry no charge at all. In ordinary matter, protons are positive, electrons are negative, and neutrons are neutral.

Important Idea:

Objects become charged when electrons are transferred. An object that gains extra electrons becomes negatively charged. An object that loses electrons becomes positively charged.

Charge is measured in coulombs (C). A very small amount of charge on a single proton or electron is called the elementary charge: $$e = 1.60 \times 10^{-19}\,\text{C}$$

Law of Electric Charges

Charges interact in predictable ways. Like charges repel, and opposite charges attract. This means two positive charges push away from each other, two negative charges also push away, and a positive charge and a negative charge pull toward each other.

Key Idea: The direction of the electric force depends on the signs of the charges, while the size of the force depends on the magnitudes of the charges and the distance between them.

Point Charges

In many physics problems, charged objects are treated as point charges. This means we assume the size of the object is small compared with the distance between the charges, so the entire charge can be treated as if it is concentrated at one point.

This simplification makes it possible to model electric interactions mathematically without describing the full shape of each object.

Coulomb’s Law

Coulomb’s Law gives the magnitude of the electric force between two point charges:

$$F_e = k\frac{|q_1 q_2|}{r^2}$$

The force becomes larger when the charges are larger and smaller when the charges are farther apart.

The distance appears squared in the denominator, so electric force follows an inverse-square relationship. If the distance doubles, the force becomes one-fourth as large. If the distance triples, the force becomes one-ninth as large.

Attraction, Repulsion, and Force Direction

Coulomb’s Law gives the magnitude of the force. The sign of the charges tells us the direction of the interaction. Opposite charges produce an attractive force, while like charges produce a repulsive force.

When solving a problem, it is often helpful to calculate the magnitude first and then state whether the force is attractive or repulsive.

Comparing Scenarios

Physicists often compare one electric-force situation to another without finding an exact numerical answer. This is useful for reasoning quickly.

Examples of proportional reasoning:

  • If one charge doubles and everything else stays the same, the force doubles.
  • If both charges double, the force becomes four times as large.
  • If the distance doubles, the force becomes one-fourth as large.
  • If the distance is cut in half, the force becomes four times as large.

Worked Derivation

Coulomb’s Law is an experimental law, but we can still reason through its structure. Experiments show that electric force behaves in two important ways:

  1. The force gets larger when either charge gets larger, so $F_e \propto q_1 q_2$.
  2. The force gets smaller as distance increases, and experiments show the relationship is inverse-square, so $F_e \propto \frac{1}{r^2}$.

Combining these two proportional relationships gives: $$F_e \propto \frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2}$$ To turn this proportional relationship into an equation, physics uses a constant of proportionality: $$F_e = k\frac{|q_1 q_2|}{r^2}$$

Why this matters: The form of the equation is not random. It reflects real experimental patterns in how electric forces behave.

Strategies

Given → Find → Equation → Solve → Check Units

  1. Given: List the known charges, distance, and signs of the charges.
  2. Find: Decide whether you are finding force magnitude, force type, or comparing scenarios.
  3. Equation: Use Coulomb’s Law, $$F_e = k\frac{|q_1 q_2|}{r^2}$$
  4. Solve: Substitute carefully using scientific notation and square the distance correctly.
  5. Check Units: The final unit of force should be newtons (N).

Common Mistakes

  • Sign confusion: Students may calculate the magnitude correctly but state the wrong interaction. Like charges repel; opposite charges attract.
  • Forgetting to square the distance: Coulomb’s Law uses $r^2$, not just $r$.
  • Unit mismatch: Distance must be in meters, not centimeters or millimeters.
  • Scientific notation errors: A small exponent mistake can change the answer by a factor of 10 or more.
  • Confusing charge with force: Charge is measured in coulombs; force is measured in newtons.
  • Ignoring the word “magnitude”: Magnitude is the size of the force only. Attraction or repulsion must still be stated separately.

Summary

  • Electric charge is a property of matter that produces electric forces.
  • Like charges repel, and opposite charges attract.
  • Coulomb’s Law models the electric force between two point charges.
  • Electric force increases with larger charges.
  • Electric force decreases with greater distance according to an inverse-square relationship.
  • Charge is measured in coulombs, and force is measured in newtons.

Detailed Example Solutions

Example 1 — Single-Formula

Question: Two point charges of $2.5 \times 10^{-6}\,\text{C}$ and $4.0 \times 10^{-6}\,\text{C}$ are separated by $0.60\,\text{m}$. What is the electric force between them?

Given: $q_1 = 2.5 \times 10^{-6}\,\text{C}$, $q_2 = 4.0 \times 10^{-6}\,\text{C}$, $r = 0.60\,\text{m}$

Equation:

$$F_e = k\frac{|q_1 q_2|}{r^2}$$

Substitute:

$$F_e = (8.99 \times 10^9)\frac{(2.5 \times 10^{-6})(4.0 \times 10^{-6})}{(0.60)^2}$$

Solve:

$$F_e = 0.250\,\text{N}$$

Final Answer: $\boxed{0.250\,\text{N}}$

Example 2 — Multi-Step Scenario

Question: Two charges of $5.0\,\mu\text{C}$ and $3.0\,\mu\text{C}$ are separated by $0.50\,\text{m}$. (a) Calculate the electric force between them. (b) If the distance is doubled, what is the new force?

Part (a) Given: $q_1 = 5.0 \times 10^{-6}\,\text{C}$, $q_2 = 3.0 \times 10^{-6}\,\text{C}$, $r = 0.50\,\text{m}$

Equation:

$$F_e = k\frac{|q_1 q_2|}{r^2}$$

Substitute:

$$F_e = (8.99 \times 10^9)\frac{(5.0 \times 10^{-6})(3.0 \times 10^{-6})}{(0.50)^2}$$

Solve:

$$F_e = 0.539\,\text{N}$$

Part (a) Answer: $\boxed{0.539\,\text{N}}$

Part (b): If the distance doubles, the force becomes one-fourth as large because force is inversely proportional to $r^2$.

$$F_{\text{new}} = \frac{0.539}{4} = 0.135\,\text{N}$$

Part (b) Answer: $\boxed{0.135\,\text{N}}$

Example 3 — Multi-Step Scenario

Question: Two point charges are $0.25\,\text{m}$ apart and experience a force of $0.86\,\text{N}$. One charge is $3.0\,\mu\text{C}$. What is the magnitude of the second charge?

Given: $F_e = 0.86\,\text{N}$, $r = 0.25\,\text{m}$, $q_1 = 3.0 \times 10^{-6}\,\text{C}$

Start with Coulomb’s Law:

$$F_e = k\frac{|q_1 q_2|}{r^2}$$

Solve for the unknown charge:

$$q_2 = \frac{F_e r^2}{k q_1}$$

Substitute:

$$q_2 = \frac{(0.86)(0.25)^2}{(8.99 \times 10^9)(3.0 \times 10^{-6})}$$

Solve:

$$q_2 = 2.0 \times 10^{-6}\,\text{C}$$

Final Answer: $\boxed{2.0 \times 10^{-6}\,\text{C}}$
