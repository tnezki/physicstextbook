SECTION 5.5

Wave Interference

When two waves meet in the same medium, they don't simply pass through each other unchanged. Instead, they interact—interfere—to create new patterns. From the dramatic amplification of sound in concert halls to the complete silence of noise-canceling headphones, wave interference shapes our world. Discover how the alignment of waves determines whether they build each other up or cancel out entirely.

Learning Objectives

  • I can identify and predict constructive and destructive interference based on phase relationships between waves.
  • I can calculate resultant amplitude when two waves of known amplitude interfere.
  • I can explain how standing waves form through wave interference and identify nodes and antinodes.
  • I can apply interference concepts to real-world phenomena including acoustics, noise cancellation, and musical instruments.

Introduction

Have you ever noticed that the sound in some parts of a concert hall is louder than in other parts? Or that when you stand in certain spots near a speaker, the bass seems to disappear? These phenomena are caused by wave interference—the interaction of two or more waves in the same medium. Understanding wave interference is essential for explaining everything from acoustics in buildings to how musical instruments produce specific notes to how noise-canceling technology works.

When two waves meet, they don't bounce off each other or separate unaffected. Instead, they overlap in space, and their amplitudes add together at each point. This simple principle—that amplitudes add—leads to two dramatically different outcomes: sometimes waves reinforce each other, creating a larger wave, and sometimes they cancel each other, reducing amplitude to zero. The difference depends on how the waves are aligned relative to each other.

Real-World Application: Noise-canceling headphones use destructive interference to eliminate unwanted sound. A microphone detects incoming noise, and the headphone generates an "opposite" sound wave that cancels it out. Airline pilots rely on this technology during long flights. Modern active noise cancellation uses the principle of destructive interference to reduce cabin noise by up to 20 decibels.

When Waves Meet: Wave Interference

What Is Wave Interference?

Wave interference is the phenomenon that occurs when two or more waves pass through the same region of space at the same time. The key principle is the superposition principle: when waves overlap, the displacement at any point is the sum of the displacements from each individual wave.

Imagine two waves on a rope moving toward each other. When they meet, the rope at that instant experiences displacement from both waves simultaneously. If one wave pushes the rope up and the other pushes it down at the same location, the effects partially or completely cancel. If both waves push the rope up, their effects add together, creating a larger displacement.

Importantly, wave interference is not permanent. After the waves pass through each other, they continue unchanged. The interference only affects the pattern while the waves overlap. This is fundamentally different from collisions between objects, where energy is transferred or lost. Waves pass through each other completely unaffected by their interaction.

Key Principle: The superposition principle states that when two or more waves occupy the same space, the resulting displacement equals the algebraic sum of the individual displacements. This applies whether displacements are in the same direction (adding) or opposite directions (canceling).

Constructive Interference

Constructive interference occurs when waves overlap in such a way that their amplitudes add together, producing a wave with greater amplitude than either original wave.

Condition for Constructive Interference: Constructive interference occurs when the crests of one wave align with the crests of another wave, and the troughs align with troughs. In other words, the waves must be in phase—their peaks and valleys occur at the same locations.

For example, if Wave A has amplitude 3 m and Wave B also has amplitude 3 m, and they interfere constructively, the resulting wave has amplitude 6 m. The waves reinforce each other completely.

Destructive Interference

Destructive interference occurs when waves overlap in such a way that their amplitudes partially or completely cancel, producing a wave with smaller amplitude than the larger of the original waves.

Condition for Destructive Interference: Destructive interference occurs when the crests of one wave align with the troughs of another wave. In other words, the waves must be out of phase—their peaks and valleys occur at opposite locations.

When two waves of equal amplitude and opposite phase interfere destructively, they can completely cancel each other out, producing zero amplitude at that location. If Wave A has amplitude 3 m and Wave B has amplitude 3 m but is inverted, the result is complete cancellation.

Phase: The Key to Interference

Understanding Phase Relationships

The outcome of wave interference depends entirely on the phase relationship between the waves—whether their peaks and valleys align or oppose each other.

Phase can be expressed as a phase difference, measured in degrees (0° to 360°) or radians (0 to 2π). Two waves are:

  • In phase (phase difference = 0°) when crests align with crests and troughs with troughs → constructive interference
  • Completely out of phase (phase difference = 180°) when crests align with troughs → destructive interference
  • Partially out of phase (phase difference between 0° and 180°) → partial cancellation

Path Difference and Phase

When two waves originate from different sources and travel different distances to meet at a point, their phase difference depends on the path difference (the difference in distances traveled).

The relationship is:

$$\text{Path Difference} = n \lambda$$

Where n is an integer (0, 1, 2, 3...) and λ is wavelength.

  • When path difference = 0λ, λ, 2λ, 3λ... (whole number of wavelengths) → constructive interference
  • When path difference = 0.5λ, 1.5λ, 2.5λ, 3.5λ... (odd number of half-wavelengths) → destructive interference

Application in Noise Cancellation: Noise-canceling headphones measure the incoming noise wave and generate a second wave that is exactly 180° out of phase (path difference = 0.5λ). When these two waves meet at your ear, destructive interference eliminates the noise. This requires precise timing—the generated wave must arrive exactly half a wavelength "late" relative to the incoming noise.

Standing Waves: Interference Creates Stationary Patterns

What Are Standing Waves?

Standing waves (also called stationary waves) are wave patterns that appear to remain in place rather than traveling. They form when two waves of the same frequency and amplitude travel in opposite directions and interfere.

The most common way standing waves form is when a wave reflects off a fixed boundary. The original wave and its reflection interfere, creating a pattern where some locations have maximum amplitude (antinodes) and others have zero amplitude (nodes).

Nodes and Antinodes

A node is a point of destructive interference where the medium never moves—the amplitude is always zero. At nodes, the original wave and reflected wave are always 180° out of phase.

An antinode is a point of constructive interference where the amplitude is maximum. At antinodes, the original wave and reflected wave are always in phase.

Resonance and Standing Waves

When a system is excited at its resonant frequency, standing waves form with maximum amplitude. This occurs when the driving frequency matches the natural frequency of the system.

For a string fixed at both ends, resonance occurs when:

$$L = n \frac{\lambda}{2}$$

Where L is the length of the string, n is a positive integer (1, 2, 3...), and λ is wavelength.

This means the length of the string must equal an integer number of half-wavelengths. Different values of n correspond to different harmonics or modes of vibration.

Musical Instruments and Standing Waves: Guitar strings, violin strings, and piano strings all produce sound through standing waves. When you pluck a guitar string, you excite multiple standing wave modes simultaneously. The fundamental frequency (lowest frequency, n=1) determines the note you hear, while higher harmonics add richness and color to the sound. This is why a piano sounds different from a guitar even when playing the same note—the relative amplitudes of the harmonics differ.

Example Problems

Example 1: Calculating Resultant Amplitude from Constructive Interference

Given:

  • Two waves interfere constructively
  • Wave 1 amplitude: $$A_1 = 2.0 \text{ m}$$
  • Wave 2 amplitude: $$A_2 = 3.0 \text{ m}$$
  • Both waves are in phase (crests align with crests)

Find: What is the amplitude of the resulting wave?

Solution:

Step 1: Identify the type of interference and the principle to use.

Since the waves are in phase, they undergo constructive interference. The amplitudes add directly.

Step 2: Add the amplitudes.

$$A_{\text{result}} = A_1 + A_2 = 2.0 \text{ m} + 3.0 \text{ m} = 5.0 \text{ m}$$

Answer: The resultant amplitude is 5.0 m.

Physical Interpretation: When waves are perfectly in phase, their amplitudes add completely. The resulting wave is larger than either original wave, with maximum possible amplitude for constructive interference.

Example 2: Calculating Resultant Amplitude from Destructive Interference

Given:

  • Two waves interfere destructively
  • Wave 1 amplitude: $$A_1 = 5.0 \text{ m}$$
  • Wave 2 amplitude: $$A_2 = 3.0 \text{ m}$$
  • Waves are 180° out of phase (crests align with troughs)

Find: What is the amplitude of the resulting wave?

Solution:

Step 1: Identify the type of interference.

Since the waves are 180° out of phase, they undergo destructive interference. The smaller amplitude is subtracted from the larger.

Step 2: Subtract the amplitudes.

$$A_{\text{result}} = A_1 - A_2 = 5.0 \text{ m} - 3.0 \text{ m} = 2.0 \text{ m}$$

Answer: The resultant amplitude is 2.0 m.

Physical Interpretation: The larger wave partially cancels the smaller wave. The result is still a wave, but with reduced amplitude. If both waves had equal amplitude (5.0 m each), complete cancellation would occur and the amplitude would be zero.

Example 3: Multi-Step Standing Wave Problem

Given:

  • A guitar string is fixed at both ends
  • String length: L = 0.65 m
  • The string vibrates at its fundamental frequency (first harmonic, n=1)
  • Speed of waves on the string: v = 130 m/s

Find:

  • a) What is the wavelength of the standing wave?
  • b) What is the frequency of vibration?
  • c) If the same string vibrates in the second harmonic (n=2), what are the new wavelength and frequency?

Solution:

Part a) Wavelength for Fundamental Frequency

Step 1: Use the standing wave condition: $$L = n \frac{\lambda}{2}$$

For the fundamental frequency, n = 1:

$$L = 1 \times \frac{\lambda}{2}$$

$$\lambda = 2L = 2(0.65 \text{ m}) = 1.30 \text{ m}$$

Answer (a): λ = 1.30 m

Part b) Frequency for Fundamental Frequency

Step 1: Use the wave equation: $$v = f\lambda$$

$$f = \frac{v}{\lambda} = \frac{130 \text{ m/s}}{1.30 \text{ m}} = 100 \text{ Hz}$$

Answer (b): f = 100 Hz

Part c) Second Harmonic (n=2)

Wavelength for n=2:

$$L = 2 \times \frac{\lambda}{2} = \lambda = 0.65 \text{ m}$$

Frequency for n=2:

$$f = \frac{v}{\lambda} = \frac{130 \text{ m/s}}{0.65 \text{ m}} = 200 \text{ Hz}$$

Answer (c): For the second harmonic, λ = 0.65 m and f = 200 Hz

Physical Insight: Notice that the frequency of the second harmonic (200 Hz) is exactly twice the fundamental frequency (100 Hz). The wavelength is halved. The string now has two antinodes instead of one, creating two "bumps" instead of one. This is why the second harmonic sounds higher pitched than the fundamental.

Summary

  • Wave interference occurs when two or more waves overlap in the same space, and their displacements add according to the superposition principle.
  • Constructive interference happens when waves are in phase (crests align with crests), causing amplitudes to add and resulting in larger amplitude than either original wave.
  • Destructive interference happens when waves are out of phase (crests align with troughs), causing amplitudes to partially or completely cancel, resulting in smaller amplitude.
  • Constructive interference occurs when path difference = $$n \lambda$$ (whole number of wavelengths); destructive interference occurs when path difference = $$(n + 0.5) \lambda$$ (odd half-wavelengths).
  • Phase difference determines the type of interference. Waves 0° out of phase interfere constructively; waves 180° out of phase interfere destructively.
  • Standing waves form when a wave and its reflection interfere. They appear stationary, with fixed points of zero amplitude (nodes) and maximum amplitude (antinodes).
  • A node is a point of permanent destructive interference where the medium never moves; an antinode is a point of permanent constructive interference with maximum amplitude.
  • For a string fixed at both ends, standing waves form when $$L = n\frac{\lambda}{2}$$, where n is the harmonic number (1, 2, 3...).
  • Harmonics are the different standing wave patterns that can form on a string. The fundamental (n=1) has lowest frequency; higher harmonics have frequencies that are integer multiples of the fundamental.
  • Wave interference has practical applications in noise cancellation, acoustics of concert halls, design of musical instruments, and understanding how resonance works.

Answer Key

Note to Teachers: This answer key provides complete solutions for all practice problems. Partial credit should be awarded based on correct understanding of interference principles and proper application of formulas.

Conceptual Questions (1-4)

Answer 1: Superposition Principle

Key Points for a Complete Answer:

  • The superposition principle states that when waves overlap, the displacement at each point equals the sum of individual wave displacements.
  • After the waves pass through each other, each wave continues with its original properties (amplitude, frequency, wavelength) completely unchanged.
  • The interference is temporary and local—it only affects the pattern while the waves overlap in space.
  • This is fundamentally different from collisions between objects, where energy is transferred or lost. Waves do not collide; they pass through each other.
  • Example: Two people can shout at the same time in the same room—their voices overlap (interfere) but each person's voice continues unchanged after passing through the space where they met.

Answer 2: Phase and Interference Type

Correct Response:

The phase relationship between two waves determines whether they interfere constructively or destructively. If waves arrive at the same point with crests and troughs aligned (in phase), they interfere constructively. If crests align with troughs (out of phase by 180°), they interfere destructively.

Yes, the same two waves can interfere both constructively and destructively at different locations. For example, ripples from two raindrops create regions of constructive interference (where the water surface is higher) and regions of destructive interference (where the water is nearly flat). The type depends on the path difference—if each source is a whole wavelength closer, you get constructive interference; if one source is a half-wavelength closer, you get destructive interference.

Answer 3: Nodes and Antinodes

Correct Response:

At nodes, destructive interference is permanent. The incident wave and reflected wave always arrive 180° out of phase at that location, so their amplitudes constantly cancel. The rope at a node never moves.

At antinodes, constructive interference is permanent. The incident wave and reflected wave always arrive in phase at that location, so their amplitudes constantly add. The rope at an antinode oscillates with the largest amplitude.

A standing wave consists of alternating nodes and antinodes due to the specific path lengths from the reflection point. This creates a "frozen" wave pattern.

Answer 4: Noise-Canceling Headphones

Key Points for a Complete Answer:

  • The headphones generate sound waves that are 180° out of phase (crests align with troughs) of the incoming noise.
  • When these two waves meet at your ear, destructive interference occurs, and the amplitudes cancel.
  • The result is silence—the unwanted noise is eliminated.
  • Limitations: This only works well at low frequencies with long wavelengths. High-frequency noise is harder to cancel. Headphones create a "quiet zone" only in your ears, not everywhere.

Calculation Problems (5-6)

Answer 5: Constructive Interference

Given: $$A_1 = 4.0 \text{ m}, A_2 = 6.0 \text{ m}$$, constructive interference
Solution:
$$A_{\text{result}} = A_1 + A_2 = 4.0 \text{ m} + 6.0 \text{ m} = 10.0 \text{ m}$$

Answer: $$A_{\text{result}} = 10.0 \text{ m}$$

Answer 6: Destructive Interference

Given: $$A_1 = 7.0 \text{ m}, A_2 = 4.0 \text{ m}$$, destructive interference
Solution:
$$A_{\text{result}} = |A_1 - A_2| = |7.0 \text{ m} - 4.0 \text{ m}| = 3.0 \text{ m}$$

Answer: $$A_{\text{result}} = 3.0 \text{ m}$$

Multi-Step Problems (7-8)

Answer 7: Standing Wave on a Rope

Given: L = 2.0 m, v = 100 m/s, fundamental frequency (n=1)

Part a) Wavelength

$$L = n\frac{\lambda}{2}$$
For n=1: $$\lambda = 2L = 2(2.0 \text{ m}) = 4.0 \text{ m}$$
Answer (a): λ = 4.0 m

Part b) Frequency

$$f = \frac{v}{\lambda} = \frac{100 \text{ m/s}}{4.0 \text{ m}} = 25 \text{ Hz}$$
Answer (b): f = 25 Hz

Part c) Number of Antinodes

For the fundamental frequency (n=1), there is exactly 1 antinode at the center of the rope. Nodes exist at both ends (fixed boundaries).

Answer 8: Path Difference and Interference

Given: f = 1000 Hz, v = 340 m/s, $$d_1 = 2.0 \text{ m}, d_2 = 2.17 \text{ m}$$

Part a) Wavelength

$$\lambda = \frac{v}{f} = \frac{340 \text{ m/s}}{1000 \text{ Hz}} = 0.34 \text{ m}$$
Answer (a): λ = 0.34 m

Part b) Path Difference

$$\Delta d = |d_2 - d_1| = |2.17 \text{ m} - 2.0 \text{ m}| = 0.17 \text{ m}$$
Answer (b): Path difference = 0.17 m

Part c) Type of Interference

Step 1: Compare path difference to wavelength:
$$\frac{\Delta d}{\lambda} = \frac{0.17 \text{ m}}{0.34 \text{ m}} = 0.5$$

The path difference equals 0.5λ (one half-wavelength).

Step 2: Determine interference type:
Path difference = 0.5λ corresponds to destructive interference.

The listener will experience destructive interference, resulting in reduced sound intensity. The sound waves from the two speakers partially cancel at point P because one wave arrives half a wavelength "behind" the other.
