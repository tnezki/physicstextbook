SECTION 4.1

Momentum and Impulse

Every moving object carries momentum—a combination of its mass and velocity that determines how hard it is to stop. From a football tackle to a car crash, understanding momentum helps us predict what happens when objects collide. In this section, you'll discover how forces change momentum over time through a concept called impulse.

Learning Objectives

  • I can define momentum and explain why it depends on both mass and velocity
  • I can calculate the momentum of moving objects using the formula p = mv
  • I can explain impulse as the change in momentum and relate it to force and time
  • I can apply the impulse-momentum theorem to solve problems involving forces acting over time

Introduction

Imagine watching a bowling ball roll down a lane toward the pins. Even before it hits, you know something dramatic is about to happen. The ball carries something with it—something that will send pins flying in all directions. That "something" is momentum.

Momentum is one of the most important concepts in physics because it helps us understand collisions and impacts. Whether it's a baseball bat hitting a ball, two cars colliding at an intersection, or a rocket launching into space, momentum plays a central role. Unlike velocity, which only tells us how fast something is moving, momentum combines both speed and mass to give us a complete picture of an object's motion.

In this section, we'll explore how momentum is defined, calculated, and changed by forces. You'll learn why a slow-moving freight train is harder to stop than a fast-moving bicycle, and why airbags save lives by extending the time of a collision. These concepts connect directly to real-world safety features in cars, protective equipment in sports, and even the design of spacecraft.

Real-World Connection: Vehicle Safety

Modern cars are designed with "crumple zones" that collapse during a crash. While this might seem counterintuitive—why would we want parts of the car to crumple?—it's actually a brilliant application of the impulse-momentum theorem. By extending the time of the collision, crumple zones reduce the force experienced by passengers, making crashes more survivable. This same principle explains why airbags, padded dashboards, and seat belts are so effective at protecting people.

What is Momentum?

Momentum is a measure of an object's motion that depends on both its mass and velocity. An object with large momentum is difficult to stop, while an object with small momentum is easy to stop. Mathematically, momentum is defined as the product of mass and velocity.

Definition: Momentum

Momentum (p) is the product of an object's mass and velocity. It is a vector quantity, meaning it has both magnitude and direction. The SI unit of momentum is kilogram-meters per second (kg·m/s).

Formula: $p = mv$

where $p$ = momentum (kg·m/s), $m$ = mass (kg), and $v$ = velocity (m/s)

Because velocity is a vector (it has direction), momentum is also a vector. This means that the direction matters: an object moving north has different momentum than the same object moving south at the same speed. The negative sign in momentum calculations indicates direction opposite to the positive reference direction.

Factors Affecting Momentum

Two factors determine an object's momentum: mass and velocity. Let's consider how each affects momentum:

Mass: A more massive object has greater momentum than a less massive object moving at the same speed. This is why a slow-moving truck is harder to stop than a fast-moving bicycle. Even though the bicycle might be moving faster, the truck's enormous mass gives it much greater momentum.

Velocity: A faster-moving object has greater momentum than a slower-moving object with the same mass. Doubling the velocity doubles the momentum. This is why a baseball thrown by a professional pitcher is so much more dangerous than one tossed gently—same ball, but vastly different momentum.

Important Note: Momentum is a Vector

Because momentum depends on velocity (which is a vector), momentum itself is a vector. This means you must keep track of direction. In one-dimensional problems, we use positive and negative signs to indicate direction. For example, if we define east as positive, then an object moving west would have negative velocity and therefore negative momentum.

What is Impulse?

When a force acts on an object, it changes the object's momentum. The change in momentum is called impulse. Impulse depends on two things: how large the force is, and how long it acts.

Definition: Impulse

Impulse (J) is the change in an object's momentum, equal to the product of the average force and the time interval during which the force acts. Impulse is also a vector quantity with units of newton-seconds (N·s), which is equivalent to kg·m/s.

Impulse Formula: $J = F\Delta t$

where $J$ = impulse (N·s or kg·m/s), $F$ = average force (N), and $\Delta t$ = time interval (s)

Impulse can also be defined as the change in momentum:

$J = \Delta p = p_f - p_i = mv_f - mv_i$

where $p_i$ is initial momentum and $p_f$ is final momentum.

The Impulse-Momentum Theorem

The impulse-momentum theorem states that the impulse on an object equals its change in momentum. This gives us a powerful relationship:

Impulse-Momentum Theorem

$$F\Delta t = \Delta p = mv_f - mv_i$$

The impulse (force × time) equals the change in momentum

This theorem tells us something very important: the same change in momentum can be produced by either a large force acting for a short time, or a small force acting for a long time. This principle is used in many safety applications.

Historical Note: Isaac Newton and Momentum

Sir Isaac Newton (1643-1727) originally formulated his second law of motion in terms of momentum rather than acceleration. In his masterwork Principia Mathematica, Newton described how forces produce changes in momentum. While we often write Newton's second law as F = ma, his original formulation was closer to F = Δp/Δt, emphasizing that force causes changes in momentum over time.

Applications of Impulse

Extending Time to Reduce Force

According to the impulse-momentum theorem, if you increase the time interval $\Delta t$, you can decrease the force $F$ needed to produce the same change in momentum. This principle is used in many safety devices:

  • Airbags: Increase the time of collision, reducing the force on passengers
  • Crumple zones: Allow the car to deform over a longer time during a crash
  • Padded gym mats: Extend the time of impact when landing, reducing injury
  • Bending knees when landing: Athletes extend the stopping time to reduce impact forces on joints

Increasing Force by Decreasing Time

Conversely, decreasing the time interval increases the force. This principle is used when we want to deliver maximum force:

  • Karate chop: Quickly stopping the hand creates a large force on the target
  • Baseball bat swing: The brief contact time with the ball creates a large force
  • Hammer blow: Short impact time produces large force on the nail

Common Misconception

Students sometimes think that momentum and kinetic energy are the same thing. They are not! Momentum is $p = mv$ (a vector), while kinetic energy is $KE = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$ (a scalar). Momentum depends linearly on velocity, but kinetic energy depends on velocity squared. An object can have momentum without having much kinetic energy (like a slow-moving truck), or have kinetic energy without much momentum (like a fast-moving ping-pong ball).

Example Problems

Problem-Solving Strategy

Follow these five steps for every problem:

  1. List the Givens: Write down all known values with units
  2. Find the Formula: Choose the appropriate equation
  3. Substitute: Plug in the known values
  4. Solve: Perform the calculation
  5. Check: Verify units and reasonableness of answer

Example 1: Calculating Momentum

Problem: A 1200 kg car travels east at 25 m/s. What is the car's momentum?

Step 1 - List the Givens:

  • $m = 1200$ kg (mass of car)
  • $v = 25$ m/s (velocity, eastward)
  • Find: $p$ = ?

Step 2 - Find the Formula:

$p = mv$

Step 3 - Substitute:

$p = (1200 \text{ kg})(25 \text{ m/s})$

Step 4 - Solve:

$p = 30{,}000$ kg·m/s

Step 5 - Check:

Units are correct (kg·m/s). The answer is reasonable—a car has significant momentum.

Answer: The car's momentum is 30,000 kg·m/s eastward.

Example 2: Calculating Impulse

Problem: A tennis racket exerts an average force of 400 N on a tennis ball for 0.008 seconds. What impulse does the racket deliver to the ball?

Step 1 - List the Givens:

  • $F = 400$ N (average force)
  • $\Delta t = 0.008$ s (time interval)
  • Find: $J$ = ?

Step 2 - Find the Formula:

$J = F\Delta t$

Step 3 - Substitute:

$J = (400 \text{ N})(0.008 \text{ s})$

Step 4 - Solve:

$J = 3.2$ N·s

Step 5 - Check:

Units are correct (N·s, which equals kg·m/s). The impulse is small, which makes sense for a light tennis ball.

Answer: The racket delivers an impulse of 3.2 N·s to the ball.

Example 3: Multi-Step Problem Using Impulse-Momentum Theorem

Problem: A 0.15 kg baseball is pitched at 40 m/s toward a batter. The batter hits the ball, sending it back toward the pitcher at 50 m/s. The bat is in contact with the ball for 0.002 seconds. (a) What is the change in the ball's momentum? (b) What average force did the bat exert on the ball?

Step 1 - List the Givens:

  • $m = 0.15$ kg (mass of baseball)
  • $v_i = 40$ m/s (initial velocity, toward batter—we'll call this positive)
  • $v_f = -50$ m/s (final velocity, toward pitcher—opposite direction, so negative)
  • $\Delta t = 0.002$ s (contact time)
  • Find: (a) $\Delta p$ = ? and (b) $F$ = ?

Part (a): Change in Momentum

Step 2 - Find the Formula:

$\Delta p = mv_f - mv_i = m(v_f - v_i)$

Step 3 - Substitute:

$\Delta p = (0.15 \text{ kg})(-50 \text{ m/s} - 40 \text{ m/s})$

$\Delta p = (0.15 \text{ kg})(-90 \text{ m/s})$

Step 4 - Solve:

$\Delta p = -13.5$ kg·m/s

Step 5 - Check:

The negative sign indicates the momentum change is in the direction toward the pitcher, which makes sense.

Answer (a): The change in momentum is -13.5 kg·m/s (toward the pitcher).

Part (b): Average Force

Step 2 - Find the Formula:

$F\Delta t = \Delta p$, so $F = \frac{\Delta p}{\Delta t}$

Step 3 - Substitute:

$F = \frac{-13.5 \text{ kg·m/s}}{0.002 \text{ s}}$

Step 4 - Solve:

$F = -6750$ N

Step 5 - Check:

The large force makes sense for the brief contact time. The negative sign indicates the force is toward the pitcher (the direction the bat pushed the ball).

Answer (b): The average force exerted by the bat is 6750 N toward the pitcher.

Summary

  • Momentum is the product of an object's mass and velocity: $p = mv$. It is a vector quantity measured in kg·m/s.
  • Momentum depends on both mass and velocity—doubling either one doubles the momentum.
  • Because momentum is a vector, direction matters. We use positive and negative signs to indicate direction in one-dimensional motion.
  • Impulse is the change in momentum and equals the product of force and time: $J = F\Delta t$.
  • The impulse-momentum theorem states that impulse equals change in momentum: $F\Delta t = \Delta p = mv_f - mv_i$.
  • The same change in momentum can result from a large force acting for a short time or a small force acting for a long time.
  • Safety devices like airbags and crumple zones work by increasing collision time, thereby decreasing the force on passengers.
  • To maximize force (as in a karate chop or bat swing), minimize the time of contact.
  • Momentum and kinetic energy are different: momentum is $mv$ (vector), kinetic energy is $\frac{1}{2}mv^2$ (scalar).
  • The SI unit for impulse (N·s) is equivalent to the SI unit for momentum (kg·m/s).

Practice Problems

1. Define momentum in your own words and explain why it is a vector quantity.

2. Explain why a slow-moving freight train has more momentum than a fast-moving bicycle, even though the bicycle is moving much faster.

3. Describe how airbags use the impulse-momentum theorem to protect passengers during a car crash.

4. Two objects have the same momentum. If object A has twice the mass of object B, how do their velocities compare? Explain your reasoning.

5. A 60 kg person running at 8.0 m/s has how much momentum?

6. A hockey puck with mass 0.16 kg is struck by a stick that exerts an average force of 95 N for 0.050 seconds. What impulse is delivered to the puck?

7. A 0.45 kg soccer ball is at rest. A player kicks it, giving it a velocity of 20 m/s. If the player's foot is in contact with the ball for 0.025 seconds, what average force did the player's foot exert on the ball?

8. A 1500 kg car traveling at 30 m/s brakes and comes to a stop in 5.0 seconds. (a) What is the car's initial momentum? (b) What is the change in the car's momentum? (c) What average braking force is required to stop the car?

Answer Key

1. Momentum is a measure of how much motion an object has, combining both its mass and velocity. It is a vector quantity because it depends on velocity, which has direction. The direction of momentum is the same as the direction of velocity. An object's momentum tells us how hard it would be to stop the object or change its motion.

2. Momentum depends on both mass and velocity ($p = mv$). While the bicycle may have greater velocity, the freight train has much greater mass. The train's enormous mass more than compensates for its lower velocity, giving it much greater momentum overall. For example, a train with mass 100,000 kg moving at 2 m/s has momentum of 200,000 kg·m/s, while a 20 kg bicycle moving at 10 m/s has momentum of only 200 kg·m/s.

3. Airbags increase the time interval during which a passenger's momentum changes to zero during a crash. According to the impulse-momentum theorem ($F\Delta t = \Delta p$), if the time interval $\Delta t$ increases, the force $F$ decreases for the same change in momentum $\Delta p$. By extending the stopping time from milliseconds to tens of milliseconds, airbags significantly reduce the force experienced by passengers, reducing injuries.

4. Object B must have twice the velocity of object A. Since $p = mv$ and both objects have the same momentum, if object A has mass $2m$ and velocity $v$, then object B has mass $m$ and velocity $2v$. We can verify: $p_A = (2m)(v) = 2mv$ and $p_B = (m)(2v) = 2mv$. The lighter object must move faster to have the same momentum.

5.

Given: $m = 60$ kg, $v = 8.0$ m/s

Find: $p = ?$

Formula: $p = mv$

Substitute: $p = (60 \text{ kg})(8.0 \text{ m/s})$

Solve: $p = 480$ kg·m/s

Answer: 480 kg·m/s

6.

Given: $F = 95$ N, $\Delta t = 0.050$ s

Find: $J = ?$

Formula: $J = F\Delta t$

Substitute: $J = (95 \text{ N})(0.050 \text{ s})$

Solve: $J = 4.75$ N·s (or 4.75 kg·m/s)

Answer: 4.75 N·s

7.

Given: $m = 0.45$ kg, $v_i = 0$ m/s, $v_f = 20$ m/s, $\Delta t = 0.025$ s

Find: $F = ?$

Step 1 - Find change in momentum:

$\Delta p = m(v_f - v_i) = (0.45 \text{ kg})(20 \text{ m/s} - 0) = 9.0$ kg·m/s

Step 2 - Use impulse-momentum theorem:

$F\Delta t = \Delta p$, so $F = \frac{\Delta p}{\Delta t} = \frac{9.0 \text{ kg·m/s}}{0.025 \text{ s}} = 360$ N

Answer: 360 N

8.

Given: $m = 1500$ kg, $v_i = 30$ m/s, $v_f = 0$ m/s, $\Delta t = 5.0$ s

(a) Initial momentum:

$p_i = mv_i = (1500 \text{ kg})(30 \text{ m/s}) = 45{,}000$ kg·m/s

Answer (a): 45,000 kg·m/s

(b) Change in momentum:

$\Delta p = mv_f - mv_i = (1500 \text{ kg})(0) - 45{,}000 \text{ kg·m/s} = -45{,}000$ kg·m/s

(Negative because momentum decreases)

Answer (b): -45,000 kg·m/s

(c) Average braking force:

$F = \frac{\Delta p}{\Delta t} = \frac{-45{,}000 \text{ kg·m/s}}{5.0 \text{ s}} = -9000$ N

(Negative indicates force opposes motion)

Answer (c): 9000 N (opposite to the direction of motion)
