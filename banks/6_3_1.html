Critical Angle (Questions 1.1 – 1.3)
1.1
What is the critical angle?
A The angle at which light travels parallel to the interface between two materials
B The angle of incidence at which the refracted ray bends to exactly 90°, beyond which total internal reflection occurs
C The angle between the normal and the reflected ray inside a dense medium
D The maximum angle at which light can enter a medium without bending
E The angle of refraction when light passes from a less dense to a more dense medium
1.2
The specific angle of incidence at which the angle of refraction equals 90° is called the:
A Angle of reflection
B Angle of transmission
C Critical angle
D Normal angle
E Snell's angle
1.3
A laser beam inside a glass block is aimed toward the glass-air interface. As the angle of incidence is slowly increased, the refracted ray in the air bends farther and farther from the normal. The exact angle at which the refracted ray would travel along the surface (90° from normal) is the:
A Angle of total reflection
B Refractive angle
C Interface angle
D Critical angle
E Snell's limit
Total Internal Reflection (Questions 2.1 – 2.3)
2.1
What is total internal reflection?
A The partial reflection of light at the surface of any transparent material
B The complete reflection of light back into a denser medium when the angle of incidence exceeds the critical angle at an interface with a less dense medium
C The bending of light as it crosses the boundary between two materials of different densities
D The reflection of light when it strikes a perfectly smooth mirror surface
E The absorption of all light by an optically dense material
2.2
When light traveling inside a denser medium strikes the boundary with a less dense medium at an angle greater than the critical angle, the light is completely reflected back into the denser medium. This phenomenon is called:
A Diffuse reflection
B Specular refraction
C Total internal reflection
D Optical absorption
E Critical refraction
2.3
Fiber optic cables carry light signals over long distances by bouncing light repeatedly off the inner walls of the cable without any light escaping. Light stays inside because it continuously strikes the glass-air boundary at angles greater than the critical angle. This is an application of:
A Specular reflection
B Diffuse refraction
C Total internal reflection
D Critical angle absorption
E Snell's Law maximum
