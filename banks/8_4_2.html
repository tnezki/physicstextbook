Multiple Choice (A–E)
2.1
A circuit contains both series and parallel sections. What is the first step in analyzing it?
A) Measure voltage across each resistor
B) Increase battery voltage
C) Simplify sections to find equivalent resistance
D) Remove parallel branches
E) Reverse current direction

A) Ohm’s Law
B) Kirchhoff’s Law only
C) Power formula only
D) Voltage addition
E) Energy equation

A) Each component may drop a different portion of the total voltage
B) Voltage is always equal everywhere
C) Current doubles in series
D) Resistance disappears
E) Energy is destroyed

A) Total circuit resistance only
B) The voltage across the branch and its resistance
C) The number of resistors in series
D) The battery size only
E) The wire length only

A) It increases power output
B) It allows calculation of equivalent resistance progressively
C) It eliminates voltage drops
D) It removes current from the circuit
E) It doubles resistance

A) Total current decreases
B) Total current increases
C) Total current becomes zero
D) Total current reverses
E) Total current remains constant regardless of resistance

A) It becomes open
B) It can now be treated as a simpler series circuit
C) Current stops flowing
D) Voltage doubles
E) Resistance disappears

A) It will carry more current than other branches
B) It will carry no current
C) It will reduce battery voltage
D) It will eliminate total resistance
E) It will increase total resistance

In a complex circuit, the total voltage supplied by the battery equals:
A) The sum of voltage drops across all components
B) The highest branch voltage only
C) The lowest branch voltage only
D) Zero volts
E) Twice the current

A) To ensure components are not overloaded
B) To eliminate resistance
C) To double voltage
D) To reduce current to zero
E) To simplify the circuit

If two parallel branches are replaced by their equivalent resistance, what remains unchanged across that section?
A) Current in each branch
B) Voltage across the section
C) Power in each resistor
D) Individual resistances
E) Energy transfer
2.12
Explain why equivalent resistance in parallel is always less than the smallest branch resistance.
A) Parallel circuits increase voltage
B) Additional paths allow more current flow
C) Resistance adds directly
D) Current becomes zero
E) Energy doubles
2.13
A complex circuit contains a series resistor followed by a parallel section. Which part should be simplified first?
A) The series resistor
B) The parallel section
C) The battery
D) The wires
E) The highest resistance only

Explain why total current must be found before calculating individual voltage drops in series sections.
A) Voltage determines resistance
B) Series voltage depends on current through each resistor
C) Current is always constant in parallel
D) Resistance becomes zero
E) Energy is destroyed

A) It carries twice the current
B) It carries half the current
C) It carries the same current
D) It carries no current
E) It doubles voltage

A) It increases
B) It remains constant
C) It decreases
D) It becomes infinite

A) It equals 18 V
B) It is less than 18 V
C) It is greater than 18 V
D) It becomes zero

2.18

A) To eliminate resistance
B) To verify components stay within safe operating limits
C) To double voltage
D) To stop current flow
E) To simplify wires
2.19

A) It increases
B) It decreases
C) It remains constant
D) It becomes zero
E) It reverses

A) Total resistance increases
B) Excessive current flows through that branch
C) Voltage doubles everywhere

E) Resistance disappears everywhere
2.21

A) It increases voltage
B) It reduces calculation errors and clarifies relationships
C) It doubles resistance
D) It eliminates current
E) It increases energy transfer

A) The total current from the battery
B) The resistance of each branch individually
C) The internal battery resistance
D) The wire temperature
