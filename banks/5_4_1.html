Diffraction (1.1 – 1.4)
Definition: The spreading of a wave around obstacles and through openings; more pronounced when wavelength is large compared to the obstacle size.
1.1
What is diffraction?
A) Reflection of waves
B) Bending of waves around obstacles
C) Permanent energy loss
D) Wave speed change
E) Particle transfer
1.2
Wave spreading after passing through an opening is called:
A) Refraction
B) Interference
C) Diffraction
D) Reflection
E) Superposition
1.3
Low-frequency sound waves bend easily around corners. This demonstrates:
A) Refraction
B) Diffraction
C) Absorption
D) Polarization
E) Dispersion
1.4
Diffraction is most noticeable when:
A) Wavelength is small compared to obstacle
B) Wavelength is large compared to obstacle
C) Amplitude is zero
D) Speed is constant
E) Frequency is extremely high
Diffraction Pattern (2.1 – 2.4)
Definition: The pattern of wave intensity created when a wave spreads around an obstacle or through an opening.
2.1

A) A reflection of waves
B) A pattern formed by wave intensity after spreading

E) A permanent displacement

D) Node locations only

A) Dispersion
B) Diffraction pattern
C) Reflection
D) Polarization
E) Refraction
2.4

A) Amplitude only
B) Wavelength
C) Density only
D) Temperature only
E) Color of the screen
Interference (3.1 – 3.4)
Definition: The combination of two or more waves that interact and produce a new wave pattern.
3.1

A) Reflection of waves
B) Interaction of waves producing a new pattern
C) Wave absorption
D) Permanent matter transfer
E) Change in wave speed

A) Diffraction

E) Dispersion

A) Reflection
B) Interference

D) Permanent motion
E) Wave loss
3.4

A) Only reinforcement
B) Only cancellation
C) Both reinforcement and cancellation
D) No amplitude change
E) Permanent displacement
Superposition (4.1 – 4.4)
Definition: The principle that when two waves overlap, the resultant displacement at any point is the sum of the displacements from each wave.
4.1
What does the principle of superposition state?
A) Waves cancel permanently
B) Waves reflect off each other
C) Displacements add algebraically
D) Frequencies multiply
E) Wavelength doubles
4.2

A) Their speeds
B) Their wavelengths
C) The sum of individual displacements
D) The larger amplitude only
E) Their frequencies only
4.3

A) Double amplitude
B) Zero displacement
C) Triple amplitude
D) Increased speed
E) Increased frequency
4.4
Superposition applies when waves:
A) Travel in different media
B) Overlap in space
C) Have identical frequency only
D) Reflect
E) Stop moving
Constructive Interference (5.1 – 5.4)
Definition: Occurs when waves arrive in phase (crest to crest); amplitudes add to produce a larger combined amplitude.

A) Waves cancel completely
B) Amplitudes subtract
C) Amplitudes add
D) Frequency doubles
E) Speed decreases

B) In phase
C) Perpendicular
D) Reflected
E) Standing still

A) Zero amplitude
B) Reduced amplitude
C) Increased amplitude
D) Wavelength change
E) Frequency change
5.4

A) Crest meets trough
B) Crest meets crest
C) Rarefaction meets compression
D) Wave meets obstacle
E) Node meets antinode
Destructive Interference (6.1 – 6.4)
Definition: Occurs when waves arrive out of phase (crest aligned with trough); amplitudes subtract and may result in zero amplitude.

B) Amplitudes subtract

D) Speed increases

A) Double amplitude

C) Zero displacement
D) Increased wavelength
E) Increased frequency
6.4

A) Crest with crest
B) Trough with trough
C) Crest with trough
D) Node with node
E) Antinode with antinode

E) Refract

A) Constructive interference
B) No interaction
C) Destructive interference
D) Increased speed
E) Larger wavelength

Definition: A wave pattern that appears to stand still, formed when incident and reflected waves interfere.

E) Compressions

A) Transfer energy along the medium
B) Exhibit interference
C) Have nodes
D) Have antinodes
