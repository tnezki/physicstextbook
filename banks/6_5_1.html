Double Convex Lenses (Questions 1.1 - 1.4)
1.1
What is the definition of double convex lenses?
A Lenses that are thinner in the middle and thicker at the edges
B Lenses that are thicker in the middle and thinner at the edges
C Lenses that have a flat surface on one side
D Lenses that do not bend light rays
E Lenses that only work for distant objects
1.2
Lenses that are thicker in the middle and thinner at the edges, and bend light rays toward each other, are called:
A Diverging lenses
B Concave lenses
C Double convex lenses
D Plane lenses
E Chromatic lenses
1.3
A magnifying glass used to examine small insect specimens is typically a double convex lens because it:
A Scatters light in all directions
B Bends light rays toward each other to create a magnified image
C Only works with sunlight
D Decreases the size of objects viewed
E Changes the color of objects being examined
1.4
When light passes through a double convex lens, its rays bend inward because:
A The lens absorbs light energy
B The lens surface curves outward, slowing light and causing convergence
C Light travels in curved paths naturally
D The lens reflects all light toward a focal point
E Double convex lenses polarize light waves
Converging Lenses (Questions 2.1 - 2.4)
2.1
What is the definition of converging lenses?
A Lenses that spread light rays apart from each other
B Lenses that bend light rays toward each other, causing them to converge at a focal point
C Lenses that do not bend light rays at all
D Lenses that only magnify objects very close to the lens
E Lenses that only produce real images
2.2
Lenses that bring light rays together and have a positive focal length are called:
A Diverging lenses
B Converging lenses
C Plane lenses
D Concentric lenses
E Reflective lenses
2.3
A camera lens that focuses light from a scene onto film or a digital sensor is most likely a converging lens because:
A It spreads light over a large area inside the camera
B It brings scattered light rays together to form a sharp, focused image
C It blocks excess light from entering the camera body
D It reverses the direction of incoming light rays
E It always produces virtual images on the film
2.4
Converging lenses with shorter focal lengths bend light more sharply than those with longer focal lengths because:
A The glass is thicker overall
B They are always larger in diameter
C The lens surfaces curve more sharply, producing greater refraction
D They use special glass that absorbs more light
E Shorter focal lengths reflect light instead of refracting it
Double Concave Lenses (Questions 3.1 - 3.4)
3.1
What is the definition of double concave lenses?
A Lenses that are thicker in the middle and thinner at the edges
B Lenses that are thinner in the middle and thicker at the edges
C Lenses that have one flat and one curved surface
D Lenses that bend all light rays in the same direction
E Lenses that do not refract light
3.2
Lenses that are thinner in the middle and thicker at the edges, and spread light rays apart, are called:
A Double convex lenses
B Converging lenses
C Double concave lenses
D Magnifying lenses
E Reflecting lenses
3.3
A peephole security viewer in a door provides a wide field of view of the hallway using a double concave lens because it:
A Concentrates light rays to a single point
B Bends light rays away from each other to spread the image and create a wider field of view
C Magnifies distant objects in the hallway
D Blocks most light from passing through
E Creates only real images of nearby objects
3.4
When light passes through a double concave lens, its rays bend outward because:
A The lens absorbs and re-emits light
B The lens surfaces curve inward, causing light to slow and diverge
C Light naturally follows curved paths
D The lens reflects light rays outward
E Double concave lenses depolarize light
Diverging Lenses (Questions 4.1 - 4.4)
4.1
What is the definition of diverging lenses?
A Lenses that bend light rays toward each other at a focal point
B Lenses that bend light rays away from each other and have a negative focal length
C Lenses that do not bend light at all
D Lenses that always produce real images
E Lenses that only work for close objects
4.2
Lenses that spread light rays apart and have a negative focal length are called:
A Converging lenses
B Convex lenses
C Diverging lenses
D Parallel lenses
E Magnifying lenses
4.3
Eyeglasses prescribed for nearsightedness (myopia) use diverging lenses because they:
A Bring all light rays to a single focal point on the retina
B Spread light rays apart before entering the eye, allowing focus to occur properly on distant objects
C Magnify distant objects so they appear closer
D Block excess light from entering the eye
E Change the wavelength of light entering the eye
4.4
Diverging lenses always produce virtual images because:
A The lens material is transparent
B Light rays never actually converge, so they only appear to come from a point behind the lens
C Virtual images are always smaller than the object
D The negative focal length reflects light internally
E Diverging lenses absorb all real light rays
