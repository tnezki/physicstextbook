SECTION 3.3

Connecting Newton's First and Second Laws

Newton's First Law explains what happens when forces are balanced—objects remain at rest or move at constant velocity. Newton's Second Law tells us what happens when forces are unbalanced—objects accelerate. Together, these laws form the foundation for understanding all motion in physics.

Learning Objectives

I can...

  • State Newton's First Law of Motion and explain the concept of inertia.
  • Apply Newton's Second Law (F = ma) to calculate net force, mass, or acceleration in various scenarios.
  • Create and analyze free-body diagrams to determine the net force acting on an object.
  • Solve multi-step problems involving multiple forces, including weight, friction, applied forces, and tension.

Introduction

Imagine you're riding in a car that suddenly stops. Your body lurches forward. Why? Or picture a hockey puck sliding across ice—it keeps moving long after the stick has stopped pushing it. Why doesn't it stop immediately? The answers lie in understanding Newton's laws of motion, which describe the fundamental relationship between forces and motion.

Sir Isaac Newton (1643-1727) revolutionized physics by discovering three laws that explain how forces affect motion. These laws, published in his masterwork Philosophiæ Naturalis Principia Mathematica (Mathematical Principles of Natural Philosophy) in 1687, remain the foundation of classical mechanics over 300 years later.

Newton's Apple: Fact or Fiction?

The famous story claims Newton discovered gravity when an apple fell on his head. While likely exaggerated, Newton himself wrote that observing falling apples did inspire his thinking about gravity. He wondered why apples fall straight down rather than sideways or upward, leading him to develop his law of universal gravitation and his three laws of motion.

Newton's First Law of Motion

Newton's First Law states: An object at rest stays at rest, and an object in motion stays in motion with the same velocity, unless acted upon by an unbalanced force.

Newton's First Law: Objects maintain their state of motion (at rest or moving at constant velocity) unless a net external force acts on them. This is also called the Law of Inertia.

This law introduces the concept of inertia, which is the tendency of objects to resist changes in their motion. The more mass an object has, the more inertia it has, and the harder it is to change its motion.

Inertia: The property of matter that causes it to resist changes in velocity (both speed and direction). Mass is a measure of inertia.

Newton's First Law tells us that forces don't cause motion—they cause changes in motion. An object doesn't need a force to keep moving at constant velocity; it only needs a force to speed up, slow down, or change direction.

Understanding "Unbalanced Force"

The key phrase in Newton's First Law is "unbalanced force." When we talk about the force acting on an object, we really mean the net force—the vector sum of all forces acting on the object.

Net force: The vector sum of all forces acting on an object. If net force is zero, forces are balanced. If net force is non-zero, forces are unbalanced.

When forces are balanced (net force equals zero), the object is in equilibrium and maintains constant velocity (which could be zero). When forces are unbalanced (net force is not zero), the object accelerates.

Important Distinction: "At rest" and "moving at constant velocity" are equivalent from Newton's First Law perspective. Both represent zero acceleration. An object moving at 50 m/s in a straight line has the same net force as an object at rest: zero.

Newton's Second Law of Motion

Newton's Second Law quantifies the relationship between force, mass, and acceleration: The acceleration of an object is directly proportional to the net force acting on it and inversely proportional to its mass.

Mathematically, this is expressed as:

$$\vec{F}_{net} = m\vec{a}$$

or simply

$$F = ma$$

Newton's Second Law: The net force on an object equals the product of its mass and acceleration: $F_{net} = ma$. This law allows us to predict acceleration when forces are known, or to calculate forces when motion is observed.

This equation tells us three important things:

  • Greater force produces greater acceleration (for the same mass)
  • Greater mass produces less acceleration (for the same force)
  • Force and acceleration point in the same direction

Connecting the Two Laws

Newton's First Law is actually a special case of Newton's Second Law. When $F_{net} = 0$, then $a = 0$, which means the object maintains constant velocity. The First Law emphasizes this important case and introduces inertia, while the Second Law provides the mathematical relationship for all situations.

Think of it this way:

  • First Law: Tells us when acceleration occurs (when forces are unbalanced)
  • Second Law: Tells us how much acceleration occurs (depends on force and mass)

Free-Body Diagrams

To apply Newton's laws effectively, we use free-body diagrams—simplified drawings that show all forces acting on a single object.

Free-body diagram (FBD): A diagram showing an object (represented as a dot or box) with all forces acting on it drawn as arrows. Each arrow's direction shows the force direction, and its length can indicate relative magnitude.

Steps for Creating Free-Body Diagrams:

  1. Isolate the object: Draw the object as a simple dot or box
  2. Identify all forces: Consider each type of force that might act:
    • Weight (always present, points downward)
    • Normal force (from surfaces in contact)
    • Friction (opposes motion or attempted motion)
    • Applied forces (pushes, pulls)
    • Tension (from ropes, strings, cables)
  3. Draw force arrows: Each force is an arrow starting from the object's center
  4. Label each force: Use clear labels like $F_{app}$, $W$, $N$, $f$, $T$

Common FBD Mistakes:

  • Including motion arrows (velocity, acceleration) on FBD—only forces belong on FBD
  • Including forces not acting on the object (e.g., force the object exerts on ground)
  • Forgetting weight—it always acts on objects with mass
  • Drawing normal force when object isn't touching a surface

Applying Newton's Second Law

To solve problems using $F = ma$, we typically follow these steps:

  1. Draw a free-body diagram
  2. Choose a coordinate system (usually horizontal/vertical)
  3. Find net force in each direction (sum all forces as vectors)
  4. Apply $F_{net} = ma$ in each direction
  5. Solve for the unknown variable

One-Dimensional Motion

For motion along a single line (horizontal or vertical), we choose one direction as positive. Forces in that direction are positive; forces in the opposite direction are negative.

For horizontal motion: $F_{net,x} = ma_x$

For vertical motion: $F_{net,y} = ma_y$

Common Force Scenarios

Object on horizontal surface: Weight and normal force are vertical, applied force and friction are horizontal. If moving horizontally, $a_y = 0$, so $N = W$.

Pulling with a rope: Tension is the pulling force transmitted through a rope or cable. For a massless rope, tension is the same throughout.

Tension: The pulling force transmitted through a rope, string, or cable when it is pulled tight by forces acting from opposite ends.

Newton's Laws in Car Safety

Airbags: In a collision, Newton's First Law means your body continues moving forward even when the car stops. Airbags increase the time over which your body decelerates. By Newton's Second Law ($F = ma$), increasing deceleration time decreases force, reducing injury.

Crumple Zones: Car fronts are designed to crumple during collisions, extending the stopping time and reducing the force on passengers.

Seat Belts: Provide the force needed to decelerate your body with the car, preventing you from continuing forward at the original velocity.

Example Problems

Problem-Solving Strategy (5 Steps):

  1. List the Givens: Write down all known values with units
  2. Find the Formula: Identify the appropriate equation
  3. Substitute: Plug values into the formula
  4. Solve: Calculate the answer
  5. Check: Verify units and reasonableness

Example 1: Calculating Acceleration

Problem: A 50 kg box is pushed with a net force of 200 N. What is the box's acceleration?

Step 1 - List the Givens:

$m = 50 \text{ kg}$

$F_{net} = 200 \text{ N}$

Step 2 - Find the Formula:

$a = \frac{F_{net}}{m}$ (rearranged from $F = ma$)

Step 3 - Substitute:

$a = \frac{200 \text{ N}}{50 \text{ kg}}$

Step 4 - Solve:

$a = 4 \text{ m/s}^2$

Step 5 - Check:

Units: N/kg = (kg·m/s²)/kg = m/s² ✓

Reasonableness: Acceleration is less than $g$, which makes sense ✓

Answer: The box accelerates at 4 m/s².

Example 2: Finding Net Force

Problem: A 1200 kg car accelerates from rest at 3.5 m/s². What net force is acting on the car?

Step 1 - List the Givens:

$m = 1200 \text{ kg}$

$a = 3.5 \text{ m/s}^2$

Step 2 - Find the Formula:

$F_{net} = ma$

Step 3 - Substitute:

$F_{net} = (1200 \text{ kg})(3.5 \text{ m/s}^2)$

Step 4 - Solve:

$F_{net} = 4200 \text{ N}$

Step 5 - Check:

Units: kg × m/s² = N ✓

Reasonableness: A large force for a large mass ✓

Answer: The net force on the car is 4200 N.

Example 3: Multi-Force Problem with Friction

Problem: A 30 kg box is pushed across a floor with an applied force of 150 N. The coefficient of kinetic friction is 0.25. (a) Draw a free-body diagram. (b) Calculate the friction force. (c) Find the net force. (d) Calculate the box's acceleration.

Part (a): Free-Body Diagram

Vertical forces: Weight (W) down, Normal force (N) up

Horizontal forces: Applied force (150 N) right, Friction (f) left

Part (b): Friction Force

Step 1 - List the Givens:

$m = 30 \text{ kg}$, $\mu_k = 0.25$, $g = 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$

Step 2 - Find the Formula:

$N = mg$ (vertical equilibrium)

$f_k = \mu_k N$

Step 3 - Substitute:

$N = (30)(9.8) = 294 \text{ N}$

$f_k = (0.25)(294)$

Step 4 - Solve:

$f_k = 73.5 \text{ N}$

Part (c): Net Force

Choose right as positive:

$F_{net} = F_{app} - f_k = 150 - 73.5 = 76.5 \text{ N}$

Part (d): Acceleration

Step 1 - List the Givens:

$F_{net} = 76.5 \text{ N}$, $m = 30 \text{ kg}$

Step 2 - Find the Formula:

$a = \frac{F_{net}}{m}$

Step 3 - Substitute:

$a = \frac{76.5}{30}$

Step 4 - Solve:

$a = 2.55 \text{ m/s}^2$

Step 5 - Check:

Net force is less than applied force due to friction ✓

Acceleration is positive (accelerating right) ✓

Answer: (b) Friction force is 73.5 N (c) Net force is 76.5 N to the right (d) Acceleration is 2.55 m/s²

Summary

  • Newton's First Law: Objects maintain constant velocity (including zero) unless acted upon by an unbalanced force.
  • Inertia is the tendency of objects to resist changes in motion; mass is a measure of inertia.
  • Forces don't cause motion—they cause changes in motion (acceleration).
  • Net force is the vector sum of all forces acting on an object.
  • When net force is zero, forces are balanced and acceleration is zero (equilibrium).
  • Newton's Second Law: $F_{net} = ma$ relates net force, mass, and acceleration.
  • Greater force produces greater acceleration; greater mass produces less acceleration.
  • Force and acceleration always point in the same direction.
  • Free-body diagrams show all forces acting on a single object and are essential for problem solving.
  • To solve force problems: draw FBD, find net force in each direction, apply F = ma, solve for unknown.

Practice Problems

1. State Newton's First Law in your own words. Give an example from everyday life that illustrates this law.

2. Explain why a passenger in a car lurches forward when the car suddenly stops. Use the concept of inertia in your explanation.

3. A book sits on a table. Draw a free-body diagram showing all forces. Are the forces balanced or unbalanced? What is the net force?

4. According to Newton's Second Law, what happens to acceleration if you double the force while keeping mass constant? What happens to acceleration if you double the mass while keeping force constant?

5. A 25 kg object is acted upon by a net force of 100 N. Calculate the object's acceleration.

6. A 900 kg car accelerates at 2.0 m/s². What net force is required to produce this acceleration?

7. A 40 kg box is pushed horizontally with a force of 200 N. The coefficient of kinetic friction between the box and floor is 0.30. (a) Calculate the friction force. (b) Find the net force on the box. (c) Calculate the box's acceleration.

8. A 60 kg person stands in an elevator. (a) What is the person's weight? (b) If the elevator accelerates upward at 2.0 m/s², what is the normal force the floor exerts on the person? (Hint: Draw a free-body diagram and use $F_{net} = ma$ in the vertical direction.)

Answer Key

1. Newton's First Law states that objects at rest stay at rest and objects in motion continue moving at constant velocity in a straight line unless an unbalanced force acts on them. Example: When you're riding in a car and it stops suddenly, your body continues moving forward at the original velocity because no force is immediately acting on your body to stop it. The seat belt provides the force needed to change your motion.

2. When a car suddenly stops, the car experiences a large braking force that decelerates it. However, the passenger's body has inertia—the tendency to maintain its current motion. The passenger continues moving forward at the original velocity because there's no immediate force acting directly on their body. The seat belt provides the necessary force to decelerate the passenger along with the car, preventing them from continuing forward through the windshield.

3. Free-body diagram should show: Weight ($W$ or $mg$) pointing downward, Normal force ($N$) pointing upward. The forces are balanced because $N = W$ (book is in equilibrium). Net force = 0 N. The book has zero acceleration and remains at rest.

4. According to $F = ma$, if you double the force while keeping mass constant, acceleration doubles (direct proportionality). If you double the mass while keeping force constant, acceleration is cut in half (inverse proportionality). This makes sense: more force creates more acceleration, while more mass (more inertia) resists acceleration.

5. Given: $m = 25 \text{ kg}$, $F_{net} = 100 \text{ N}$
Formula: $a = \frac{F_{net}}{m}$
Substitute: $a = \frac{100 \text{ N}}{25 \text{ kg}}$
Solve: $a = 4 \text{ m/s}^2$
Answer: The acceleration is 4 m/s².

6. Given: $m = 900 \text{ kg}$, $a = 2.0 \text{ m/s}^2$
Formula: $F_{net} = ma$
Substitute: $F_{net} = (900 \text{ kg})(2.0 \text{ m/s}^2)$
Solve: $F_{net} = 1800 \text{ N}$
Answer: The required net force is 1800 N.

7. Given: $m = 40 \text{ kg}$, $F_{app} = 200 \text{ N}$, $\mu_k = 0.30$, $g = 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$

(a) Friction force:
$N = mg = (40)(9.8) = 392 \text{ N}$
$f_k = \mu_k N = (0.30)(392) = 117.6 \text{ N}$

(b) Net force (taking right as positive):
$F_{net} = F_{app} - f_k = 200 - 117.6 = 82.4 \text{ N}$

(c) Acceleration:
$a = \frac{F_{net}}{m} = \frac{82.4}{40} = 2.06 \text{ m/s}^2$

Answer: (a) 117.6 N (b) 82.4 N to the right (c) 2.06 m/s²

8. Given: $m = 60 \text{ kg}$, $g = 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$, $a = 2.0 \text{ m/s}^2$ (upward)

(a) Weight: $W = mg = (60)(9.8) = 588 \text{ N}$

(b) Free-body diagram shows: Normal force (N) up, Weight (W) down
Taking up as positive: $F_{net} = N - W$
Using $F_{net} = ma$:
$N - W = ma$
$N = ma + W = (60)(2.0) + 588 = 120 + 588 = 708 \text{ N}$

Answer: (a) 588 N (b) 708 N. Note: The normal force is greater than the weight because the elevator is accelerating upward.
