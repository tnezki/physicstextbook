SECTION 8.3

Series and Parallel Circuits

Most circuits in your home use both series and parallel connections. A circuit can have resistors connected in series (one after another) or in parallel (side by side), and real circuits often use both configurations. Understanding these two fundamental circuit types helps you understand how household wiring works, why lights stay on when one bulb burns out, and how to design efficient electrical systems.

Learning Objectives

  • I can distinguish between series and parallel circuits by analyzing circuit diagrams and component configurations.
  • I can predict how voltage and current distribute across components in series and parallel circuits.
  • I can build and test series and parallel circuits to verify circuit behavior and explain the advantages of each configuration.

Why Does This Matter?

Hook: Why do the lights in your bedroom stay on when the hallway light burns out? Why do Christmas light strings sometimes stop working when one bulb fails? The answer depends on whether the lights are connected in series or parallel.

Real-World Connection: Your home's electrical system uses parallel circuits so that each outlet and light works independently. Car electrical systems use series-parallel combinations. Understanding these circuit types helps electricians design safe and reliable systems.

Historical Context: Early home wiring used series circuits, which had a major problem: if one light went out, all lights on that circuit failed. This led to the adoption of parallel circuits, which revolutionized residential electrical systems.

Connect to Prior Knowledge: You've learned about voltage, current, resistance, and Ohm's Law. Now you'll apply these concepts to understand how circuits behave when resistors are arranged in different configurations.

Series Circuits

What is a Series Circuit?

A series circuit is one where all components are connected in a single path. The current flows from the battery, through one component after another, and back to the battery. There is only one path for the current to take.

Key characteristics of series circuits:

  • Single path for current
  • Current is the same everywhere in the circuit
  • Voltages add up to equal the source voltage
  • Total resistance is the sum of all resistances
  • If one component fails, the entire circuit stops working

Current in Series Circuits

In a series circuit, the same current flows through every component. This is because there is only one path for the charge to follow.

If a circuit has resistors $R_1$, $R_2$, and $R_3$ in series powered by voltage $V$, the current through each resistor is identical:

$$I_{total} = I_1 = I_2 = I_3 = \frac{V}{R_{total}}$$

Voltage in Series Circuits

In a series circuit, the voltage divides among the components. Each component "uses up" or drops some of the voltage. The voltages across all components add up to equal the total source voltage:

$$V_{total} = V_1 + V_2 + V_3 + ...$$

Using Ohm's Law, we can find each voltage drop:

  • $V_1 = I \times R_1$
  • $V_2 = I \times R_2$
  • $V_3 = I \times R_3$

A component with higher resistance will have a larger voltage drop across it.

Resistance in Series Circuits

When resistors are in series, their resistances add directly:

$$R_{total} = R_1 + R_2 + R_3 + ...$$

For example, if you have three 10Ω resistors in series, the total resistance is $10 + 10 + 10 = 30$ Ω. Adding more resistors in series always increases the total resistance.

Real-World Example: Old Christmas Lights

Older Christmas light strings connected 50 or more small bulbs in series. When one bulb burned out, the circuit opened and all lights went dark. This frustration led manufacturers to develop parallel Christmas lights, where one burned-out bulb doesn't affect the others. Some modern "mini lights" are actually series-parallel: groups of lights in parallel, with groups in series.

Parallel Circuits

What is a Parallel Circuit?

A parallel circuit is one where components are connected so that there are multiple paths for current to flow. Each component has both terminals connected to the same points, creating independent branches.

Key characteristics of parallel circuits:

  • Multiple paths for current
  • Voltage is the same across all components
  • Currents add up to equal the total current
  • Total resistance is less than any individual resistance
  • If one component fails, others continue working

Voltage in Parallel Circuits

In a parallel circuit, all components experience the same voltage because they're all connected to the same two points (the battery terminals). This is the most important difference from series circuits.

$$V_{total} = V_1 = V_2 = V_3 = ...$$

For example, if a parallel circuit is powered by a 12V battery, each resistor in the parallel circuit experiences 12V across it, regardless of the number of branches.

Current in Parallel Circuits

In a parallel circuit, the total current splits among the branches. Each branch carries some of the total current, and all branch currents add up to the total:

$$I_{total} = I_1 + I_2 + I_3 + ...$$

Using Ohm's Law, we can find the current in each branch:

  • $I_1 = \frac{V}{R_1}$
  • $I_2 = \frac{V}{R_2}$
  • $I_3 = \frac{V}{R_3}$

Branches with lower resistance carry more current.

Resistance in Parallel Circuits

When resistors are in parallel, their resistances combine using a different formula. The reciprocals of the resistances add:

$$\frac{1}{R_{total}} = \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2} + \frac{1}{R_3} + ...$$

This can also be written as:

$$R_{total} = \frac{1}{\frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2} + \frac{1}{R_3} + ...}$$

An important result: The total resistance of parallel resistors is always less than the smallest individual resistance. Adding more parallel branches decreases total resistance and increases total current.

For two equal resistors in parallel, the formula simplifies:

$$R_{total} = \frac{R_1 \times R_2}{R_1 + R_2} = \frac{R}{2} \text{ (when } R_1 = R_2 = R\text{)}$$

Real-World Example: Household Outlets

All outlets in your home are wired in parallel. Each outlet can deliver the full voltage (120V or 240V) from the main panel. If you plug in a lamp, a charger, and a coffee maker, each device gets full voltage and operates independently. This is why you can turn one device off without affecting others. If they were in series, turning on more devices would reduce voltage to each one.

Practical Advantages and Disadvantages

Series Circuits:

  • Advantage: Simple to build and understand
  • Advantage: Works with very low currents (battery life)
  • Disadvantage: If one component fails, entire circuit fails
  • Disadvantage: Voltage divides, so components get less than full voltage

Parallel Circuits:

  • Advantage: Each component gets full voltage
  • Advantage: If one component fails, others continue
  • Advantage: Easy to add or remove components
  • Disadvantage: Higher total current (requires heavier wire)
  • Disadvantage: Slightly more complex to analyze

Example Problems

Example 1: Series Circuit Analysis

Given: A 12V battery powers three 4Ω resistors in series.

Find: (a) Total resistance; (b) Current through the circuit; (c) Voltage drop across each resistor

Step 1a: Series resistance formula: $R_{total} = R_1 + R_2 + R_3 = 4 + 4 + 4 = 12$ Ω
Step 1b: Current using Ohm's Law: $I = \frac{V}{R} = \frac{12 \text{ V}}{12 \text{ Ω}} = 1$ A
Step 1c: Voltage drop on each: $V = IR = 1 \text{ A} \times 4 \text{ Ω} = 4$ V per resistor
Check: $4 + 4 + 4 = 12$ V ✓

Answer: (a) 12 Ω; (b) 1 A; (c) 4 V across each resistor

Example 2: Parallel Circuit Analysis

Given: A 12V battery powers two resistors in parallel: $R_1 = 6$ Ω and $R_2 = 4$ Ω.

Find: (a) Voltage across each resistor; (b) Current through each; (c) Total current

Step 2a: Parallel voltages are equal: $V_1 = V_2 = 12$ V
Step 2b: Current in branch 1: $I_1 = \frac{V}{R_1} = \frac{12 \text{ V}}{6 \text{ Ω}} = 2$ A
Current in branch 2: $I_2 = \frac{V}{R_2} = \frac{12 \text{ V}}{4 \text{ Ω}} = 3$ A
Step 2c: Total current: $I_{total} = I_1 + I_2 = 2 + 3 = 5$ A

Answer: (a) 12 V across each; (b) $I_1 = 2$ A, $I_2 = 3$ A; (c) 5 A total

Example 3: Equivalent Resistance in Parallel

Given: Three resistors in parallel: 6Ω, 6Ω, and 3Ω

Find: Total resistance and verify by calculating voltage/current.

Step 1: Use parallel resistance formula: $\frac{1}{R_{total}} = \frac{1}{6} + \frac{1}{6} + \frac{1}{3}$
Step 2: Convert to common denominator: $\frac{1}{R_{total}} = \frac{1}{6} + \frac{1}{6} + \frac{2}{6} = \frac{4}{6} = \frac{2}{3}$
Step 3: Invert to find resistance: $R_{total} = \frac{3}{2} = 1.5$ Ω
Note: 1.5Ω is less than the smallest (3Ω), confirming the rule.

Answer: $R_{total} = 1.5$ Ω

Summary of Key Concepts

  • A series circuit has one path for current; parallel circuit has multiple paths.
  • In series circuits, current is constant everywhere; voltage divides among components.
  • In parallel circuits, voltage is constant across all components; current divides among branches.
  • Series resistances add: $R_{total} = R_1 + R_2 + R_3 + ...$
  • Parallel resistances combine via: $\frac{1}{R_{total}} = \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2} + \frac{1}{R_3} + ...$
  • Total resistance in parallel is always less than the smallest individual resistance.
  • In series circuits, one component failure stops the entire circuit.
  • In parallel circuits, one component failure doesn't affect others.
  • Household electrical systems use parallel circuits so each outlet/light works independently.
  • Real circuits often combine series and parallel sections for optimal performance.

Answer Key

Question 1: Circuit Paths

Answer: In a series circuit, there is only one path for current to flow. The current must flow through each component one after the other. In a parallel circuit, there are multiple paths available for current. Current can flow through different branches independently, and some current goes through each path.

Question 2: Voltage Distribution

Answer: In a parallel circuit, both terminals of each device are directly connected to the battery terminals, so each device experiences the full voltage. In a series circuit, each device is in the path, and the voltage "drops" as charge flows through each device. Since charge is conserved, the voltage drops must add up to the total source voltage, so each device shares the total.

Question 3: Reliability

Answer: In home systems, parallel circuits are used because if one device fails (like a burned-out bulb), it doesn't affect other devices. Each device has its own branch, so it can be turned off or fail without opening the other paths. In a series circuit, one failure breaks the entire circuit and stops all devices from working. This reliability is essential for homes where people need different devices to work independently.

Question 4: Resistance Comparison

Answer: When you add a resistor in parallel, the total resistance decreases. This is because parallel resistors provide alternative paths for current to flow, making it easier for charge to move through the circuit. The formula $\frac{1}{R_{total}} = \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2}$ shows that adding a new term on the right side increases the sum of reciprocals, which decreases the total resistance. The total resistance is always less than any individual resistor in parallel.

Question 5: Series Circuit Resistance

Given: $R_1 = 8$ Ω, $R_2 = 12$ Ω, $R_3 = 5$ Ω

Find: $R_{total} = ?$

Solution:

$$R_{total} = R_1 + R_2 + R_3 = 8 + 12 + 5 = 25 \text{ Ω}$$

Answer: $R_{total} = 25$ Ω

Question 6: Parallel Circuit Resistance

Given: $R_1 = 12$ Ω, $R_2 = 12$ Ω (equal resistors)

Find: $R_{total} = ?$

Solution:

For two equal resistors in parallel: $R_{total} = \frac{R}{2} = \frac{12}{2} = 6$ Ω

Or using the full formula: $\frac{1}{R_{total}} = \frac{1}{12} + \frac{1}{12} = \frac{2}{12} = \frac{1}{6}$, so $R_{total} = 6$ Ω

Answer: $R_{total} = 6$ Ω

Question 7: Series Circuit Analysis

Given: $V_{battery} = 24$ V, $R_1 = 6$ Ω, $R_2 = 10$ Ω

Find: (a) Total resistance; (b) Current; (c) Voltage drops

Solution:

(a) $R_{total} = R_1 + R_2 = 6 + 10 = 16$ Ω

(b) $I = \frac{V}{R_{total}} = \frac{24 \text{ V}}{16 \text{ Ω}} = 1.5$ A

(c) Voltage drops:
$V_1 = I \times R_1 = 1.5 \text{ A} \times 6 \text{ Ω} = 9$ V
$V_2 = I \times R_2 = 1.5 \text{ A} \times 10 \text{ Ω} = 15$ V
Check: $9 + 15 = 24$ V ✓

Answer: (a) 16 Ω; (b) 1.5 A; (c) $V_1 = 9$ V, $V_2 = 15$ V

Question 8: Parallel Circuit Analysis

Given: $V_{battery} = 12$ V, $R_1 = 8$ Ω, $R_2 = 4$ Ω

Find: (a) Voltage across each; (b) Current through each; (c) Total current; (d) Total resistance

Solution:

(a) In parallel, voltage is the same: $V_1 = V_2 = 12$ V

(b) Current in each branch:
$I_1 = \frac{V}{R_1} = \frac{12 \text{ V}}{8 \text{ Ω}} = 1.5$ A
$I_2 = \frac{V}{R_2} = \frac{12 \text{ V}}{4 \text{ Ω}} = 3$ A

(c) Total current: $I_{total} = I_1 + I_2 = 1.5 + 3 = 4.5$ A

(d) Total resistance:
$\frac{1}{R_{total}} = \frac{1}{8} + \frac{1}{4} = \frac{1}{8} + \frac{2}{8} = \frac{3}{8}$
$R_{total} = \frac{8}{3} = 2.67$ Ω
Verification: 2.67Ω < 4Ω < 8Ω ✓ (less than smallest resistor)

Answer: (a) 12 V across each; (b) $I_1 = 1.5$ A, $I_2 = 3$ A; (c) 4.5 A; (d) 2.67 Ω (verified)
